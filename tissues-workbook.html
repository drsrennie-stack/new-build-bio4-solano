Tissue origins and overview 6 questions

01 DOK 1 · Recall

Define histology and define tissue. How are the two terms related?

Reveal answer

Histology is the microscopic study of tissues. A tissue is a group of structurally similar cells, plus their extracellular matrix, that work together to perform a common function. Histology is what you do to study tissues.

02 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the three primary germ layers and one major tissue or structure each gives rise to.

Reveal answer

Ectoderm forms the epidermis of the skin and all nervous tissue. Mesoderm forms muscle, bone, cartilage, blood, and most connective tissue. Endoderm forms the epithelial lining of the digestive and respiratory tracts and their glands.

03 DOK 1 · Recall

Place the three germ layers in order from outermost to innermost as they sit in the early embryo.

Reveal answer

Ectoderm (outer), mesoderm (middle), endoderm (inner). The name of each layer tells you where it sits: ecto means outside, meso means middle, endo means inside.

04 DOK 1 · Recall

List the four primary tissue types and the primary function of each.

Reveal answer

Epithelial tissue covers body surfaces, lines cavities and vessels, and forms glands. Connective tissue binds, supports, protects, and transports. Muscle tissue produces movement by contracting. Nervous tissue senses change, processes information, and directs responses.

05 DOK 1 · Recall

For each of the four tissue types, name one location where it dominates.

Reveal answer

Epithelial: the epidermis (or any gut, vessel, or airway lining). Connective: the dermis, between and within organs, in bone and cartilage, in blood. Muscle: the skeletal muscles attached to bone, the heart wall, or the walls of hollow organs. Nervous: the brain, spinal cord, and nerves.

06 DOK 2 · Compare

Rank the four tissue types by how much extracellular matrix they contain, from most to least, and explain why the ranking makes sense.

Reveal answer

Connective has the most matrix by far (especially cartilage and bone). Muscle and nervous have intermediate amounts. Epithelial has the least, almost none, because epithelial cells sit packed tightly together to act as barriers. The more a tissue depends on its non-cell material for its job (supporting, cushioning, transporting), the more matrix it carries.

Epithelial tissue: general features 9 questions

07 DOK 1 · Recall

List the five major functions of epithelial tissue.

Reveal answer

Protection, absorption, secretion, filtration, and sensory reception. Some epithelia also do excretion as part of secretion.

08 DOK 1 · Recall

List the general structural features that define epithelial tissue.

Reveal answer

Cells packed tightly with little extracellular matrix; one free apical surface and one attached basal surface (polarity); attached to a basement membrane; avascular but innervated; high capacity to regenerate.

09 DOK 2 · Apply

Explain what polarity means in an epithelial cell and why it matters for function.

Reveal answer

Polarity means the cell has a structurally and functionally different apical (free) surface and basal (attached) surface. It matters because directional transport depends on it: absorbing cells move material from apical to basal, secreting cells move material from basal to apical. Without polarity an epithelium could not move things one direction across itself.

10 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the basement membrane, what does it sit between, and what does it do?

Reveal answer

The basement membrane is a thin sheet of extracellular material between an epithelium and the connective tissue beneath it. It anchors the epithelium, supports it physically, and acts as a selective filter for what passes between the two layers.

11 DOK 2 · Apply

Epithelial tissue is avascular. How does it still receive oxygen and nutrients, and what is the trade-off?

Reveal answer

By diffusion from blood vessels in the underlying connective tissue, across the basement membrane. The trade-off is that the epithelium must stay thin enough for diffusion to work; stratified epithelia depend on their basal layer to anchor and feed the cells above.

12 DOK 2 · Apply

Epithelia regenerate well. Why is that an advantage given where epithelia sit in the body?

Reveal answer

Epithelia are barriers exposed to mechanical wear, abrasion, and chemical insult. They are first to be damaged. Quick regeneration restores the barrier fast, which protects the body from fluid loss and pathogen entry. A slow-healing epithelium would mean an open door.

13 DOK 1 · Recall

Epithelia are classified on two main axes. What are they?

Reveal answer

The number of cell layers (simple if one layer, stratified if many) and the shape of the surface cells (squamous if flat, cuboidal if cube-shaped, columnar if tall). Two arrangements (pseudostratified, transitional) are named for special features instead.

14 DOK 2 · Apply

Compare simple and stratified epithelia by structure and the kind of job each is built for.

Reveal answer

Simple epithelium is one cell layer thick, built for absorption, secretion, and filtration (jobs that need thinness for diffusion). Stratified epithelium is many cell layers thick, built for protection against abrasion and wear (the top layers can be sacrificed and replaced from below).

15 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare squamous, cuboidal, and columnar cells by shape and the job that shape suits.

Reveal answer

Squamous cells are flat and thin: they support rapid diffusion and filtration. Cuboidal cells are cube-shaped: common in glands and kidney tubules for secretion and absorption. Columnar cells are tall: they line absorptive and secretory surfaces like the gut, where their height provides room for organelles.

Epithelial tissue: named types and locations 7 questions

16 DOK 1 · Recall

Where is simple squamous epithelium found, and what is it doing there?

Reveal answer

Alveoli of the lungs (gas diffusion), the lining of blood vessels and the heart (endothelium, for smooth flow and diffusion), and the lining of the serous body cavities (mesothelium). The common thread is rapid diffusion or low-friction flow.

17 DOK 1 · Recall

Where is simple cuboidal epithelium found?

Reveal answer

Kidney tubules, the ducts and secretory units of small glands, the surface of the ovary. The shape supports secretion and absorption.

18 DOK 1 · Recall

Where is simple columnar epithelium found, and what specialized cells often appear within it?

Reveal answer

The lining of most of the digestive tract from the stomach through the rectum, and the gallbladder. Goblet cells (single-cell mucous glands) and absorptive cells with microvilli appear within it.

19 DOK 2 · Apply

What is pseudostratified epithelium, why does it look layered when it is not, and where in the body is it most important?

Reveal answer

It is a single layer of cells that all touch the basement membrane, but their nuclei sit at different heights, so it looks layered. It is most important as the pseudostratified ciliated columnar epithelium that lines the trachea and most of the upper respiratory tract; its cilia sweep mucus and trapped debris upward toward the throat.

20 DOK 1 · Recall

Compare keratinized and non-keratinized stratified squamous epithelium by structure and by where they are found.

Reveal answer

Keratinized stratified squamous epithelium has dead surface cells filled with keratin; it forms the epidermis of the skin and resists water loss and abrasion. Non-keratinized stratified squamous epithelium has living surface cells and stays moist; it lines the mouth, esophagus, and vagina.

21 DOK 2 · Apply

What is transitional epithelium, where is it found, and what is the special property of its surface cells?

Reveal answer

It is a stratified epithelium whose surface cells change shape as the tissue stretches and recoils. It lines the urinary tract (renal pelvis, ureters, bladder, upper urethra). The surface cells flatten when the bladder fills and round up when it empties, so the tissue accommodates volume changes without losing barrier function.

22 DOK 1 · Recall

Define endothelium and mesothelium. Where is each found?

Reveal answer

Endothelium is the simple squamous epithelium that lines blood vessels and the heart chambers. Mesothelium is the simple squamous epithelium that lines the serous cavities (pleural, pericardial, peritoneal) and covers the organs in those cavities.

Epithelial junctions 7 questions

23 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the major types of cell-cell junctions in epithelial tissue and the job of each.

Reveal answer

Tight junctions seal neighboring cells so substances cannot leak between them. Desmosomes anchor cells to each other against mechanical stress. Gap junctions are channels that let neighboring cells share ions and small molecules. (Adherens junctions also anchor cells; they often work with desmosomes.)

24 DOK 1 · Recall

What does a hemidesmosome do, and how does that differ from a desmosome?

Reveal answer

A hemidesmosome anchors an epithelial cell to the basement membrane (cell-to-matrix attachment). A desmosome anchors a cell to its neighbor (cell-to-cell attachment). The hemi- prefix signals you only get half of the structure, because the partner is matrix, not another cell.

25 DOK 1 · Recall

What proteins make up a tight junction, and what does each contribute?

Reveal answer

Claudins are the transmembrane proteins that form the actual seal between neighboring cells; the specific claudin isoforms present set how selective the seal is. Occludin is a transmembrane protein that helps stabilize the seal. ZO proteins (ZO-1, ZO-2, ZO-3) are cytoplasmic scaffolding proteins inside the cell that link the seal to the actin cytoskeleton.

26 DOK 1 · Recall

What proteins make up an adherens junction, and what is the junction anchored to inside the cell?

Reveal answer

The transmembrane proteins are cadherins (E-cadherin in most epithelia); cadherins on neighboring cells bind each other in the presence of calcium. On the cytoplasmic side, catenins (alpha, beta, and p120) link the cadherin tail to the cell's actin cytoskeleton. Adherens junctions anchor cell to cell via cadherin, and the internal anchor in each cell is the actin filament network.

27 DOK 1 · Recall

What proteins make up a desmosome, and what is the desmosome anchored to inside the cell?

Reveal answer

The transmembrane proteins are desmogleins and desmocollins, both members of the cadherin family. They bind their partners on the neighboring cell. The cytoplasmic plaque contains plakoglobin and plakophilin, which connect to desmoplakin. Desmoplakin anchors the junction to keratin intermediate filaments inside the cell. So desmosomes hold cell to cell via cadherins, and the internal anchor in each cell is the keratin intermediate filament network.

28 DOK 1 · Recall

What proteins make up a hemidesmosome, and what does it attach the cell to?

Reveal answer

The transmembrane proteins are integrins, especially alpha-6 beta-4 integrin, which bind to laminin in the basement membrane outside the cell. On the cytoplasmic side, plectin and BP230 link the integrin tail to keratin intermediate filaments. Unlike a desmosome (cell to cell), a hemidesmosome anchors a cell to the basement membrane below it.

29 DOK 1 · Recall

What proteins make up a gap junction, and how are they organized to form the channel?

Reveal answer

Gap junctions are built from connexins. Six connexin subunits assemble in the membrane of one cell to form a hemichannel called a connexon. Two connexons, one contributed by each of the neighboring cells, align across the narrow intercellular gap to form one continuous channel through which ions and small molecules pass directly between cells. Different connexin isoforms make channels with different size and selectivity.

Cilia, flagella, and apical surface specializations 10 questions

30 DOK 1 · Recall

What are microvilli, where are they found, and what is their function?

Reveal answer

Microvilli are finger-like extensions of the apical cell surface. They are abundant in absorptive epithelia (small intestine, kidney tubules) and they multiply the surface area available for absorption.

31 DOK 1 · Recall

What are cilia, where are they prominent, and what do they do?

Reveal answer

Cilia are hair-like motile extensions on the apical surface of certain epithelial cells. They are prominent in the respiratory airways and the female reproductive tract. They sweep mucus, debris, or an ovum across the surface in coordinated waves.

32 DOK 1 · Recall

What is a goblet cell, and where is it found?

Reveal answer

A goblet cell is a single-cell exocrine gland that secretes mucus directly onto an epithelial surface. Goblet cells are scattered within the simple columnar epithelium of the intestines and the pseudostratified epithelium of the airways.

33 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the anatomy of a motile cilium from outside in, including the axoneme, dynein arms, radial spokes, and nexin links. What does each part contribute?

Reveal answer

A motile cilium is a hair-like projection covered by an extension of the plasma membrane. Inside, the core is the axoneme, a precisely arranged bundle of microtubules. Nine doublets of microtubules form a ring around two single central microtubules (the 9+2 pattern). Each peripheral doublet carries dynein arms, motor proteins that grip the adjacent doublet and slide it. Radial spokes project from each doublet toward the central pair. Nexin links connect neighboring doublets. When dynein arms try to slide doublets but the spokes and nexin links resist, the axoneme bends, and the cilium beats.

34 DOK 1 · Recall

What does the 9+2 arrangement refer to in a cilium, and how does it differ from a 9+0 arrangement?

Reveal answer

The 9+2 arrangement describes the cross section of a motile cilium's axoneme: nine doublets of microtubules around the perimeter and two single microtubules at the center. The 9+0 arrangement describes the basal body that anchors the cilium (nine triplets of microtubules, no central pair) and the axoneme of a non-motile primary cilium (nine doublets, no central pair). The presence or absence of the central pair is one structural marker of motile versus non-motile.

35 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the basal body, where does it sit, what is it structurally similar to, and what does it do for the cilium?

Reveal answer

The basal body sits just beneath the surface of the cell at the base of the cilium. Structurally it is identical to a centriole: nine triplets of microtubules in a 9+0 ring. It anchors the cilium in the cell, organizes the microtubules that grow upward to form the axoneme, and positions the cilium correctly relative to the membrane. Every cilium and flagellum grows from a basal body.

36 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare cilia and flagella by length, number per cell, motion pattern, and where each appears in the human body.

Reveal answer

Cilia are short and present in large numbers on a single cell. They beat in coordinated waves (metachronal rhythm) that sweep mucus, debris, or an ovum across the cell surface. They line the respiratory airways, the female reproductive tract, and the brain ventricles. Flagella are long and usually present singly. They beat in whip-like undulations that propel the cell forward. The only flagellated cell in the human body is the sperm. The internal structure of cilia and flagella is the same (9+2 axoneme, basal body); the differences are size, number, and motion pattern.

37 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare motile cilia and primary (non-motile) cilia by axoneme structure, function, and one location of each.

Reveal answer

A motile cilium has a 9+2 axoneme with full dynein arm machinery and beats actively to move material across surfaces (e.g., mucus in the airway). A primary cilium has a 9+0 axoneme (no central pair, often missing dynein arms) and does not beat. Instead it acts as a sensory antenna for the cell. Examples of primary cilia: kidney tubule cells (sensing fluid flow), photoreceptors in the retina (the outer segment is a modified primary cilium), olfactory neurons (sensing odorants), and many others. Most cells in the body have a primary cilium of some kind.

38 DOK 2 · Apply

What are stereocilia, where are they found, and how do they differ structurally from true cilia?

Reveal answer

Despite the name, stereocilia are not cilia at all. They are very long, slender extensions of the apical cell surface filled with actin filaments rather than the microtubule axoneme of a real cilium. They are non-motile. Stereocilia are found on the hair cells of the inner ear (where bending them produces hearing and balance signals) and on the epithelium of the epididymis and ductus deferens (where they aid absorption). Structurally they are oversized microvilli, not cilia.

39 DOK 3 · Reason

Primary ciliary dyskinesia (Kartagener's syndrome) is caused by defects in the dynein arms of cilia. Using what you know about ciliary structure and where cilia and flagella live in the body, predict three clinical features of someone with this condition.

Reveal answer

Without functional dynein arms, cilia cannot bend, so motile cilia and flagella stop working. Predicted features: (1) chronic respiratory infections, because the airway cilia can no longer sweep mucus and trapped pathogens upward toward the throat. (2) infertility in men (and sometimes women), because sperm flagella share the same axoneme structure and cannot swim, and uterine tube cilia cannot move the ovum. (3) situs inversus (organs on the opposite side from typical), because cilia in the early embryo direct left-right asymmetry; without working cilia the asymmetry is randomized.

Glands 5 questions

40 DOK 1 · Recall

What is a gland, and what cells form one?

Reveal answer

A gland is one or more cells specialized to make and secrete a product. Glandular epithelium provides those cells. A gland can be a single cell (a goblet cell) or a multicellular organ (the pancreas, salivary glands).

41 DOK 1 · Recall

Exocrine and endocrine glands differ in one defining structural feature. What is it, and how does that feature determine where each delivers its product?

Reveal answer

Exocrine glands have a duct, so they secrete onto a surface through the duct (sweat, salivary, mucous). Endocrine glands are ductless, so they release hormones directly into the blood (thyroid, adrenal, pituitary).

42 DOK 1 · Recall

Give one example of a unicellular exocrine gland and two examples of multicellular exocrine glands.

Reveal answer

Unicellular: goblet cells. Multicellular: sweat glands, salivary glands, the pancreas, sebaceous glands.

43 DOK 2 · Apply

Compare merocrine, apocrine, and holocrine glands by how they release their product.

Reveal answer

Merocrine glands release product by exocytosis with no loss of cell material; most exocrine glands work this way, including most sweat glands and the pancreas. Apocrine glands release product along with a small portion of the apical cell; classical examples include some specialized sweat glands. Holocrine glands accumulate product inside the cell and then rupture, releasing both the product and the cell itself; sebaceous glands work this way.

44 DOK 1 · Recall

Compare serous and mucous secretions by composition and a typical site.

Reveal answer

Serous secretion is watery and protein-rich (enzymes, antibodies); the parotid salivary gland is mostly serous. Mucous secretion is thick and glycoprotein-rich (mucin); goblet cells and many sublingual gland cells secrete mucus. Some glands are mixed (sublingual, submandibular).

Connective tissue: general plan 5 questions

45 DOK 1 · Recall

List the major functions of connective tissue.

Reveal answer

Binding and supporting, protecting (cushioning organs, walling off infection), insulating (fat), storing energy (fat), and transporting (blood and lymph). It is the most diverse and widespread tissue type.

46 DOK 1 · Recall

Every connective tissue is built from three ingredients. Name them, and name the term for the two non-cell ingredients together.

Reveal answer

Cells, fibers, and ground substance. The fibers and ground substance together form the extracellular matrix, where most of the tissue volume sits.

47 DOK 2 · Apply

How does the physical state of the ground substance differ across connective tissues, and what is one example of each?

Reveal answer

Fluid in blood and lymph (plasma is the matrix). Gel-like in connective tissue proper (areolar, adipose). Firm and pliable in cartilage. Solid and mineralized in bone. The same three ingredients, but the matrix state changes radically across subtypes.

48 DOK 2 · Compare

What do the suffixes -blast and -cyte mean in connective tissue cell names? Give two examples.

Reveal answer

-blast means an immature cell that is actively building matrix. -cyte means a mature cell that maintains the matrix. Examples: fibroblast versus fibrocyte; osteoblast versus osteocyte; chondroblast versus chondrocyte. The pattern is consistent across connective tissue families.

49 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the resident cell types you would expect to find in connective tissue proper and what each one does.

Reveal answer

Fibroblasts build the fibers and ground substance. Macrophages phagocytose pathogens and debris. Mast cells release histamine and other mediators of inflammation. Plasma cells (derived from B lymphocytes) secrete antibodies. Adipocytes store fat.

Connective tissue: fibers and ground substance 4 questions

50 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare the three connective tissue fiber types by physical property and role.

Reveal answer

Collagen fibers are strong and ropelike, resist pulling forces, and are the most abundant fiber in the body. Elastic fibers stretch and recoil to original shape, allowing tissue to spring back (skin, lungs, arteries). Reticular fibers form fine branching networks that support soft organs such as lymph nodes, the spleen, and bone marrow.

51 DOK 2 · Apply

Collagen is the strongest fiber. Where in the body would you predict the highest density of collagen, and why?

Reveal answer

Tendons and ligaments, the dermis, organ capsules, fascia, and bone. Anywhere the tissue must resist tension or hold its shape against pulling forces. The connection: more collagen, more tensile strength.

52 DOK 1 · Recall

Name three places in the body where elastic fibers are especially abundant, and why each location needs them.

Reveal answer

The walls of large arteries (recoil after each heartbeat to keep blood flowing), the lungs (recoil during exhalation), and the skin (return to original shape after stretch). Every site needs to deform predictably and then snap back.

53 DOK 1 · Recall

What is ground substance, what is it made of in general terms, and what does it do?

Reveal answer

Ground substance is the gel-like material that fills the space between cells and fibers in connective tissue. It is made of large sugar-and-protein molecules (proteoglycans, glycoproteins, glycosaminoglycans) that hold water. It supports the tissue, slows the spread of pathogens, and is the medium that lets nutrients and waste diffuse to and from cells.

Connective tissue proper: loose and dense 8 questions

54 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare loose and dense connective tissue by how much fiber they contain and what each is built to do.

Reveal answer

Loose connective tissue has relatively few fibers in a gel matrix; it is soft, flexible, and pliable, and it cushions and packs around structures. Dense connective tissue is packed with fibers (mostly collagen) and is built to resist tension; it forms tendons, ligaments, and the dermis.

55 DOK 1 · Recall

Where is areolar connective tissue found, and what does it do?

Reveal answer

Everywhere as soft packing: under epithelia (the lamina propria of mucous membranes), wrapping around vessels and nerves, between organs. It cushions, holds tissue fluid, and provides the matrix for immune cells to patrol.

56 DOK 1 · Recall

List the major functions of adipose tissue, and name two locations where it is concentrated.

Reveal answer

Functions: energy storage, insulation, cushioning, and (less appreciated) some endocrine secretion. Major locations: the subcutaneous layer of the skin (hypodermis), around the kidneys, behind the eyeballs, and within the breasts.

57 DOK 1 · Recall

Compare white and brown adipose tissue by appearance and basic role.

Reveal answer

White adipose stores energy in a single large lipid droplet per cell, the dominant adult fat. Brown adipose has many small lipid droplets and many mitochondria, gives off heat (thermogenesis), and is prominent in infants and to a lesser extent in adults around the neck and upper back.

58 DOK 1 · Recall

What is reticular connective tissue, and where is it found?

Reveal answer

A loose connective tissue whose matrix is dominated by reticular fibers forming a fine branching network. It is the structural scaffold of lymph nodes, the spleen, bone marrow, and the thymus. The network holds resident cells in place while letting blood or lymph filter through.

59 DOK 2 · Apply

Describe dense regular connective tissue and predict its body locations from its structure.

Reveal answer

Dense regular CT has collagen fibers running in parallel along one direction. That structure resists tension along the line of fibers but not in other directions. Predicted locations are wherever a tissue must pull along a single line: tendons (muscle to bone) and ligaments (bone to bone).

60 DOK 2 · Apply

Describe dense irregular connective tissue and predict its body locations.

Reveal answer

Dense irregular CT has collagen fibers running in all directions. That structure resists tension from any direction. Predicted locations include the dermis of the skin (pulled from any angle), organ capsules (kidney, liver), and the fibrous covering of joints and bones (joint capsules, periosteum).

61 DOK 1 · Recall

Where is dense elastic connective tissue found, and why?

Reveal answer

In the walls of large arteries (aorta and its main branches) and in some elastic ligaments of the spine. These sites need to deform and recoil with each heartbeat or movement, and elastic-dominated matrix is what provides that property.

Cartilage and bone 8 questions

62 DOK 1 · Recall

List the general features of cartilage as a tissue.

Reveal answer

Firm but flexible matrix dominated by collagen and chondroitin sulfate; cells (chondrocytes) sit in spaces called lacunae; avascular and not innervated; surrounded by a perichondrium in most locations; heals slowly because it is avascular.

63 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare the three cartilage types by distinguishing property and typical location.

Reveal answer

Hyaline cartilage is most common, has fine collagen in a glassy matrix; ends of long bones, the nose, costal cartilages, trachea, larynx. Elastic cartilage is flexible with abundant elastic fibers; external ear and epiglottis. Fibrocartilage is tough with thick collagen bundles for shock absorption; intervertebral discs, the knee menisci, the pubic symphysis.

64 DOK 1 · Recall

Compare chondroblasts and chondrocytes, and explain where each is found.

Reveal answer

Chondroblasts are immature cartilage cells that actively secrete matrix. Chondrocytes are mature cartilage cells that maintain it and sit in lacunae. Chondroblasts are typical at the growing surface; chondrocytes are typical in the body of mature cartilage.

65 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the perichondrium, what is it made of, and what does it do?

Reveal answer

The perichondrium is the dense irregular connective tissue covering on most cartilage. It supplies nutrients to the avascular cartilage by diffusion, holds the cartilage in place, and contains the cells that can build new cartilage at the surface.

66 DOK 1 · Recall

List the general features of bone as a tissue.

Reveal answer

Hard, calcified extracellular matrix; cells (osteocytes) sit in lacunae connected by canaliculi; richly vascularized; surrounded by a periosteum on the outside and an endosteum lining the marrow cavities; heals well because of its blood supply.

67 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the three main bone cell types and what each does.

Reveal answer

Osteoblasts build new bone matrix. Osteocytes are mature osteoblasts that have been trapped in the matrix; they maintain it. Osteoclasts are large multinucleated cells that break bone down to release minerals or remodel.

68 DOK 1 · Recall

Compare compact and spongy bone by structure and role.

Reveal answer

Compact bone is the dense outer layer, built around repeating units called osteons. It resists stress and forms the strong outer surface of bone. Spongy bone is an open lattice of trabeculae, lighter than compact bone, that houses bone marrow and lightens the bone overall.

69 DOK 3 · Reason

Cartilage heals slowly; bone heals well. Explain the structural reason for this difference.

Reveal answer

Cartilage is avascular. Without vessels, nutrients reach cells only by diffusion, and the rate of repair is limited by how fast cells can multiply on those terms. Bone has a rich blood supply that delivers cells, oxygen, and nutrients quickly to a fracture site, so repair is fast and complete. The general rule: vascular tissues heal better than avascular ones.

Blood and lymph 3 questions

70 DOK 2 · Apply

Blood is classified as a connective tissue. What feature of blood justifies that classification?

Reveal answer

Blood fits the connective tissue plan of cells, fibers, and ground substance. Its cells are red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets. Its matrix is plasma, a fluid ground substance. Its fibers are proteins (fibrinogen) that polymerize into fibrin only during clotting.

71 DOK 1 · Recall

List the major functions of blood.

Reveal answer

Transport (oxygen, carbon dioxide, nutrients, hormones, waste), defense (white blood cells, antibodies), and regulation (temperature, pH, fluid balance). Clotting (hemostasis) prevents fluid loss after injury.

72 DOK 1 · Recall

What is lymph, where does it come from, and where is it heading?

Reveal answer

Lymph is interstitial fluid that has entered a lymphatic vessel. It originates as filtrate from blood capillaries, drains through lymphatic capillaries and vessels, passes through lymph nodes for immune surveillance, and returns to the venous blood near the heart.

Muscle tissue 6 questions

73 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the basic function of muscle tissue?

Reveal answer

Muscle tissue produces movement by contracting. It moves the body (skeletal), pumps blood (cardiac), and moves contents through hollow organs and vessels (smooth).

74 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare the three muscle tissue types by striations, control, location, and cell description.

Reveal answer

Skeletal muscle is striated, voluntary, attached to bone, with long cylindrical cells that have many nuclei. Cardiac muscle is striated, involuntary, found only in the heart wall, with branching cells joined by intercalated discs. Smooth muscle is non-striated, involuntary, found in the walls of hollow organs and vessels, with spindle-shaped cells that each have one nucleus.

75 DOK 1 · Recall

What does striated mean in muscle tissue, and which of the three muscle types are striated?

Reveal answer

Striated means the cells show alternating light and dark bands under a microscope, the visible result of organized contractile protein arrays. Skeletal and cardiac muscle are striated. Smooth muscle is not.

76 DOK 1 · Recall

What are intercalated discs, where are they found, and what do they do?

Reveal answer

Intercalated discs are specialized junctions that join cardiac muscle cells end to end. They contain desmosomes that hold cells together against the force of contraction and gap junctions that let electrical signals pass instantly between cells. Together they let the heart contract as a coordinated whole.

77 DOK 1 · Recall

Which muscle types are under voluntary control and which are involuntary?

Reveal answer

Skeletal muscle is voluntary; you decide when to contract it. Cardiac and smooth muscle are involuntary; they contract on their own under autonomic and intrinsic control.

78 DOK 1 · Recall

Name four locations where smooth muscle is found, and one job it does at each.

Reveal answer

Walls of the digestive tract (peristalsis, moving food along). Walls of blood vessels (changing vessel diameter, controlling blood flow). Walls of airways (changing airway diameter). Walls of the urinary bladder (emptying it). The iris of the eye (changing pupil size).

Nervous tissue 7 questions

79 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the basic function of nervous tissue?

Reveal answer

To sense change (internal and external), process information, and direct the body's responses by sending signals to muscles, glands, and other neurons.

80 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the two cell types of nervous tissue and state what each does.

Reveal answer

Neurons are the signaling cells; they carry electrical impulses. Neuroglia (glial cells) are the supporting cells; they protect, insulate, and assist neurons.

81 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the three main parts of a neuron and the role of each.

Reveal answer

The cell body (soma) houses the nucleus and most organelles. Dendrites receive incoming signals and pass them toward the cell body. The axon carries the outgoing signal away from the cell body toward another neuron, muscle, or gland.

82 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the glial cells of the central nervous system and what each does, in basic terms.

Reveal answer

Astrocytes support and feed neurons and help form the blood-brain barrier. Oligodendrocytes wrap axons in myelin (multiple axons per cell). Microglia are the immune cells of the CNS, clearing debris and pathogens. Ependymal cells line the brain ventricles and spinal cord canal and help circulate cerebrospinal fluid.

83 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the glial cells of the peripheral nervous system and what each does.

Reveal answer

Schwann cells wrap PNS axons in myelin (one cell wraps one section of one axon). Satellite cells surround the cell bodies of PNS neurons in ganglia and support them, somewhat like astrocytes in the CNS.

84 DOK 2 · Apply

What is myelin, what is it made from, and what does it do for the neuron it wraps?

Reveal answer

Myelin is the insulating sheath wrapped around many axons by glial cells (oligodendrocytes in the CNS, Schwann cells in the PNS). It is made mostly of lipid plus some protein. It insulates the axon and speeds the conduction of electrical signals along it.

85 DOK 2 · Compare

What is the difference between a nerve and a tract?

Reveal answer

A nerve is a bundle of axons in the peripheral nervous system, wrapped in connective tissue. A tract is a bundle of axons in the central nervous system. Same kind of structure, different location, different name.

Body membranes 5 questions

86 DOK 1 · Recall

What is a body membrane in the anatomical sense, and what two tissue types build most of them?

Reveal answer

A body membrane is a thin sheet that covers a surface, lines a cavity, or separates one region from another. Most body membranes are built from an epithelium layered over a connective tissue.

87 DOK 2 · Compare

Name the four body membrane types and describe where each is found.

Reveal answer

Cutaneous membrane is the skin (covers the body surface). Mucous membranes line cavities that open to the outside (digestive, respiratory, urinary, reproductive tracts). Serous membranes line closed body cavities and cover the organs in them (pleura, pericardium, peritoneum). Synovial membranes line freely movable joint cavities.

88 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare the parietal and visceral layers of a serous membrane, and explain what fills the space between them.

Reveal answer

The parietal layer lines the wall of the body cavity. The visceral layer covers the organ inside the cavity. The space between them holds serous fluid, which lubricates the surfaces so they can slide against each other without friction.

89 DOK 2 · Apply

How is the synovial membrane different from the other three body membranes?

Reveal answer

It has no epithelium. It is connective tissue only. It lines joint cavities and produces synovial fluid that lubricates the joint, but the lining cells are not arranged as a true epithelium.

90 DOK 1 · Recall

What are the typical functions of a mucous membrane, and what kind of epithelium often lines it?

Reveal answer

Mucous membranes line passages that open to the outside, so they must protect against pathogens and abrasion, absorb (gut) or secrete mucus, and in some sites trap and clear debris (airways). The epithelium varies by location: stratified squamous in the esophagus, simple columnar in the gut, pseudostratified ciliated columnar in much of the airway.

Tissue repair 4 questions

91 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the three general events that follow tissue injury in order.

Reveal answer

Inflammation (the immediate response: blood vessels dilate, fluid and immune cells move in). Organization (a temporary connective tissue replaces the clot and a new vascular bed grows in). Regeneration or fibrosis (either the original tissue regrows or scar tissue forms in its place).

92 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare regeneration and fibrosis as outcomes of tissue repair.

Reveal answer

Regeneration replaces the lost tissue with new tissue of the same type, restoring full function. Fibrosis replaces the lost tissue with dense irregular connective tissue (scar) that holds the area together but does not perform the original function.

93 DOK 1 · Recall

Which tissues regenerate well and which scar instead?

Reveal answer

Tissues that regenerate well: epithelia (skin, gut lining), bone, areolar connective tissue. Tissues that regenerate poorly and tend to scar: cardiac muscle, mature skeletal muscle, neurons in the central nervous system, cartilage.

94 DOK 2 · Apply

A heart attack damages a region of cardiac muscle. Predict the structural outcome and explain its functional consequence.

Reveal answer

Cardiac muscle does not regenerate. The damaged region is replaced by scar tissue (dense irregular connective tissue). The scar holds the heart wall together but cannot contract or conduct electrical signals like cardiac muscle, so the heart's pumping capacity is permanently reduced in that area.

Clinical reasoning and synthesis 6 questions

95 DOK 3 · Reason

A tendon must resist strong pulling forces along the line from muscle to bone. Predict which connective tissue subtype you would expect there, and justify your prediction structurally.

Reveal answer

Dense regular connective tissue. Its collagen fibers run in parallel along one direction, which makes it very strong against pulling forces along that line but not in other directions. That structural pattern matches exactly what a tendon needs.

96 DOK 3 · Reason

The urinary bladder must stretch as it fills and recoil as it empties, all without leaking urine. Predict the epithelium that lines it and the feature that suits it to that job.

Reveal answer

Transitional epithelium. Its cells flatten when the bladder stretches and stack back up when it empties, so the lining accommodates dramatic volume changes without losing its barrier function.

97 DOK 3 · Reason

Predict the epithelium that lines the alveoli of the lungs, and explain why no other type would fit.

Reveal answer

Simple squamous epithelium. Alveolar function is rapid diffusion of oxygen and carbon dioxide across the wall. Only a single layer of flat, thin cells permits diffusion to occur fast enough. A thicker or taller epithelium would slow gas exchange to a dangerous degree.

98 DOK 3 · Reason

A deep burn destroys all layers of the epidermis and exposes the underlying dermis. Using what you know about epithelium and connective tissue, explain why the wound looks raw and weeps fluid, and why re-covering it is medically urgent.

Reveal answer

The epithelial barrier is gone. The dense irregular connective tissue of the dermis, full of blood vessels and tissue fluid, is now exposed. Fluid weeps out because the tight-junction barrier that normally held it back is missing, and pathogens have direct access to the body's interior. Re-covering the wound, by re-epithelialization, grafting, or dressing, restores the barrier and shuts the door on both fluid loss and infection.

99 DOK 3 · Reason

In some chronic inflammatory conditions of the airway, goblet cell numbers increase dramatically. Predict the clinical consequence using what you know about goblet cells.

Reveal answer

Goblet cells secrete mucus. More goblet cells means more mucus in the airway. Excess mucus narrows the airway, obstructs airflow, and provides a medium that traps debris and bacteria. Patients experience cough, congestion, and increased infection risk.

100 DOK 3 · Reason

An athlete tears the meniscus of the knee, a fibrocartilage structure. Predict how this injury will heal and what the long-term consequences are likely to be.

Reveal answer

Fibrocartilage is avascular and heals slowly and often incompletely. Without intervention the tear is unlikely to fully repair, and the meniscus may continue to fragment with use. Long-term consequences include persistent knee pain, mechanical symptoms (locking, catching), and accelerated wear of the cartilage on the bone surfaces it normally protects, which can progress to osteoarthritis.
