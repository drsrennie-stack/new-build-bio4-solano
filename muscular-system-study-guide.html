1. Attachments and actions

Gross anatomy
  1. Define origin and insertion, and state which is usually proximal and which moves. Recall
  2. Using the spring-on-a-door analogy, label the origin, insertion, and action. Apply
  3. When a muscle contracts, which attachment moves and toward what? Reason

2. Levers and lever classes

Biomechanics
  1. Name the fulcrum, effort, and load for the biceps flexing the forearm. Recall
  2. Distinguish first-, second-, and third-class levers by the order of fulcrum, effort, and load, with one anatomical example each. Apply
  3. Why does moving the load closer to the fulcrum reduce the effort needed? Reason

3. Muscle coordination

Teamwork
  1. Define agonist (prime mover) and antagonist. Recall
  2. Contrast a synergist and a fixator by what each one stabilizes or assists. Apply
  3. What is a muscle compartment, and what does it group together? Recall

4. Naming muscles

Terminology
  1. List the characteristics used to name muscles. Recall
  2. Decode the name sternocleidomastoid by its origin and insertion. Apply
  3. What do rectus, maximus, deltoid, and biceps each tell you about a muscle? Apply

5. The three muscle tissues

Histology
  1. Compare skeletal, cardiac, and smooth muscle by striations and voluntary control. Recall
  2. What are intercalated discs, which tissue has them, and what two junctions do they hold? Apply
  3. Match each tissue to its location and cell shape. Apply

6. Connective-tissue sheaths

Gross anatomy
  1. Name the three CT sheaths from outside in and the structure each wraps. Recall
  2. Contrast direct (fleshy) and indirect attachments; what are a tendon and an aponeurosis? Apply
  3. Why can all three sheaths transmit the pull of contraction to bone? Reason

7. Inside the muscle fiber

Cell biology
  1. Define sarcoplasm, sarcolemma, glycosomes, and myoglobin. Recall
  2. Name the three specialized structures of a skeletal muscle fiber. Recall
  3. Identify the striation bands and lines: A band, I band, H zone, M line, Z disc. Apply

8. Sarcomere and filaments

Microanatomy
  1. Define the sarcomere and give its boundaries. Recall
  2. Contrast thick, thin, and elastic filaments by their protein and the band they occupy. Apply
  3. How do tropomyosin and troponin control whether myosin can bind actin? Reason
