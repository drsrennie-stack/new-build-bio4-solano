The Respiratory System

A retired mechanic, 50 years of smoke, lungs that can't exhale fast enough, and an airway whose walls are gone.

Patient case

Frank is 71, retired auto mechanic, smoked a pack a day from age 16 to 65 (about 50 pack-years). He has known COPD. He comes in today with three days of worsening cough, increased green sputum, and more shortness of breath, especially when walking from the parking lot.

On exam he is tripoding, pursed-lip breathing, with a barrel-shaped chest. Auscultation reveals diminished breath sounds bilaterally with prolonged expiration and diffuse wheezes. O2 sat is 88% on room air. CXR shows hyperinflated lungs with flattened diaphragms. PFTs from last month: FEV1/FVC 0.55, total lung capacity increased, residual volume increased. He is admitted for a COPD exacerbation.

COPD is what happens when decades of smoke destroy the alveolar walls (emphysema) and inflame the airways (chronic bronchitis). Understanding why Frank can't exhale and why his lungs are getting bigger requires knowing exactly how the respiratory system is built, from the nose to the alveoli, and what each part does.

Guiding question What is the path of air from nose to alveoli, what makes the conducting airways different from the respiratory airways, and why does destruction of alveolar walls trap air inside lungs that should empty easily?

1 Upper respiratory

Nose to larynx

The respiratory system is divided into upper (nose, nasal cavity, pharynx) and lower (larynx, trachea, bronchi, lungs). Air goes through all of them on every breath.

Nose & nasal cavityWarms, humidifies, and filters air. Lined by pseudostratified ciliated columnar epithelium (the mucociliary escalator). Conchae increase surface area.
Paranasal sinusesFrontal, ethmoid, sphenoid, maxillary. Lighten the skull, warm and humidify air, drain into the nasal cavity.
PharynxCommon passage for air and food. Three regions: nasopharynx (air only), oropharynx (both), laryngopharynx (both, splits below).
Larynx"Voice box." Cartilage framework (thyroid, cricoid, epiglottis, paired arytenoids) housing the vocal cords. Guards the airway from food entry (epiglottis closes during swallowing) and produces sound.
2 Lower respiratory

Trachea down to alveoli

From the larynx, air enters the trachea (10-12 cm long, supported by C-shaped cartilage rings open posteriorly toward the esophagus). The trachea bifurcates at the carina (~T4/T5, the sternal angle level) into right and left main bronchi.

  • Right main bronchus: wider, shorter, more vertical than left. Aspirated objects favor the right side.
  • Left main bronchus: narrower, longer, more horizontal. Passes under the aortic arch.

From there, the airway branches repeatedly (~23 generations of branching):

Main bronchi → lobar bronchi → segmental bronchiStill cartilage-supported. Air conducting only — no gas exchange.
Bronchioles< 1 mm diameter. No cartilage. Walls are smooth muscle (which is why they bronchoconstrict in asthma).
Terminal bronchiolesLast of the purely conducting airways.
Respiratory bronchiolesFirst place gas exchange happens (a few alveoli bud off).
Alveolar ducts → alveolar sacs → alveoliThe terminal gas-exchange units. ~300 million alveoli per adult.
3 Alveoli

Where actual gas exchange happens

An alveolus is a thin-walled air sac, about 250-300 μm in diameter, wrapped in a dense capillary network. Three cell types in the alveolar wall:

Type I pneumocytesSquamous cells that form ~95% of the alveolar surface. So thin that O2 and CO2 diffuse across them in milliseconds.
Type II pneumocytesCuboidal cells that secrete surfactant, a phospholipid mixture that reduces surface tension and prevents alveolar collapse on exhalation. Premature infants are surfactant-deficient → respiratory distress syndrome.
Alveolar macrophagesPhagocytose particles and bacteria that reach the alveoli. The lung's last line of defense after the mucociliary escalator.

The respiratory membrane (where O2 and CO2 actually cross) is just three layers: alveolar epithelium (Type I cell), fused basement membranes, capillary endothelium. Total thickness about 0.5 μm. Diseases that thicken this membrane (fibrosis, pulmonary edema) impair gas exchange.

4 Lungs & pleurae

Gross structure and the membranes that wrap them

The right lung has three lobes (upper, middle, lower) separated by horizontal and oblique fissures. The left lung has two lobes (upper, lower) separated by an oblique fissure — the heart takes the space where the middle lobe would be (instead, there's a notch and a tongue-shaped projection of the upper lobe called the lingula).

Each lung is enveloped by two pleural membranes:

Visceral pleuraCovers the lung surface.
Parietal pleuraLines the thoracic wall, diaphragm, and mediastinum.

The thin space between them is the pleural cavity, containing a thin film of serous fluid. The negative intrapleural pressure keeps the lung inflated against the chest wall. If air enters the pleural space (pneumothorax), the lung collapses.

5 Ventilation mechanics

How a breath actually happens

Inspiration is active. The diaphragm contracts and flattens, the external intercostals lift the ribs up and out, and accessory muscles (sternocleidomastoid, scalenes) help in distress. The thoracic cavity expands. Intrapleural pressure becomes more negative. The lungs are pulled outward, alveolar pressure drops below atmospheric, and air flows in.

Expiration at rest is passive. The diaphragm and external intercostals relax. The elastic recoil of the lung tissue (and the chest wall in some conditions) pulls everything back. Alveolar pressure rises above atmospheric, air flows out.

Forced expiration uses abdominal muscles and internal intercostals. This becomes critical in obstructive disease like Frank's.

6 Obstructive vs restrictive

Why FEV1/FVC tells you so much

Lung disease falls into two big patterns:

  • Obstructive: airflow OUT is impaired. FEV1 (volume exhaled in 1 second) drops more than FVC (total volume exhaled). FEV1/FVC ratio < 0.70. Examples: asthma, COPD, chronic bronchitis, emphysema. Frank's pattern.
  • Restrictive: lung CAN'T EXPAND fully. Both FEV1 and FVC drop proportionally. FEV1/FVC ratio is preserved or increased. Examples: pulmonary fibrosis, kyphoscoliosis, neuromuscular weakness.
What COPD does anatomically. Two processes, often together. Emphysema: alveolar walls are destroyed; small airways lose the tethering that normally holds them open during exhalation; they collapse on expiration, trapping air. The lung becomes hyperinflated. Chronic bronchitis: chronic inflammation thickens bronchial walls and produces excess mucus, narrowing the airway and providing a substrate for infection. Both produce obstruction.

Returning to Frank

Why he can't exhale, why his chest looks the way it does, why pursed lips help

Frank's exam findings are a directory of COPD pathophysiology. Each one maps to specific damage in his lungs.

"Barrel-shaped chest"
Chronic air trapping holds the lungs in a persistently inflated state. The diaphragm flattens, the ribs sit elevated, the AP diameter of the chest increases. The chest is stuck in inspiration.
"Pursed-lip breathing"
Maintains positive pressure in the airways during exhalation. This positive pressure splints open the small airways (which would otherwise collapse from loss of alveolar tethering) and prolongs expiration.
"Prolonged expiration with wheezes"
Narrowed airways (mucus, smooth muscle constriction, loss of tethering) generate turbulent flow during the slow exhalation. Wheezes are the audible sign.
"Diminished breath sounds"
Hyperinflated lungs are full of trapped air that doesn't move with each breath. Air movement is reduced; breath sounds are quieter.
"FEV1/FVC 0.55"
Diagnostic of airflow obstruction. He exhales only 55% of his vital capacity in the first second; a normal person exhales > 70%. Below 0.70 = obstructive disease.
"O2 sat 88%"
Loss of alveolar surface area (emphysema) + ventilation-perfusion mismatch (some alveoli are damaged, others get less air). Plus the current exacerbation adds inflammation, mucus, possibly infection on top of his baseline disease.

Airway, alveolus, surface area, recoil. Frank's lungs are showing you the price of fifty years of smoke at every level of the respiratory system. When you go back to the prework, the alveolar diagram will land differently — it's exactly what he's missing.
