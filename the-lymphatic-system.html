The Lymphatic System

Six months after a mastectomy, her wedding ring no longer fits on the right hand. The axillary nodes she lost were doing more than she knew.

Patient case

Beverly is 58. Six months ago she had a right mastectomy with axillary lymph node dissection for stage II breast cancer, followed by chemotherapy and radiation. Treatment went well and her oncology follow-ups have been reassuring.

Over the past two months her right arm has been progressively swelling. Her wedding ring no longer fits her right ring finger. The arm feels heavy. Initially the swelling pitted with pressure; now it feels firmer and pitting is less marked. Skin folds at the wrist are deeper than on the left. She has no fever, no pain, no redness. The diagnosis is secondary lymphedema from disruption of axillary lymphatic drainage.

The lymphatic system runs alongside the cardiovascular system and is invisible until something disrupts it. To understand why removing nodes in Beverly's armpit caused swelling in her hand and forearm half a year later, you have to understand what lymph is, where it comes from, and how it gets back to the bloodstream.

Guiding question Where does lymph come from, what path does it take from the tissues back to the bloodstream, and what stops working when a chunk of the route is cut out?

1 Why we have a lymphatic system

The leak you didn't know you had

Capillaries are leaky. They have to be — that's how nutrients and oxygen reach tissues. But the leak isn't balanced. About 3 liters per day more fluid leaves blood capillaries than returns to them at the venous end. If this fluid stayed in the tissues, you would swell to immobility within hours.

The lymphatic system is the drainage system that collects this excess interstitial fluid, filters it through lymph nodes, and returns it to the bloodstream. It also: absorbs dietary fats from the intestine (via specialized lymph capillaries called lacteals), and delivers immune surveillance to every tissue (lymph nodes are where immune cells encounter pathogens collected from drained tissues).

2 Lymph and lymph capillaries

Where it starts

Lymph is the fluid in the lymphatic system. It starts as interstitial fluid (the same fluid that bathes cells) and becomes lymph the moment it enters a lymphatic vessel. It looks like watery plasma; chyle (lymph from the intestine after a meal) is milky from absorbed fats.

Lymphatic capillaries are blind-ended tubes scattered throughout most tissues. They are more permeable than blood capillaries because their endothelial cells overlap loosely like roof shingles, creating one-way flap valves. Interstitial fluid pushes the flaps inward and enters; rising pressure inside closes them. Lymphatic capillaries collect not just fluid but also proteins, dead cells, microbes, and other large particles that escape from blood capillaries.

3 Vessels, trunks, ducts

From capillary to bloodstream

Lymph capillaries merge into progressively larger lymphatic vessels, just like venules into veins. Larger lymphatic vessels have one-way valves (lymph flow is driven mostly by skeletal muscle contraction and respiratory pump, similar to veins).

Lymphatic vesselsCarry lymph from capillaries to trunks. Pass through lymph nodes along the way.
Lymphatic trunksLarge vessels that drain major body regions. Named by region: jugular, subclavian, bronchomediastinal, intestinal, lumbar.
Right lymphatic ductDrains the right upper quadrant of the body (right side of head, neck, thorax, right arm). Empties into the right venous angle (junction of right subclavian and right internal jugular veins).
Thoracic ductDrains everything else — the rest of the body. The largest lymphatic vessel. Starts at the cisterna chyli in the abdomen, ascends through the chest, empties at the left venous angle.
4 Lymph nodes

Filters and immune surveillance stations

Lymph nodes are small bean-shaped organs (1-25 mm) studded along the course of lymphatic vessels. Lymph enters a node through several afferent lymphatic vessels, percolates through a network of sinuses lined with macrophages and lymphocytes, and exits through a single efferent vessel at the hilum (along with a blood supply).

Two jobs:

  • Filter the lymph. Macrophages engulf pathogens, debris, and cancer cells. This is why cancer can spread via lymphatics — the first nodes downstream of a tumor often contain the first metastatic cells.
  • Mount immune responses. B and T lymphocytes encounter antigens delivered in the lymph. Activated lymphocytes proliferate and exit via the efferent vessel.

Lymph node clusters are concentrated in regions: cervical (head and neck), axillary (upper extremity and breast), inguinal (lower extremity and perineum), and along the great vessels (mesenteric, para-aortic).

5 Primary & secondary lymphoid organs

Where lymphocytes are born vs where they work

Primary: red bone marrowAll lymphocytes are born here. B cells mature here. T-cell precursors leave here for the thymus.
Primary: thymusT cells mature and are educated here (learn to distinguish self from non-self). Large in childhood, involutes in adulthood.
Secondary: lymph nodesFilter lymph; site of B and T cell activation.
Secondary: spleenFilters BLOOD (not lymph). Removes old/damaged RBCs (red pulp). Mounts immune responses against blood-borne antigens (white pulp). Functional asplenia (as in sickle cell) → vulnerability to encapsulated bacteria.
Secondary: tonsils & MALTMucosa-associated lymphoid tissue. Tonsils guard the pharynx; Peyer's patches in the ileum sample gut contents; appendix; bronchus-associated lymphoid tissue.
6 When the drainage breaks

Lymphedema

If lymphatic drainage from a region is disrupted, interstitial fluid (especially protein-rich fluid that lymph normally clears) accumulates. The result is lymphedema: chronic swelling that initially pits with pressure but becomes progressively firm as protein-rich fluid drives fibrosis of the subcutaneous tissue.

Two types:

  • Primary lymphedema: congenital lymphatic malformations. Rare. Often hereditary.
  • Secondary lymphedema: acquired disruption. Causes worldwide include filariasis (parasitic infection blocking lymphatics, the most common cause globally). In high-income countries, the most common cause is cancer treatment: surgical lymph node removal and/or radiation. Beverly's.
Why axillary node dissection causes arm lymphedema. Almost all lymphatic drainage of the upper extremity passes through axillary nodes on its way to the right lymphatic duct. Removing those nodes (and the lymphatic channels around them) physically disconnects the lymphatic system of the arm from its return route. Some collateral channels form over time, but in 20-30% of patients drainage never fully compensates. Radiation worsens the risk by fibrosing remaining channels.

Returning to Beverly

Six months later, the drainage that didn't compensate

Beverly's arm swelling is the predictable consequence of an axillary node dissection. The lymph is still being produced; it just has nowhere to go.

"Why six months later, not immediately"
Collateral lymphatic pathways take months to attempt to develop. In ~70% of patients, alternate drainage routes form adequately. In the other 20-30%, they don't, and lymphedema develops slowly over months to years.
"Pitting then non-pitting edema"
Early lymphedema is fluid only — pits with pressure. Over time the protein-rich fluid drives subcutaneous fibrosis. The tissue becomes firm. Pitting decreases. This is harder to treat and harder to reverse.
"Heaviness of the arm"
Subjective sensation of carrying extra fluid weight in the limb. A typical lymphedematous arm can weigh 1-2 kg more than the contralateral arm.
"Wedding ring stuck"
Distal swelling at the fingers is often where lymphedema first becomes obvious. The hand is the most dependent part of the limb and where fluid accumulates first.
"Why this matters clinically"
Lymphedematous tissue has impaired immune defense. Minor breaks in skin → cellulitis. Recurrent cellulitis → more lymphatic damage → worse lymphedema. The cycle is hard to break.
"Treatment"
Complete decongestive therapy: manual lymphatic drainage, multilayer compression bandaging, exercise, skin care, and lifelong compression garment use. Newer surgical options (lymphovenous bypass, vascularized lymph node transfer) for selected patients.

Capillary, vessel, node, duct. Beverly's arm is the lymphatic system's plumbing failing in slow motion. When you go back to the prework, the lymphatic drainage map will land differently — it's the pathway her arm can no longer use.
