Cranial bones 7 questions

01 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the eight cranial bones.

Reveal answer

Frontal (1), parietal (2), temporal (2), occipital (1), sphenoid (1), ethmoid (1). The parietal and temporal are paired; the rest are single midline bones.

02 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the frontal bone and its key features.

Reveal answer

Forms the forehead, the superior orbital rims (with the supraorbital foramen or notch), and the anterior part of the cranial floor. It contains the frontal sinuses.

03 DOK 1 · Recall

Where are the parietal bones, and how do they join other cranial bones?

Reveal answer

The two parietal bones form most of the superior and lateral skull. They meet each other at the sagittal suture (midline), the frontal bone at the coronal suture, the occipital bone at the lambdoid suture, and the temporal bone at the squamous suture.

04 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the major parts of the temporal bone.

Reveal answer

Squamous part (forms the lateral skull and the zygomatic process that contributes to the cheekbone), petrous part (houses the inner and middle ear), mastoid process (a bony bump posterior to the ear, attachment for the sternocleidomastoid), styloid process (a needle-like inferior projection, attachment for ligaments and tongue muscles), external auditory meatus (the opening of the ear canal).

05 DOK 1 · Recall

What are the major features of the occipital bone?

Reveal answer

Forms the posterior and inferior cranium. The foramen magnum is the large opening through which the brainstem becomes the spinal cord. The occipital condyles sit on either side of the foramen magnum and articulate with the first cervical vertebra. The external occipital protuberance is a midline bump on the back of the head.

06 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the sphenoid bone and the structure on it that holds the pituitary gland.

Reveal answer

The sphenoid is a butterfly-shaped bone in the cranial floor that articulates with every other cranial bone. Its body holds the sella turcica, a saddle-shaped depression that cradles the pituitary gland. The greater and lesser wings extend laterally; the pterygoid processes hang inferiorly.

07 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the ethmoid bone and where it sits in the skull.

Reveal answer

The ethmoid sits between the orbits and forms much of the medial walls of the orbits, the upper nasal cavity, and a section of the cranial floor. Its cribriform plate is perforated by tiny olfactory foramina that the olfactory nerves pass through. The crista galli is a midline projection above; the perpendicular plate forms the upper nasal septum; ethmoid air cells form the ethmoid sinus.

Facial bones 5 questions

08 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the fourteen facial bones.

Reveal answer

Mandible (1), maxilla (2), zygomatic (2), nasal (2), lacrimal (2), palatine (2), inferior nasal conchae (2), vomer (1). The mandible and vomer are single; the rest are paired.

09 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the mandible and its key features.

Reveal answer

The mandible is the lower jaw, the only freely movable bone of the skull. It has a body (anterior horseshoe-shaped portion holding the lower teeth), two rami (vertical posterior parts), an angle where each ramus meets the body, a coronoid process for temporalis muscle attachment, and a condylar process (mandibular condyle) that articulates with the temporal bone at the TMJ. The mental foramen is in the body, anterior to each ramus.

10 DOK 1 · Recall

What does the maxilla form, and what major sinus does it contain?

Reveal answer

The maxillae fuse along the midline to form the upper jaw, holding the upper teeth, the anterior part of the hard palate, the inferior orbital rim, and the medial walls of the orbits. Each maxilla contains a large maxillary sinus, the largest of the paranasal sinuses.

11 DOK 1 · Recall

What does the zygomatic bone contribute to?

Reveal answer

The zygomatic is the 'cheekbone', forming the prominence of the cheek and contributing to the inferolateral orbital rim. It articulates with the frontal, maxillary, sphenoid, and temporal bones.

12 DOK 1 · Recall

Compare the vomer and the inferior nasal conchae.

Reveal answer

The vomer is a thin midline plate forming the inferior part of the nasal septum (the upper part is the perpendicular plate of the ethmoid). The inferior nasal conchae are paired curled bones projecting medially from the lateral nasal wall, increasing the surface area inside the nasal cavity.

Major skull foramina 3 questions

13 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the foramen magnum, where is it, and what passes through it?

Reveal answer

The foramen magnum is the large opening in the occipital bone. The brainstem becomes the spinal cord here, and the vertebral arteries and the spinal roots of the accessory nerves (CN XI) pass through it.

14 DOK 1 · Recall

Name three other key foramina of the cranial base and one structure each transmits.

Reveal answer

Optic canal (sphenoid): optic nerve (CN II) and ophthalmic artery. Foramen ovale (sphenoid): mandibular division of trigeminal (CN V3). Internal acoustic meatus (temporal): facial nerve (CN VII) and vestibulocochlear nerve (CN VIII). Many more exist; these three illustrate the pattern: each foramen transmits a specific nerve and often an artery.

15 DOK 2 · Apply

The cribriform plate of the ethmoid carries the olfactory nerves. Predict the consequence of a head injury that fractures this plate.

Reveal answer

The olfactory nerves can be torn, producing anosmia (loss of smell). Because the plate sits at the cranial floor and meninges and cerebrospinal fluid lie just above, fracture can also allow CSF to drip out through the nose (CSF rhinorrhea), opening a route for meningitis-causing bacteria to enter the cranial cavity.

Sinuses and joints 2 questions

16 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the paranasal sinuses and the bone each is housed in.

Reveal answer

Frontal (frontal bone), maxillary (maxilla), ethmoid (ethmoid bone, several small air cells), sphenoid (sphenoid bone). All drain into the nasal cavity. They lighten the skull, add resonance to the voice, and produce mucus.

17 DOK 2 · Apply

What kind of joints connect most skull bones, and how do they differ in newborns and adults?

Reveal answer

Sutures: fibrous joints in adults, classified as synarthroses (immovable). In newborns, the same regions are wider unossified gaps called fontanelles (the 'soft spots'), which let the skull deform during birth and accommodate brain growth. Fontanelles close progressively in infancy, replaced by suture lines.
