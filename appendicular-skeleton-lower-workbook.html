Pelvic girdle 6 questions

01 DOK 1 · Recall

What bones form the pelvic girdle, and how is it different from the pectoral girdle?

Reveal answer

The pelvic girdle is formed by two hip (coxal) bones articulating posteriorly with the sacrum at the sacroiliac joints, and anteriorly with each other at the pubic symphysis. Unlike the pectoral girdle, it is firmly fused to the axial skeleton, sacrificing mobility for stability and weight-bearing.

02 DOK 1 · Recall

Each hip bone is formed by the fusion of three bones. Name them, and where they meet.

Reveal answer

Ilium, ischium, and pubis. They meet at the acetabulum, the deep cup-shaped socket that articulates with the femoral head. The fusion is complete by adulthood; in childhood the three bones are still separated by cartilage.

03 DOK 1 · Recall

What are the major landmarks of the ilium?

Reveal answer

Iliac crest (the curved superior ridge), anterior superior iliac spine (ASIS), anterior inferior iliac spine (AIIS), posterior superior iliac spine (PSIS), posterior inferior iliac spine (PIIS), iliac fossa (the broad concavity on the inner surface), and the greater sciatic notch.

04 DOK 1 · Recall

What are the major landmarks of the ischium?

Reveal answer

Ischial body, ischial spine (a sharp posterior projection), ischial tuberosity (the bump you sit on), ramus of the ischium (the inferior portion that joins the pubis to complete the obturator foramen).

05 DOK 1 · Recall

What are the major landmarks of the pubis?

Reveal answer

Body of the pubis (anterior), superior pubic ramus, inferior pubic ramus. The two pubic bones meet at the midline pubic symphysis (a fibrocartilage joint). Together with the ischium, the pubic rami enclose the obturator foramen.

06 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare the female and male pelvis by structural features relevant to childbirth.

Reveal answer

The female pelvis is wider, shallower, and lighter; the pubic arch (angle below the symphysis) is wider (about 80-90 degrees, vs about 50-60 in males); the pelvic inlet is rounder and wider; the pelvic outlet is wider; the sacrum is shorter and less curved. All of these features create more room for a baby to pass through during birth.

Femur 4 questions

07 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the proximal features of the femur.

Reveal answer

Head (articulates with the acetabulum, has a small pit called the fovea capitis for the ligament of the head), neck (a narrowed region distal to the head, commonly fractured in 'broken hip'), greater trochanter (lateral, large, palpable, attachment for hip abductors), lesser trochanter (medial, attachment for iliopsoas), intertrochanteric line (anterior) and intertrochanteric crest (posterior, where the trochanters connect).

08 DOK 1 · Recall

Name a major feature of the femoral shaft.

Reveal answer

Linea aspera: a longitudinal ridge running down the posterior shaft, attachment site for multiple thigh muscles. The shaft itself slopes medially from hip to knee, which helps put the foot near the body's midline when standing.

09 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the distal features of the femur.

Reveal answer

Medial and lateral condyles (rounded surfaces that articulate with the tibia at the knee), medial and lateral epicondyles (palpable bumps above the condyles, for ligament and muscle attachment), patellar surface (smooth anterior groove between the condyles where the patella tracks), intercondylar fossa (deep notch between the condyles on the posterior side, where the cruciate ligaments attach).

10 DOK 1 · Recall

What kind of bone is the patella, and what does it do?

Reveal answer

The patella is a sesamoid bone (embedded in the tendon of the quadriceps femoris). It improves the mechanical advantage of the quadriceps by holding the tendon away from the joint, and it protects the front of the knee.

Tibia and fibula 4 questions

11 DOK 1 · Recall

Which leg bone is medial and weight-bearing, and which is lateral and not weight-bearing?

Reveal answer

The tibia is medial and bears nearly all of the body's weight transmitted from the femur. The fibula is lateral and slender, and it does not bear significant weight; its job is to provide muscle attachment and stabilize the ankle.

12 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the major landmarks of the tibia.

Reveal answer

Tibial condyles (medial and lateral, articulate with the femoral condyles), intercondylar eminence (a midline projection between the condyles), tibial tuberosity (anterior bump where the patellar ligament attaches), anterior border (the sharp shin you can feel), medial malleolus (the medial bump at the ankle, articulates with the talus).

13 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the major landmarks of the fibula.

Reveal answer

Head (proximal, articulates with the lateral tibial condyle, the common fibular nerve wraps around it), shaft (slender), lateral malleolus (the lateral bump at the ankle, articulates with the talus).

14 DOK 2 · Apply

Describe how the tibia, fibula, and talus form the ankle joint.

Reveal answer

The medial malleolus of the tibia, the lateral malleolus of the fibula, and the inferior surface of the tibia form a U-shaped socket (the ankle mortise) that grips the trochlea of the talus on three sides. This is a hinge joint allowing dorsiflexion and plantarflexion. The malleoli stop side-to-side motion of the talus, which is why an inversion sprain instead twists the foot and tears ligaments.

Foot 4 questions

15 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the seven tarsal bones.

Reveal answer

Talus, calcaneus, navicular, cuboid, and three cuneiforms (medial, intermediate, lateral). The talus articulates with the tibia and fibula above and the calcaneus below. The calcaneus is the heel bone, the largest tarsal.

16 DOK 1 · Recall

How are the metatarsals and phalanges of the foot organized?

Reveal answer

Five metatarsals, numbered I through V from medial (great toe side) to lateral. Each toe has three phalanges (proximal, middle, distal) except the great toe (hallux), which has two.

17 DOK 2 · Apply

Name the arches of the foot and explain what they do.

Reveal answer

Medial longitudinal arch (highest, from calcaneus through talus, navicular, cuneiforms, to the first three metatarsals), lateral longitudinal arch (lower, from calcaneus through cuboid to the fourth and fifth metatarsals), transverse arch (across the foot at the bases of the metatarsals). The arches distribute body weight to the heel and the heads of the metatarsals, and they spring back as you push off, making walking and running efficient.

18 DOK 3 · Reason

In elderly patients, a 'broken hip' usually refers to a fracture of the femoral neck. Predict why this region fractures more easily with age and what makes it dangerous.

Reveal answer

The femoral neck is a thin region of bone that bears the entire weight of the body transmitted through the hip joint. As bone density decreases with age (especially in postmenopausal women due to osteoporosis), the neck becomes brittle and fractures with minor falls. The blood supply to the femoral head runs along the neck; a displaced fracture can cut off that supply, leading to avascular necrosis of the head. Surgical fixation or hip replacement is often required.
