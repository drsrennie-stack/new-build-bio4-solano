Anatomy of the Cell

A parasite that hijacks one specific surface of one specific cell. Why cell architecture matters at the bedside.

Patient case

Sarah is 24, a graduate student who loves hiking. One weekend she and several friends spend three days backpacking in the Sierra Nevada mountains. The weather is warm, the scenery is beautiful, and everything seems to be going perfectly.

On the second day of the trip, Sarah runs low on water. She notices a clear mountain stream and refills her bottle. The water looks clean. It tastes clean. She doesn't think much about it.

About a week later Sarah begins feeling unwell. At first it's mild: bloating, abdominal cramps, more gas than usual. Then it gets worse, with watery, foul-smelling diarrhea that won't quit. She loses five pounds in a few days. Her physician orders a stool test and the result comes back: Giardia duodenalis, a single-celled parasite that lives in mountain water and infects the small intestine.

Guiding question The cells in Sarah's small intestine are doing a very specific job, and they are built with a very specific architecture to do it. What is that architecture, what is the parasite attacking, and why does her body lose water through the front door of her gut?

1 The generalized cell

The unit you'll learn first, then keep modifying for the rest of the course

Before you can see why Sarah's intestinal cells are different, you need a baseline cell. The generalized cell is a simplified diagram of "what every human cell has." It has three big parts: a plasma membrane that wraps it, a nucleus that holds its DNA, and a cytoplasm filled with organelles that do the work.

Cells share this floor plan because they share the same fundamental jobs: keep stuff in, keep stuff out, make proteins, generate energy, communicate with neighbors, and reproduce. Specialization is what comes next; the brush-border cells lining Sarah's gut and the neurons in her brain start from the same blueprint and modify it for the work each one needs to do.

Plasma membraneSelectively permeable outer boundary. Phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins. Decides what gets in and what gets out.
NucleusHolds the DNA. Site of transcription. The control center.
CytoplasmEverything between membrane and nucleus. Cytosol (the gel) plus organelles (the machinery).
CytoskeletonInternal scaffolding of microtubules, microfilaments, and intermediate filaments. Gives shape, drives movement.
2 The plasma membrane

A fluid mosaic, and why it has a top and a bottom

The plasma membrane is built from a phospholipid bilayer: two sheets of phospholipid molecules arranged tail-to-tail. The hydrophilic phosphate heads face the watery environment on each side; the hydrophobic fatty-acid tails hide in the middle. Embedded in this bilayer are proteins (channels, pumps, receptors, anchors), cholesterol molecules that tune fluidity, and carbohydrate chains attached to outward-facing proteins and lipids (the glycocalyx). It is called a fluid mosaic because the components float and shift in the plane of the membrane like a mosaic that is also liquid.

The thing most students miss is that the membrane has polarity: in epithelial cells like Sarah's, the membrane facing the gut lumen (the apical surface) is built differently from the membrane facing the bloodstream (the basolateral surface). Different proteins, different pumps, different functions. The apical surface of an intestinal cell carries digestive enzymes and absorbs nutrients; the basolateral surface releases those nutrients into the body. Polarity is what makes "intake" and "output" possible in the same cell.

Why polarity matters for Sarah. If the apical and basolateral membranes weren't kept separate, the cell could not move sugar and water from gut to blood. Tight junctions between neighboring cells maintain that separation. Damage the junctions and the gut leaks, in both directions.
3 The nucleus

The library that runs the cell

The nucleus is the cell's largest organelle. It contains the cell's DNA, packaged into chromatin. A double membrane called the nuclear envelope surrounds it; pores in that envelope (nuclear pores) let RNA and proteins cross in and out. Inside the nucleus is a darker region called the nucleolus, where ribosomes are assembled.

Think of the nucleus as a reference library that never lends out its books. DNA stays inside; instead, the cell makes copies of the relevant genes as messenger RNA (mRNA), which exit through the nuclear pores and travel to ribosomes in the cytoplasm where the protein is built. Sarah's intestinal cells make brush-border enzymes the same way: gene transcribed in nucleus, mRNA exported, protein built in cytoplasm, protein delivered to apical surface.

4 Cytoplasm and organelles

The factory floor

Outside the nucleus, inside the plasma membrane, the cell is packed with organelles ("little organs"). Each one has a specific job and a specific structure that makes the job possible.

MitochondriaDouble-membrane organelle with infoldings (cristae). Generates ATP through aerobic respiration. Cells with high energy demand (cardiac muscle, brush border) carry many of them.
Rough ER (endoplasmic reticulum)Network of membrane sacs studded with ribosomes. Makes membrane proteins and secreted proteins.
Smooth ERNo ribosomes. Synthesizes lipids and steroids, detoxifies drugs, stores calcium.
Golgi apparatusStack of flattened sacs that modifies, sorts, and packages proteins from the rough ER. Cell's "shipping department."
LysosomesMembrane-bound bags of digestive enzymes. Break down worn-out organelles and ingested material. The cell's recycling and demolition crew.
PeroxisomesSmaller than lysosomes. Break down fatty acids and detoxify hydrogen peroxide.
RibosomesNot membrane-bound. Read mRNA and build proteins. Free in cytoplasm (proteins stay in the cell) or attached to rough ER (proteins for export).
CytoskeletonMicrofilaments (actin), intermediate filaments (keratin and others), microtubules. Maintain shape, anchor organelles, drive movement, build the brush border in Sarah's intestinal cells.

The brush border on the apical surface of an intestinal cell is built from microvilli, each one supported by a core bundle of actin microfilaments. Thousands of microvilli on a single cell multiply the surface area for digestion and absorption by twentyfold or more. This is where Sarah's cells do most of their work, and it is exactly where Giardia attacks.

5 The path materials take through the cell

How something gets from outside, to where it needs to be, and out

Cells move material across the plasma membrane in several ways, scaled to what they need to move.

MechanismHowEnergyExample
Simple diffusionsmall or fat-soluble molecules slip directly through the bilayerpassive (no ATP)oxygen, carbon dioxide
Facilitated diffusionmolecule moves through a protein channel or carrier, down its concentration gradientpassiveglucose into red blood cells via GLUT-1
Osmosiswater moves through the membrane (often via aquaporin channels) toward higher solute concentrationpassivewater following sodium and glucose into intestinal cells
Active transportprotein pump uses ATP to move a molecule against its gradientrequires ATPNa-K ATPase on the basolateral surface of intestinal cells
Endocytosismembrane folds inward and pinches off, bringing material into the cell in a vesiclerequires ATPcells engulfing bacteria (phagocytosis), or LDL uptake
Exocytosisvesicle inside the cell fuses with the plasma membrane and releases its contents outsiderequires ATPsecretion of digestive enzymes, hormones, neurotransmitters

Inside the cell, material doesn't just float around freely; it gets handed from compartment to compartment in vesicles. A protein bound for secretion is made on the rough ER, packaged into a vesicle that buds off and travels to the Golgi, modified and re-packaged in another vesicle, and finally delivered to the plasma membrane where it fuses and releases. This is the cell's logistics system.

6 Cells vary with their job

The brush-border cell, optimized for absorption

The cells that line Sarah's small intestine are simple columnar epithelial cells with a brush border. Their architecture is the textbook example of how form follows function:

Apical microvilli (brush border)Thousands of finger-like projections on the lumen-facing surface, each supported by actin filaments. Carry digestive enzymes and transporters. Multiply absorptive surface dramatically.
Tight junctionsJust below the apical surface, seal cell-to-cell so material has to go THROUGH cells, not between them. Maintain apical/basolateral polarity.
Many mitochondriaActive transport on the basolateral side is energy-hungry. Mitochondria pile up to supply ATP.
Basal nucleusPositioned at the bottom of the cell, out of the way of the absorptive machinery on top.
Basement membraneA thin sheet of extracellular matrix below the cell, anchoring the epithelium to underlying connective tissue and providing a route to capillaries.
Goblet cells interspersedSpecialized for mucus secretion. Lubricate and protect the surface.

The same blueprint becomes a neuron by elongating into an axon and dropping organelles into the cell body. Becomes a red blood cell by ejecting its nucleus and packing the cytoplasm with hemoglobin. Becomes a cardiac muscle cell by filling with mitochondria and contractile proteins. Cells vary because the body needs them to do different things. Same parts list, different proportions.

Returning to Sarah

One parasite, one cell architecture, every symptom explained

Giardia duodenalis swims down the lumen of the small intestine and lands on the brush-border surface of the duodenum and proximal jejunum. Its ventral adhesive disc, a flat cytoskeletal suction cup made largely of microtubules, presses onto the microvilli and holds the parasite in place. It doesn't invade the cell. It doesn't need to. What it does is disrupt the surface that Sarah's body uses to absorb everything.

"Watery diarrhea"
Giardia damages microvilli, reducing brush-border surface and blunting brush-border disaccharidases. Sugars (including lactose) can't be split and absorbed. They stay in the lumen, pull water in by osmosis, and exit as watery stool.
"Bloating and excess gas"
Unabsorbed sugars reach the colon, where bacteria ferment them. Fermentation produces gas. The osmotic load also distends bowel loops.
"Foul-smelling stool"
Fat malabsorption from disrupted brush-border lipases gives a steatorrhea quality. Sulfur-containing fermentation products from unabsorbed carbohydrates contribute.
"Five pounds in a few days"
Fluid loss through diarrhea plus reduced absorption of calories. The body literally cannot extract from food what it normally would.
"Why no fever, no blood in stool?"
Giardia attaches to the apical surface but does not invade the epithelium or trigger neutrophilic inflammation the way invasive bacteria (Shigella, invasive E. coli) do. The mechanism is mechanical disruption of absorption, not invasion.
"How will she heal?"
Intestinal epithelium turns over every 3 to 5 days. Once Giardia is treated (typically metronidazole), the crypt cells replace the damaged surface cells quickly. The brush border rebuilds. Lactose intolerance may persist for weeks after treatment because the brush-border lactase is the slowest to recover.

The cell biology is the disease. Polarity, microvilli, tight junctions, osmosis. Sarah's symptoms aren't a list of unrelated complaints. They are what happens when one structure on one surface of one specialized cell gets disrupted. When you go back to the prework, the membrane, the cytoskeleton, and the brush border won't feel abstract. They'll have her face on them.
