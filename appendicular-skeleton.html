The Appendicular Skeleton

The appendicular skeleton is the bones of the limbs and the two girdles that hang them from the axial skeleton. The upper limb is built for mobility; the lower limb is built heavier to carry weight. Dr. Sharilyn Rennie

Part 1

The Upper Extremity

The pectoral girdle plus the arm, forearm, and hand, built for reach and a wide range of motion.

Part 1 · Overview

The upper limb, proximal to distal

Upper limb
  • Pectoral girdle (clavicle + scapula): attaches the limb to the trunk.
  • Arm (shoulder to elbow): the humerus.
  • Forearm (elbow to wrist): the radius and ulna.
  • Hand (wrist to fingertips): carpals, metacarpals, and phalanges.

Part 1 · Pectoral girdle

The clavicle and scapula

Upper limb
  • Clavicle: the S-shaped collarbone. Its sternal end meets the manubrium and its acromial end meets the scapula. It is the only bony link of the upper limb to the axial skeleton.
  • Scapula: the triangular shoulder blade with superior, medial, and lateral borders. Key markings: the spine, the acromion (meets the clavicle), the coracoid process, and the glenoid cavity that receives the head of the humerus.

Part 1 · The arm

The humerus

Upper limb
  • Proximal: the head (into the glenoid cavity), the anatomical neck, the surgical neck (a common fracture site), and the greater and lesser tubercles with the intertubercular sulcus between them.
  • Shaft: the deltoid tuberosity.
  • Distal: the capitulum (meets the radius), the trochlea (meets the ulna), the medial and lateral epicondyles, and the posterior olecranon fossa that receives the ulna when the elbow straightens.

Part 1 · The hand

Carpals, metacarpals, and phalanges

Upper limb
  • Carpals: eight wrist bones in two rows. Proximal: scaphoid, lunate, triquetrum, pisiform; distal: trapezium, trapezoid, capitate, hamate. Memory aid: "So Long To Pinky, Here Comes The Thumb."
  • Metacarpals (5) form the palm; phalanges (14) the fingers. The thumb (pollex) has two; each finger has three.
  • Carpal tunnel: the flexor retinaculum roofs the carpals; the tunnel beneath carries the finger flexor tendons and the median nerve. Narrowing compresses the nerve (carpal tunnel syndrome).
  • Clinically: capitate is largest, scaphoid most often fractured, lunate most often dislocated.

Part 1 · Clinical

When you fall on an outstretched hand

Clinical
  • The clavicle is the most commonly broken bone, usually in its middle third, taking the force of a fall onto the shoulder or arm.
  • The surgical neck of the humerus fractures often, and the axillary nerve runs close by, so the limb is checked for nerve injury.
  • The scaphoid is the carpal most often fractured; its blood supply is poor, so a missed fracture may fail to heal. Tenderness in the anatomical snuffbox is the clue.

Part 2

The Lower Extremity

The pelvic girdle plus the thigh, leg, and foot, built heavier to carry the body's weight.

Part 2 · Overview

The lower limb, proximal to distal

Lower limb
  • Pelvic girdle: the two hip bones, attaching the limb to the axial skeleton.
  • Thigh (hip to knee): the femur (and the patella at the knee).
  • Leg (knee to ankle): the tibia and fibula.
  • Foot (ankle to toes): tarsals, metatarsals, and phalanges.

Part 2 · Pelvic girdle

The hip bone and the pelvis

Lower limb
  • Each hip bone (os coxae) is three bones fused at the acetabulum: the ilium, ischium, and pubis.
  • Ilium markings: iliac crest, anterior and posterior superior iliac spines, iliac fossa, auricular surface (sacroiliac joint), and the greater sciatic notch.
  • The acetabulum is the hip socket; the obturator foramen is the large opening of the ischium and pubis.
  • The bony pelvis = both hip bones + sacrum + coccyx. The female pelvis is wider and shallower with a broader pubic angle, adapted for childbirth.

Part 2 · Thigh & knee

The femur and patella

Lower limb
  • Femur, the longest, strongest bone: the head (with the fovea capitis) fits the acetabulum; the neck is a common fracture site; the greater and lesser trochanters and the posterior linea aspera are muscle attachments.
  • Distally, the medial and lateral condyles meet the tibia, with epicondyles above and the intercondylar fossa behind.
  • Patella: the kneecap, a sesamoid bone in the quadriceps tendon, with a broad base and a pointed apex.

Part 2 · The foot

Tarsals, metatarsals, and arches

Lower limb
  • Tarsals (7): the talus meets the tibia and fibula and takes the body's weight; the calcaneus is the heel and the largest tarsal; plus the navicular, cuboid, and three cuneiforms.
  • Metatarsals (5) form the sole; phalanges (14) the toes. The great toe (hallux) has two; the others three.
  • Arches: the medial and lateral longitudinal arches and the transverse arch spread weight and spring back with each step; a dropped medial arch is flat feet.

Part 2 · Clinical

The skeleton that carries your weight

Clinical
  • A broken hip is usually a femoral neck fracture, common in older adults with bone thinned by osteoporosis; the nearby blood supply makes it threaten the femoral head.
  • The ASIS and iliac crest are surface landmarks for injection sites and pelvic alignment.
  • The tibia bears weight and the fibula does not, so fibula can be harvested for grafts; the lateral malleolus is the common ankle fracture site from a rolled ankle.

Check Your Understanding

From recall to reasoning, then the key takeaways.

Part 4 · Recall (DOK 1)

Name the link

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 1 · Recall

Which bone is the only bony attachment of the upper limb to the axial skeleton?

Answer

Clavicle. Its sternal end meets the manubrium; the scapula attaches to the trunk only through muscles and the clavicle.

Part 4 · Skill / concept (DOK 2)

Apply the anatomy

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 2 · Skill / concept

Why can a length of fibula be removed and used as a bone graft with little effect on standing?

Answer

The fibula does not bear weight. The tibia carries the body's load, so fibula can be harvested for grafting.

Part 4 · Strategic reasoning (DOK 3)

Reason it through

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 3 · Strategic reasoning

An older adult falls and has a broken hip with concern for the femoral head's survival. Why does a femoral neck fracture threaten the head?

Answer

The blood supply runs along the neck. A neck fracture can interrupt circulation to the femoral head, risking its death (avascular necrosis).

Wrap-up

Key takeaways

Summary
  • The appendicular skeleton = the limbs + the two girdles.
  • Upper limb: pectoral girdle (clavicle, scapula) -> humerus -> radius (lateral) + ulna (medial) -> 8 carpals, 5 metacarpals, 14 phalanges.
  • Lower limb: pelvic girdle (ilium + ischium + pubis fuse at the acetabulum) -> femur + patella -> tibia (weight) + fibula -> 7 tarsals, 5 metatarsals, 14 phalanges.
  • Clinical: clavicle and scaphoid break from a fall on an outstretched hand; the femoral neck and lateral malleolus are common lower-limb fracture sites.
  • The limbs correspond part for part, with the lower built heavier for weight-bearing.
