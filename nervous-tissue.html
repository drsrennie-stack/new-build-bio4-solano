The Nervous System, an Overview

The nervous system is the body's fast control network. It is built from two kinds of cell, the neurons and the neuroglia, organized into a central and a peripheral part.

  • Nervous systemthe body's fast control system, which detects change, processes information, and directs responses
  • Central nervous systemthe CNS, the brain and the spinal cord, the control center
  • Peripheral nervous systemthe PNS, all the nervous tissue outside the CNS: the nerves and ganglia
  • Nervous tissuethe tissue of the system, made of neurons and neuroglia
  • Three basic functionssensory input, integration of that information, and motor output

The Neuron

The neuron is the signaling cell. Its shape, with branches in and a long fiber out, suits its one job: receiving a signal and sending it on.

  • Neuronthe signaling cell of the nervous system, which generates and conducts nerve impulses
  • Cell bodyalso called the soma, the part of the neuron that contains the nucleus
  • Dendritesthe branched extensions that receive signals and carry them toward the cell body
  • Axonthe single long extension that carries the signal away from the cell body
  • Axon hillockthe cone-shaped region of the cell body where the axon begins
  • Axon terminalsthe branched endings of the axon that pass the signal on to the next cell
  • Myelin sheaththe fatty insulating wrap around many axons that speeds the nerve impulse
  • Nodes of Ranvierthe regular gaps in the myelin sheath along an axon

Neuroglia

Neuroglia, or glial cells, are the support cells of nervous tissue. They greatly outnumber the neurons. Four types are found in the CNS and two in the PNS. Compare them.

The Synapse

A neuron does not touch the next cell. It passes its signal across a tiny gap, the synapse, using a chemical messenger.

  • Synapsethe junction where a neuron passes its signal to another cell
  • Presynaptic neuronthe neuron that sends the signal across the synapse
  • Postsynaptic cellthe neuron, muscle, or gland that receives the signal
  • Synaptic cleftthe tiny gap between the two cells at a chemical synapse
  • Synaptic vesiclesthe small sacs in the axon terminal that store neurotransmitter
  • Neurotransmitterthe chemical messenger released into the cleft to carry the signal across
  • Neuromuscular junctionthe synapse between a motor neuron and a skeletal muscle fiber

Clinical tie-in

Why myelin keeps coming up

The myelin sheath is an insulating wrap that speeds the nerve impulse. Strip it away and signals slow or stop, which is why so many nervous tissue diseases are myelin diseases. The site of the damage names the disease: lost CNS myelin is multiple sclerosis, lost PNS myelin is Guillain-Barre syndrome.

The repair cells differ too. Schwann cells in the PNS can help a damaged peripheral nerve regrow, so a cut nerve in a limb may slowly recover. The oligodendrocytes of the CNS do not support regrowth in the same way, which is part of why CNS injury is so much harder to reverse.

Most brain tumors are not tumors of neurons at all. They are gliomas, growths of the glial cells, because glia keep the ability to divide while mature neurons largely do not.
