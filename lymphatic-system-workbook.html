Lymph and vessels 4 questions

01 DOK 1 · Recall

What is lymph, where does it come from, and where does it ultimately go?

Reveal answer

Lymph is interstitial fluid that has entered a lymphatic vessel. It originates as filtrate from blood capillaries (the fluid that does not return directly to the venous end of the capillary). It drains through lymphatic capillaries and vessels, passes through lymph nodes for filtering, and returns to the venous blood near the heart via the thoracic duct (left side and most of the body) or the right lymphatic duct.

02 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe lymphatic capillaries and how they let fluid in.

Reveal answer

Lymphatic capillaries are blind-ended tubes lined by overlapping endothelial cells that form one-way mini-valves. Higher interstitial fluid pressure outside pushes the flaps inward and lets fluid enter; once inside, the flaps close so fluid cannot leak back out.

03 DOK 2 · Apply

What drives lymph flow, given that the system has no pump?

Reveal answer

The skeletal muscle pump (squeezing lymphatic vessels), the respiratory pump (pressure changes during breathing), the rhythmic contraction of smooth muscle in larger lymphatic vessels, and valves that prevent backflow. Without these, lymph would stagnate.

04 DOK 2 · Apply

What does the thoracic duct drain, and where does it empty?

Reveal answer

It drains lymph from the entire body below the diaphragm and the left side of the body above the diaphragm. It begins as the cisterna chyli in the upper abdomen and ascends through the thorax to empty into the junction of the left internal jugular and left subclavian veins. The right lymphatic duct drains the smaller right upper region and empties on the right side.

Lymph nodes and lymphoid organs 6 questions

05 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the structure of a lymph node, including how lymph enters and exits.

Reveal answer

A lymph node is a bean-shaped encapsulated organ. Lymph enters through several afferent lymphatic vessels on the convex surface, percolates through sinuses and reticular tissue packed with lymphocytes and macrophages, then exits through one or two efferent vessels at the indented hilum. The node has an outer cortex (rich in B-cell follicles and germinal centers) and an inner medulla.

06 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the function of a lymph node?

Reveal answer

To filter lymph and survey for pathogens. Macrophages in the sinuses phagocytose debris and microbes. B and T cells encounter antigens here and initiate adaptive immune responses. Swollen 'glands' during infection are lymph nodes actively producing immune cells.

07 DOK 1 · Recall

Where is the thymus, what is it, and what does it do?

Reveal answer

The thymus is a bilobed lymphoid organ in the superior mediastinum, anterior to the heart and great vessels. It is a primary lymphoid organ where T lymphocytes mature. It is most active in childhood and shrinks (involutes) with age, replaced largely by fat in adults.

08 DOK 1 · Recall

Where is the spleen, and what does it do?

Reveal answer

In the left upper quadrant, deep to the ninth through eleventh ribs. It is the largest lymphoid organ. It filters blood (not lymph), removes old red blood cells, recycles iron, stores platelets, and mounts immune responses to blood-borne pathogens. White pulp does immune work; red pulp does the red-cell filtering.

09 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the major tonsils and where each sits.

Reveal answer

Palatine tonsils sit on either side of the back of the throat (oropharynx). Pharyngeal tonsil (adenoid) sits in the nasopharynx. Lingual tonsils sit at the base of the tongue. Tubal tonsils sit around the openings of the auditory tubes. Together they form a ring of lymphoid tissue (Waldeyer's ring) guarding the entrance to the pharynx.

10 DOK 1 · Recall

What is MALT, and where is it most prominent?

Reveal answer

Mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue: scattered lymphoid follicles in the walls of mucous membranes. Most prominent in the gut (Peyer's patches in the ileum, the appendix), the airways (BALT, bronchus-associated), and the urogenital tract. MALT protects against pathogens that enter at mucosal surfaces.

Clinical reasoning 2 questions

11 DOK 3 · Reason

After mastectomy with axillary lymph node removal, the arm on the same side sometimes swells (lymphedema). Predict why and explain using lymphatic anatomy.

Reveal answer

The axillary nodes drain the arm. Removing them eliminates the downstream collection point, so lymph from the arm has nowhere to go. Interstitial fluid that would normally be returned to circulation through the lymphatic system accumulates instead, swelling the arm. Compression therapy and lymphatic massage are used to encourage drainage through alternate routes.

12 DOK 3 · Reason

A patient has a sore throat and a tender swollen lymph node in the neck. Using node anatomy, explain why the node enlarged.

Reveal answer

Bacteria or viral antigens from the throat reached the cervical nodes through afferent lymphatics. B and T cells in the node began rapid proliferation in response, expanding the germinal centers and the node as a whole. The swelling is the visible sign of an active immune response. As the infection clears, the node returns to normal size over days to weeks.
