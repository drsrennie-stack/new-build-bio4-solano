BIO 004 · Course Mechanic

How the case clues work.

Each week, a small clue drops Monday, Tuesday, and Wednesday. Thursday, the full clinical workup arrives and your team applies the week's anatomy to it. Here is how to use the system so it pays off.

Back to syllabus Course home
Why we do it this way

The clues are a frame for your anatomy.

Anatomy memorized in isolation fades. Anatomy attached to a real-feeling person sticks. The case-clue system gives you a patient at the start of each week, then asks you to learn the structures that will help your team make sense of that patient on Thursday.

The clues themselves do not solve the case. They cannot. They give you context: who the patient is, what they do, what they noticed. The clinical findings (exam, imaging, labs) arrive on Thursday with the full TBL application activity, and that is when your team puts the pieces together.

By the time Thursday's case lands, you have spent three days quietly priming the structures that matter. The TBL session becomes about reasoning, not about scrambling to remember anatomy under time pressure.

The week, clue by clue

One case, unfolding across the week.

  1. Who the patient is

    Monday

    One or two sentences. Age, occupation, life context. Just enough to start picturing a real person. Read it once, hold it in mind during lab.

  2. What they noticed

    Tuesday

    The first thing the patient noticed, when, and in what context. Re-read Monday's clue alongside it. Notice the pattern starting to build.

  3. What changed

    Wednesday

    What got worse, sharper, or different, and when. Re-read all three clues together. Notice what is still missing. You should not be able to diagnose anything yet.

  4. The clinical workup

    Thursday TBL

    Physical exam, range of motion, neurological screen, imaging, labs. This is when your team integrates the week's anatomy with the clinical findings and works the application activity.

What clues do, what they don't

Use them for what they are.

The clues are a study tool, not a quiz. They will not contain enough information to figure out the diagnosis before Thursday. If you think you have solved it by Tuesday, you have made up information that is not there. Wait.

Do

  • Read each day's clue. It takes under a minute.
  • Hold the patient in mind during that day's lab work and atlas study.
  • Let the clues guide your attention toward likely-relevant structures.
  • Re-read all the clues so far before Thursday's TBL session.

Don't

  • Try to diagnose the case from clues alone.
  • Skip your assigned anatomy reading because you have a hunch.
  • Argue with teammates on Thursday based on Monday's hypothesis.
  • Worry if your guess turns out to be wrong. That is the point.
The clues do not replace your prep. Your iRAT and tRAT still test the week's assigned anatomy reading. Read first. Use the clues to make that reading feel relevant.
If you miss a day

No one falls behind because of a missed clue.

Each clue takes under sixty seconds to read. If you miss a day, open Canvas the next morning and read the missed clue alongside the new one. You will be caught up before your next class.

Thursday's TBL application activity opens with a brief recap of all three clues, so even a student who saw none of them lands on the same page as their team for the workup.
