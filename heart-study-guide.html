1. Location, surfaces, apex and base

Surface anatomy
  1. Where in the thoracic cavity does the heart sit, and which cavity of the mediastinum holds it? Recall
  2. Which structures form the anterior, inferior, right, and left surfaces of the heart? Recall
  3. Which chamber forms the apex, and which chambers form the base? Recall
  4. In which direction does the apex point, and on what structure does it rest? Apply
  5. Name the three cardiac sulci and the vessel each one carries. Apply
  6. Why does roughly two-thirds of the heart lie to the left of the midline? Reason

2. Pericardium and pericarditis

Covering
  1. Distinguish the fibrous pericardium from the serous pericardium, and name the two layers of the serous pericardium. Recall
  2. What is the pericardial cavity, what fluid fills it, and what does that fluid do? Recall
  3. To what structure is the fibrous pericardium anchored inferiorly, and why does that matter for breathing? Apply
  4. Define pericarditis. How do acute and chronic pericarditis differ in cause and course? Apply
  5. What is a pericardial friction rub, and which layers produce it? Reason
  6. Trace how chronic pericarditis can lead to cardiac tamponade, and explain how pericardiocentesis treats it. Reason

3. Heart wall and its disorders

Wall layers
  1. Name the three layers of the heart wall from superficial to deep. Recall
  2. Which wall layer is also the visceral serous pericardium, and what is it called? Recall
  3. What tissue makes up the myocardium, and roughly what fraction of the wall is it? Recall
  4. What lines the endocardium, and why is a smooth lining important for blood flow? Apply
  5. Compare myocarditis and endocarditis by cause, signs, and treatment. Apply
  6. Why does bacterial endocarditis so often involve the valves? Reason

4. Chambers and internal features

Chambers
  1. Which chambers are the receiving chambers and which are the pumping chambers? Recall
  2. Name the four veins that drain into the right atrium. Recall
  3. Identify each internal feature and give its purpose: interatrial septum, fossa ovalis, pectinate muscles, auricle, trabeculae carneae, moderator band, papillary muscles, chordae tendineae. Apply
  4. Compare the wall thickness and lumen shape of the right and left ventricles. Apply
  5. Both ventricles eject the same volume per beat. Why is the left ventricular wall so much thicker? Reason
  6. Why are pectinate muscles confined to the atria while trabeculae carneae line the ventricles? Reason

5. Fetal structures and congenital defects

Development
  1. Name the two fetal shunts and the adult remnant each one becomes. Recall
  2. Where is the foramen ovale, and which way does blood move through it? Recall
  3. What does the ductus arteriosus connect, and why is that shunt useful before birth? Apply
  4. How does an atrial septal defect relate to the fetal foramen ovale? Apply
  5. List the four components of tetralogy of Fallot. Apply
  6. In transposition of the great vessels, which vessel leaves each ventricle, and why is this life-threatening? Reason

6. Valves and the fibrous skeleton

Valves
  1. Name the four valves, their type (AV or semilunar), number of cusps, and the chambers or vessels each lies between. Recall
  2. What holds the AV-valve cusps shut against ventricular pressure? Recall
  3. Describe when each valve set opens and closes during the pumping cycle. Apply
  4. List the four functions of the fibrous skeleton of the heart. Apply
  5. Explain how the fibrous skeleton acts as an electrical insulator and why that matters for the heartbeat. Reason
  6. Predict what would happen to blood flow if the chordae tendineae of an AV valve ruptured. Reason

7. Valve disorders

Clinical
  1. Define valve stenosis and valve insufficiency (incompetence). Recall
  2. What happens to a cusp in mitral valve prolapse, and how common is it? Recall
  3. How can rheumatic fever damage the heart valves? Apply
  4. Which valve is replaced most often, and what materials are used for replacement? Apply
  5. Predict how aortic stenosis would change the workload of the left ventricle over time. Reason

8. Cardiac muscle tissue

Histology
  1. List the distinguishing features of a cardiac muscle cell (shape, nucleus, branching, striations). Recall
  2. What is an intercalated disc, and what two structures does it contain? Recall
  3. State the job of desmosomes versus gap junctions at the intercalated disc. Apply
  4. Why does cardiac muscle hold so many large mitochondria compared with skeletal muscle? Reason

9. Great vessels and the blood pathway

Circulation
  1. Name the great vessels that return blood to the heart and those that carry blood away. Recall
  2. Trace a drop of blood from the right atrium all the way to the aorta, naming every chamber and valve in order. Apply
  3. Distinguish the systemic circuit from the pulmonary circuit, including which side of the heart drives each. Apply
  4. At which point in the pathway does blood change from oxygen-poor to oxygen-rich, and where does the reverse happen? Reason

10. Coronary circulation

Blood supply
  1. Where do the coronary arteries arise, and why does the myocardium need its own blood supply? Recall
  2. Name the major cardiac veins, the sulcus each runs in, and where they ultimately drain. Apply
  3. Why do the coronary arteries fill during heart relaxation rather than contraction? Reason
  4. Define an anastomosis and explain how collateral circulation can protect the heart when a vessel narrows. Reason

11. Conduction system (anatomy)

Conduction
  1. Name the parts of the conduction system in order, from the pacemaker to the ventricular muscle. Recall
  2. Where is the SA node located? Where is the AV node located? Recall
  3. Which part of the conduction system crosses the fibrous skeleton, and why is it the only route from the atria to the ventricles? Apply
  4. How does the moderator band relate to the conduction system? Apply
  5. Explain how the insulation provided by the fibrous skeleton forces the atria to contract before the ventricles. Reason
