Neurons 5 questions

01 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the three main parts of a neuron and the role of each.

Reveal answer

Cell body (soma, perikaryon): houses the nucleus and most organelles; metabolic center. Dendrites: branched processes that receive incoming signals from other neurons and conduct them toward the cell body. Axon: a single long process that carries the outgoing signal away from the cell body to another neuron, muscle, or gland.

02 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the major features of an axon.

Reveal answer

Axon hillock (where the axon leaves the cell body, the trigger zone for action potentials), axon collaterals (side branches), telodendria (terminal branches), axon terminals (the ends, also called synaptic boutons or synaptic knobs).

03 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare the three structural types of neurons.

Reveal answer

Multipolar: many dendrites and one axon; most neurons (motor neurons, most CNS neurons). Bipolar: one dendrite and one axon; uncommon, found in special sensory tissues (retina, olfactory epithelium). Unipolar (pseudounipolar): one process that splits into two; sensory neurons of the PNS.

04 DOK 1 · Recall

Compare the three functional types of neurons.

Reveal answer

Sensory (afferent): carry signals from receptors to the CNS. Motor (efferent): carry signals from the CNS to muscles or glands. Interneurons (association neurons): connect sensory and motor neurons within the CNS; the vast majority of neurons.

05 DOK 1 · Recall

What are Nissl bodies in a neuron, and what does their presence tell you?

Reveal answer

Nissl bodies are clusters of rough endoplasmic reticulum and ribosomes in the cell body and dendrites. They reflect the neuron's heavy protein-synthesis demand. They are notably absent from the axon hillock and axon, which is a useful microscopic landmark.

Neuroglia 4 questions

06 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the four glial cells of the central nervous system and what each does.

Reveal answer

Astrocytes: most abundant glial cell; support and feed neurons, help form the blood-brain barrier, regulate the extracellular environment. Oligodendrocytes: wrap CNS axons in myelin (one oligodendrocyte myelinates multiple axons). Microglia: small phagocytic cells, the immune cells of the CNS. Ependymal cells: line the ventricles and central canal, help circulate cerebrospinal fluid.

07 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the two glial cells of the peripheral nervous system and what each does.

Reveal answer

Schwann cells: wrap PNS axons in myelin (each Schwann cell myelinates only one section of one axon). Satellite cells: surround the cell bodies of PNS neurons in ganglia and provide support.

08 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare myelination in the CNS and PNS.

Reveal answer

In the CNS, oligodendrocytes wrap multiple axons (one cell myelinates several axons, with each process forming one segment). In the PNS, Schwann cells wrap a single axon segment (one Schwann cell per myelin internode). The lipid composition and surface proteins also differ slightly. Both systems leave small unmyelinated gaps called nodes of Ranvier between adjacent myelin segments.

09 DOK 2 · Apply

What are nodes of Ranvier, and why do they matter functionally?

Reveal answer

Nodes of Ranvier are small gaps in the myelin sheath between adjacent myelinating cells. Action potentials jump from node to node (saltatory conduction), which is much faster than continuous conduction along an unmyelinated axon. Myelinated fibers therefore conduct signals far more rapidly than unmyelinated ones.

Synapse and signal transmission 2 questions

10 DOK 1 · Recall

What is a synapse, and name its main structural components.

Reveal answer

A synapse is the site where one neuron communicates with another cell (a neuron, muscle fiber, or gland cell). Components: presynaptic axon terminal (with vesicles of neurotransmitter), synaptic cleft (the narrow gap between cells), and postsynaptic membrane (with receptors for the neurotransmitter).

11 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare chemical and electrical synapses.

Reveal answer

Chemical synapses use neurotransmitter molecules released into a synaptic cleft to communicate; they introduce a small delay but are versatile and can be excitatory or inhibitory. Electrical synapses use gap junctions to pass current directly from one cell to the next; they are fast and synchronize cell activity (common in cardiac muscle and some smooth muscle). Most synapses in the nervous system are chemical.
