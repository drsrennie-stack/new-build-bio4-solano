What a great session looks like

You don't need a lesson plan. The best sessions are simple: everyone is doing the anatomy out loud, not watching one person talk. Your job as host is to keep people active and on the clock.

Why this matters

An 8-week intensive is a lot, fast. If you feel behind or overwhelmed some weeks, that isn't a you problem, it's the pace, and almost everyone around you feels it too, even the ones who look like they have it together.

You are not meant to do this alone. The students who get through are usually the ones who found a few people to study with, lean on, and keep going with on the hard weeks. A little encouragement when someone is fried is sometimes the thing that keeps them from quitting. That might be them for you, or you for them.

And here is the quiet part: teaching someone else is one of the best ways to learn it yourself. When you host, you are not just doing a favor, you are locking the material in for your own exam. Share the load, quiz each other, send the encouraging text. That is what gets a whole group to the finish line, together.

Pick one thing to do

Quiz each other

Call out a structure, the group names it. Take turns being the caller.

Play a Kahoot

Make a quick one on this week's structures and run it together.

Model or image ID

Pull up the atlas or a model and identify the parts one by one.

Draw it from memory

Everyone sketches and labels a structure with no notes, then compares. Given, not Googled.

Work a practice set

Do problems with an AI tutor, then talk out why each answer is right or wrong.

A simple run of show

  1. Warm up. Everyone names five structures from the last class.
  2. Do your one thing. Spend the bulk of the time on it, and take turns so no one hides.
  3. Hit the misses. Re-quiz whatever people got wrong. The misses are the point.
  4. Wrap. Each person says one thing they'll review tonight.

Keep it on track

  • Start and end on time, and set a timer. A session with an end time gets more done.
  • Name the goal in the first minute: "Today we nail the forearm muscles." One topic beats trying to cover everything.
  • A few minutes to catch up is fine, then get to work. Park the off-topic talk for the end.
  • If it drifts, pull it back with a question: "Okay, who can name the carpals?" Keep everyone doing, not just chatting.

Make it count

  • Keep it 30 to 60 minutes. Short and focused beats long and loose.
  • If you're on Zoom, hit record at the start. That recording is what earns your host credit.
  • Afterward, post your recording link and attendance on the schedule so everyone gets tracked.
  • Check answers against the course notes or Recall Rx. If the group isn't sure, look it up, don't guess.
