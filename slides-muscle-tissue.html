Part 1

Muscle Tissue

Three types, their traits, and what muscle does.

Tissue types

Three types of muscle tissue

Core
Histology

Key points

  • Skeletal: attached to the skeleton; the longest fibers, striated, voluntary.
  • Cardiac: heart walls; striated, involuntary, with intercalated discs (desmosomes for adhesion, gap junctions for electrical coupling), branching fibers, one central nucleus.
  • Smooth: walls of hollow organs and vessels; spindle-shaped, no striations, involuntary, slow sustained contractions.
Three types, one job done three ways: skeletal moves you, cardiac pumps, smooth squeezes.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Tissue types

Comparing the three

Core
Reference

Key points

  • Skeletal uses many peripheral nuclei; cardiac one central nucleus; smooth one central nucleus.
  • Striations: present in skeletal and cardiac, absent in smooth.
  • Smooth muscle uses dense bodies (the Z-disc equivalent) and caveolae (instead of T-tubules); visceral smooth muscle has gap junctions.
Only smooth is unstriated; only cardiac is branched with intercalated discs.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Properties

Characteristics of muscle tissue

Core
Four traits

Key points

  • Excitability: responds to a stimulus by changing membrane potential.
  • Contractility: shortens and thickens when stimulated.
  • Extensibility: can stretch beyond resting length.
  • Elasticity: recoils to resting length after stretch.
Excite it, it contracts; stretch it, it springs back.

Roles

Functions of muscle tissue

Core
What muscle does

Key points

  • Produce movement (locomotion and moving substances through organs).
  • Maintain posture and body position.
  • Stabilize joints.
  • Generate heat.
Move, hold, stabilize, warm.

Language

Muscle terminology

Core
Naming the parts

Key points

  • Muscle fibers: elongated muscle cells (skeletal and smooth).
  • Prefixes: myo- and mys- mean muscle; sarco- means flesh.
  • Sarcoplasm: the muscle cytoplasm. Sarcolemma: the muscle plasma membrane.
When you see sarco- or myo-, think muscle.

Part 2

Organization and Attachments

Wrappings, supply, and how muscle grips bone.

Connective tissue

The connective-tissue sheaths

Core
Wrappings

Key points

  • Epimysium: dense irregular CT around the entire muscle.
  • Perimysium: dense irregular CT around each fascicle (bundle of fibers).
  • Endomysium: areolar CT around each fiber.
  • The sheaths converge into the tendon at the ends.
Three wrappings, outer to inner: whole muscle, bundle, single fiber.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Supply

Nerve and blood supply

Core
Innervation

Key points

  • Typically one nerve, one artery, and one or more veins serve each muscle.
  • A rich capillary bed feeds the fibers; capillaries coil and straighten as the muscle changes length.
  • Each fiber is innervated so it can be activated individually.
Every fiber has its own nerve ending and a capillary nearby.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Attachments

Muscle attachments

Core
Origin and insertion

Key points

  • A muscle attaches to bone at an origin (usually the stationary, proximal bone) and an insertion (the movable, distal bone pulled toward the origin).
  • Direct (fleshy): the epimysium fuses to the periosteum.
  • Indirect: a tendon (cord) or aponeurosis (sheet) bridges to bone; more common, durable, and space-saving.
Origin holds still, insertion moves; the connection is direct or through a tendon.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Naming

How muscles are named

Core
Vocabulary

Key points

  • Direction: rectus, transverse, oblique. Size: maximus, minimus, longus, brevis.
  • Shape: deltoid, trapezius, serratus, rhomboid. Action: flexor, extensor, abductor, adductor.
  • Number of origins: biceps, triceps, quadriceps. Location/attachments: temporalis, sternocleidomastoid.
A muscle's name is a clue to its direction, size, shape, action, or attachments.

Part 3

Levers and Coordination

How muscle and bone produce movement.

Biomechanics

Lever systems

Mechanism
Levers

Key points

  • Bones act as levers, joints act as fulcrums.
  • Effort (muscle contraction) works against load (the part moved or resistance); motion happens when effort exceeds load.
  • First class: fulcrum between effort and load (head on the spine). Second class: load between (standing on toes). Third class: effort between (elbow flexion, most common).
  • Load closer to the fulcrum = mechanical advantage (less effort).
Levers trade force for speed or range; most body levers are third-class.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Teamwork

Coordination among muscles

Mechanism
Muscle roles

Key points

  • Muscles work in opposing (antagonistic) pairs: flexor and extensor, abductor and adductor.
  • Agonist (prime mover) causes the action; antagonist yields to it.
  • Synergists assist or prevent unwanted movement; fixators stabilize the origin.
  • A compartment is a group of muscles, vessels, and nerves with a shared function.
For every mover there is an opposer, a helper, and a stabilizer.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Clinical

Benefits of stretching

Clinical
Clinical

Key points

  • Goal: normal range of motion and soft-tissue mobility.
  • Static stretching: slow, sustained, held about 30 seconds, best after a warm-up.
  • Benefits: better performance, less injury risk, reduced soreness, improved posture.
Warm up first, then hold the stretch; the payoff is mobility and fewer injuries.

Part 4

Inside the Fiber

From whole fiber down to the filaments.

Microanatomy

The skeletal muscle fiber

Core
The cell

Key points

  • A long cylindrical, multinucleated cell.
  • Sarcoplasm is rich in glycosomes (glycogen) and myoglobin (oxygen store).
  • Specialized organelles: myofibrils, sarcoplasmic reticulum, and T-tubules.
One giant cell with many nuclei, packed with contractile machinery.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Microanatomy

Myofibrils

Core
Contractile rods

Key points

  • Rod-like organelles filling about 80% of the cell.
  • Made of repeating sarcomeres, the contractile units.
  • The alternating dark A bands and light I bands create the striations.
Striations are just sarcomeres lined up in rows.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Microanatomy

The sarcomere

Mechanism
Contractile unit

Key points

  • The smallest contractile unit, running Z disc to Z disc.
  • A band (dark): thick filaments (with overlap). I band (light): thin filaments only.
  • H zone: lighter mid-A-band (thick only). M line: anchors thick filaments. Z disc: anchors thin filaments.
  • On contraction: I bands shorten, Z discs pull closer, H zones vanish, but A bands stay the same length.
The A band never shortens; everything that changes tells you the filaments slid.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Microanatomy

Myofilaments

Mechanism
Thick and thin

Key points

  • Thick filaments (myosin): two heavy and four light chains; a rod-like tail and globular heads that form cross-bridges with actin.
  • Thin filaments (actin): twisted strands carrying myosin-binding sites.
Myosin heads reach out; actin carries the sites they grab.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Microanatomy

Regulatory and structural proteins

Mechanism
Control and scaffold

Key points

  • Tropomyosin: blocks the binding sites at rest.
  • Troponin complex: TnI (binds actin), TnT (binds tropomyosin), TnC (binds Ca2+).
  • Titin: elastic filament from Z disc to M line; stabilizes thick filaments and adds recoil.
  • Dystrophin links thin filaments to the sarcolemma; nebulin and myomesin keep alignment.
Troponin and tropomyosin are the switch; titin and dystrophin are the scaffolding.

Part 5

The Excitation Apparatus

How the signal reaches the filaments.

Organelles

Sarcoplasmic reticulum

Mechanism
Calcium store

Key points

  • A specialized smooth ER network wrapping each myofibril.
  • Terminal cisterns sit at the A-I junction.
  • Function: stores and releases calcium (Ca2+).
The SR is the fiber's calcium warehouse, parked right on the myofibrils.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Organelles

T-tubules and triads

Mechanism
Signal delivery

Key points

  • T-tubules: sarcolemma invaginations that carry the depolarization deep and fast into the fiber.
  • A triad = one T-tubule flanked by two terminal cisterns.
  • Integral proteins act as voltage sensors (on the T-tubule) coupled to Ca2+ release channels (on the SR).
The T-tubule carries the message in; the triad hands it to the calcium store.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Junction

The neuromuscular junction

Mechanism
Where nerve meets muscle

Key points

  • Axon terminal: synaptic vesicles filled with acetylcholine (ACh).
  • Synaptic cleft: the gap between nerve and muscle.
  • Motor end plate: folded sarcolemma studded with ACh receptors.
  • Schwann cells cap the axon terminal.
Nerve releases ACh across a gap onto a folded, receptor-rich end plate.

Part 6

Fiber Types, Repair, and Contraction

Varieties, regeneration, and the sliding filament model.

Variation

Fiber types

Mechanism
Histological classes

Key points

  • Type I (slow oxidative): red, small, many mitochondria, fatigue-resistant.
  • Type IIa (fast oxidative-glycolytic): intermediate properties.
  • Type IIb/x (fast glycolytic): white, large, powerful, fatigue quickly.
Red and slow for endurance; white and fast for power.

Repair

Satellite cells

Core
Regeneration

Key points

  • Stem-cell-like cells lying between the sarcolemma and basal lamina.
  • Quiescent until activated by injury.
  • Give skeletal muscle a limited capacity to regenerate.
A small reserve of stem cells means muscle heals, but only so much.

Contraction

The sliding filament model

Mechanism
How it shortens

Key points

  • Myosin heads pull the actin filaments toward the center, so the sarcomere shortens.
  • I bands shorten and H zones disappear.
  • The A bands stay the same length because the filaments slide, they do not shrink.
Nothing shortens except the overlap: the filaments slide past each other.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Clinical

Clinical correlations

Clinical
Disease

Key points

  • Duchenne muscular dystrophy: defective dystrophin leaves the fiber fragile, so it tears with use.
  • Myasthenia gravis: autoimmune destruction of ACh receptors at the neuromuscular junction, causing weakness.
Break the scaffold (dystrophin) and the fiber tears; block the receptor and the signal fails.

Wrap-up

Key takeaways

Review
  • Three muscle types: skeletal (striated, voluntary, multinucleated), cardiac (striated, involuntary, intercalated discs), smooth (unstriated, involuntary).
  • The whole muscle is wrapped by epimysium, fascicles by perimysium, each fiber by endomysium, all converging into the tendon.
  • A fiber is a giant multinucleated cell packed with myofibrils; the sarcomere (Z to Z) is the contractile unit.
  • Thick myosin and thin actin filaments, controlled by troponin and tropomyosin, scaffolded by titin and dystrophin.
  • The T-tubule and triad deliver the signal to the SR's calcium store; contraction is the filaments sliding, not shrinking.
