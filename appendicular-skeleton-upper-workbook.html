Pectoral girdle 3 questions

01 DOK 1 · Recall

What two bones make up the pectoral girdle, and how does it attach to the axial skeleton?

Reveal answer

Clavicle and scapula. The clavicle articulates medially with the manubrium of the sternum at the sternoclavicular joint — the only bony connection between the upper limb and the axial skeleton. The scapula is held to the trunk only by muscles.

02 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the shape and articulations of the clavicle.

Reveal answer

The clavicle is S-shaped. Its sternal (medial) end is rounded and articulates with the manubrium. Its acromial (lateral) end is flattened and articulates with the acromion of the scapula at the acromioclavicular joint. It acts as a strut holding the shoulder out from the trunk.

03 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the key features of the scapula.

Reveal answer

Body (triangular flat blade), spine (the prominent ridge across the posterior surface), acromion (the lateral end of the spine, forms the high point of the shoulder, articulates with the clavicle), coracoid process (an anterior beak-like projection), glenoid cavity (the shallow lateral socket that articulates with the humeral head), supraspinous and infraspinous fossae (depressions above and below the spine, for the rotator cuff muscles of the same names), subscapular fossa (the depression on the anterior surface), superior, medial, and lateral borders, and the inferior angle.

Humerus 3 questions

04 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the proximal features of the humerus.

Reveal answer

Head (the rounded ball that articulates with the glenoid), anatomical neck (just distal to the head), greater tubercle (lateral, attachment for rotator cuff), lesser tubercle (anterior, attachment for subscapularis), intertubercular (bicipital) groove (between the tubercles, holds the long head of the biceps tendon), surgical neck (just distal to the tubercles, a common fracture site).

05 DOK 1 · Recall

Name two notable features of the humeral shaft.

Reveal answer

Deltoid tuberosity (rough area on the lateral shaft, attachment for the deltoid muscle). Radial groove (a spiral groove on the posterior shaft, carries the radial nerve).

06 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the distal features of the humerus.

Reveal answer

Medial and lateral epicondyles (palpable bumps for muscle attachment, with the ulnar nerve grooving around the back of the medial one — the 'funny bone'). Trochlea (medial spool-shaped surface that articulates with the trochlear notch of the ulna). Capitulum (lateral rounded surface that articulates with the head of the radius). Coronoid fossa anteriorly, olecranon fossa posteriorly: depressions that receive the corresponding ulnar processes in flexion and extension.

Forearm bones 4 questions

07 DOK 2 · Compare

In anatomical position, which forearm bone is medial and which is lateral, and which sits on the thumb side?

Reveal answer

In anatomical position (palm forward), the ulna is medial (little-finger side) and the radius is lateral (thumb side). When the forearm pronates (palm back), the radius rotates over the ulna and they cross.

08 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the proximal features of the ulna.

Reveal answer

Olecranon (the elbow tip you lean on), coronoid process (anterior projection just below the trochlear notch), trochlear notch (the C-shaped notch between olecranon and coronoid, articulates with the trochlea of the humerus), radial notch (lateral notch that articulates with the head of the radius).

09 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the proximal features of the radius.

Reveal answer

Head (the disc-shaped proximal end that articulates with the capitulum of the humerus and the radial notch of the ulna), neck (the narrowing just below the head), radial tuberosity (a roughened bump where the biceps brachii tendon attaches).

10 DOK 1 · Recall

What are the major distal features of the ulna and radius?

Reveal answer

Ulna: a small head and a styloid process medially. Radius: a wide articular surface that joins the carpals (mostly via the scaphoid and lunate), a styloid process laterally, and an ulnar notch medially that articulates with the head of the ulna at the distal radioulnar joint.

Hand 3 questions

11 DOK 1 · Recall

How many carpals are there, and what is the standard order of naming them?

Reveal answer

Eight, arranged in two rows of four. Proximal row (lateral to medial in anatomical position): scaphoid, lunate, triquetrum, pisiform. Distal row (lateral to medial): trapezium, trapezoid, capitate, hamate. A common mnemonic: 'So Long To Pinky, Here Comes The Thumb'.

12 DOK 1 · Recall

How are the metacarpals and phalanges numbered, and how many phalanges does each digit have?

Reveal answer

There are five metacarpals, numbered I through V from lateral (thumb side) to medial (little finger). Each metacarpal has a base, shaft, and head; the heads are the knuckles. Each finger has three phalanges (proximal, middle, distal) for a total of three; the thumb has only two (proximal, distal).

13 DOK 3 · Reason

A fall on an outstretched hand commonly fractures the scaphoid. Using carpal anatomy, predict why scaphoid fractures heal poorly.

Reveal answer

The scaphoid receives its main blood supply from a single small artery that enters distally and flows back toward the proximal pole. A fracture across the waist of the scaphoid can cut off blood flow to the proximal fragment, which then heals slowly or develops avascular necrosis. Vascularity is the bottleneck.
