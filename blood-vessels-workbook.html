General vessel anatomy 4 questions

01 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the three tunics of an artery or vein from inside out and describe each.

Reveal answer

Tunica intima: innermost layer; endothelium plus a thin subendothelial connective tissue, sitting on an internal elastic lamina (in arteries). Tunica media: middle layer; smooth muscle and elastic fibers, the thickest layer in most arteries. Tunica externa (adventitia): outer layer of connective tissue that anchors the vessel and contains the vasa vasorum, small vessels that feed large vessel walls.

02 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare arteries and veins by wall thickness, lumen size, and presence of valves.

Reveal answer

Arteries have a thicker tunica media because they carry blood at higher pressure. Their lumen tends to be smaller relative to wall thickness. Veins have thinner walls, a larger relative lumen, and many of them (especially in limbs) have valves that prevent backflow.

03 DOK 1 · Recall

What is a capillary, what is it made of, and what does it do?

Reveal answer

A capillary is the smallest blood vessel, just one endothelial cell thick on a basement membrane, with no tunica media or externa. Capillaries are the site of exchange: oxygen, nutrients, hormones, and waste move between blood and tissue here. Their thinness is what enables exchange by diffusion.

04 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare the three types of capillaries by structure and location.

Reveal answer

Continuous capillaries: tight endothelium with intercellular clefts; most common (skin, muscle, lungs, CNS where they form the blood-brain barrier with no clefts). Fenestrated capillaries: endothelial cells with small pores (fenestrae); found where rapid exchange is needed (kidneys, small intestine, endocrine glands). Sinusoid capillaries: large irregular lumens with wide gaps between endothelial cells; found in liver, spleen, bone marrow, and some endocrine organs, allowing whole cells and large proteins to pass.

Artery types 3 questions

05 DOK 1 · Recall

What are elastic (conducting) arteries, and where are they found?

Reveal answer

Elastic arteries are the largest arteries with abundant elastic fibers in the tunica media. They expand to accommodate the pressure surge during ventricular contraction and recoil during relaxation, smoothing flow. Examples: aorta, brachiocephalic, common carotid, subclavian, common iliac arteries.

06 DOK 1 · Recall

What are muscular (distributing) arteries, and where are they found?

Reveal answer

Muscular arteries are medium-sized arteries with a tunica media dominated by smooth muscle rather than elastic fibers. They distribute blood to specific organs and can change diameter to control flow. Examples: brachial, radial, femoral, renal arteries.

07 DOK 1 · Recall

What are arterioles, and why are they sometimes called resistance vessels?

Reveal answer

Arterioles are the smallest arteries, with a relatively thick layer of smooth muscle for their size. They control how much blood reaches capillary beds by constricting or dilating. Because they are the major site of resistance to flow, they set systemic blood pressure and regulate organ-by-organ perfusion.

Veins 3 questions

08 DOK 1 · Recall

Why do most veins have valves, and where in the body are valves especially important?

Reveal answer

Valves prevent backflow when blood is moving slowly under low pressure, especially against gravity. Valves are most important in the veins of the limbs, where blood must move upward toward the heart. The skeletal muscle pump and the respiratory pump help drive flow forward, and valves keep it from sliding back between pump cycles.

09 DOK 2 · Apply

Explain how the skeletal muscle pump moves venous blood toward the heart.

Reveal answer

Muscles surround deep veins. When the muscles contract, they squeeze the veins. Valves above the squeeze open while valves below stay closed, forcing blood upward toward the heart. When the muscles relax, the squeezed veins refill from below. The action is like milking blood up the limb against gravity.

10 DOK 1 · Recall

What is a venous sinus, and where are sinuses found?

Reveal answer

A venous sinus is a vein with a very thin wall and no smooth muscle, sitting in tissue that provides support instead. The dural venous sinuses of the brain (between layers of dura mater) and the coronary sinus on the heart are examples.

Major systemic arteries 4 questions

11 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the major segments of the aorta in order.

Reveal answer

Ascending aorta (leaving the left ventricle) → aortic arch → descending aorta, which is the thoracic aorta in the chest and the abdominal aorta below the diaphragm. The abdominal aorta ends by splitting into the right and left common iliac arteries at about L4.

12 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the three branches of the aortic arch in order.

Reveal answer

Brachiocephalic trunk (which splits into right common carotid and right subclavian), left common carotid artery, and left subclavian artery.

13 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the major unpaired branches of the abdominal aorta in order superior to inferior.

Reveal answer

Celiac trunk (to liver, stomach, spleen), superior mesenteric artery (to most of the small intestine and proximal large intestine), and inferior mesenteric artery (to distal large intestine).

14 DOK 1 · Recall

What does each common iliac artery split into?

Reveal answer

Internal iliac artery (supplies pelvic organs) and external iliac artery (continues as the femoral artery to the lower limb).

Major systemic veins 3 questions

15 DOK 1 · Recall

What do the superior and inferior venae cavae drain, and where do they empty?

Reveal answer

Superior vena cava drains the head, neck, upper limbs, and upper thorax into the right atrium. Inferior vena cava drains the trunk below the diaphragm and the lower limbs into the right atrium.

16 DOK 2 · Apply

What is the hepatic portal system, and what is its purpose?

Reveal answer

The hepatic portal vein collects venous blood from the digestive tract, spleen, and pancreas and delivers it to the liver, which processes nutrients and detoxifies before that blood enters the general circulation via the hepatic veins. It is a 'portal' system because it has capillary beds at both ends (gut and liver) rather than returning straight to the heart.

17 DOK 1 · Recall

What do the internal and external jugular veins drain?

Reveal answer

Internal jugular vein drains the brain (via the dural venous sinuses), much of the head, and the neck. External jugular vein drains the superficial structures of the face and scalp. Both empty into the subclavian or brachiocephalic veins.

Clinical reasoning 3 questions

18 DOK 3 · Reason

Atherosclerosis is buildup of fatty and fibrous plaques in the tunica intima of arteries. Using what you know about vessel anatomy, predict the structural consequences in the affected artery.

Reveal answer

Plaque thickens the intima, narrows the lumen, and stiffens the wall. Less blood gets through, and the artery's ability to dilate in response to demand is reduced. Plaque can rupture and trigger thrombus formation, suddenly occluding the vessel. The consequence depends on territory: in coronary arteries, MI; in carotid arteries, stroke; in peripheral arteries, claudication and tissue ischemia.

19 DOK 3 · Reason

Varicose veins are dilated, tortuous superficial veins, usually in the legs. Predict the underlying anatomical problem and explain why the result is a dilated vein.

Reveal answer

Venous valves have failed. Without working valves, blood pools in the vein under gravity, raising pressure and stretching the wall. Over time the vein remains stretched, looking dilated and twisted. The pattern explains the location: most common in superficial leg veins where venous return must climb against gravity with help from the muscle pump and valves.

20 DOK 3 · Reason

An aortic aneurysm is a localized dilation of the aorta. Predict what is happening to the wall and why rupture is dangerous.

Reveal answer

The wall has weakened, often through loss of elastic fibers or wall components. As the wall thins and the lumen widens, wall tension increases (Laplace law: tension increases with radius). The wall becomes prone to tearing. Rupture causes massive internal hemorrhage from the largest artery in the body, often fatal within minutes if not immediately controlled.
