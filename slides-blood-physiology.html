Part 1

Transport

How blood moves gases, and the pigment that carries them.

Overview

Functions of blood

Core
Homeostasis

Key points

  • Transport: oxygen and carbon dioxide, nutrients, wastes, hormones.
  • Regulation: body temperature, pH buffering, fluid balance.
  • Protection: clotting stops blood loss; white cells and antibodies fight infection.
Everything blood does serves one goal: keeping the internal environment steady.

Gas transport

Carrying oxygen and carbon dioxide

Core
Respiration

Key points

  • Oxygen: about 98.5% rides bound to hemoglobin (as oxyhemoglobin); 1.5% is dissolved in plasma.
  • Hemoglobin loads O2 where it is high (lungs) and unloads where it is low (tissues).
  • Carbon dioxide: about 70% as bicarbonate in plasma, 20% on the globin chains, 10% dissolved.
Hemoglobin carries almost all the oxygen; most CO2 travels home as bicarbonate.

Clinical

When the carrier fails

Clinical
Abnormal hemoglobin

Key points

  • Carboxyhemoglobin: carbon monoxide binds hemoglobin ~200x tighter than O2, so it blocks oxygen carriage (CO poisoning).
  • Methemoglobin: iron oxidized to Fe3+ can no longer bind oxygen.
  • Both cut delivery even when breathing and lung function look normal.
Oxygen can be normal in the lungs yet never reach the tissue if the carrier is blocked.

Part 2

Making Blood Cells

The signals that control production.

Regulation

Erythropoiesis and EPO

Mechanism
Feedback

Key points

  • Red cells are made in red bone marrow from the hemocytoblast, a ~15 day assembly line.
  • Erythropoietin (EPO), about 90% from the kidney, is released when tissue oxygen falls (hypoxia).
  • EPO drives proerythroblasts to divide and mature; a negative feedback loop shuts it off as oxygen recovers.
  • Raw materials required: iron, vitamin B12, and folate.
The kidney is the oxygen sensor; EPO is the throttle on red-cell production.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Regulation

Iron homeostasis

Mechanism
Recycling

Key points

  • Worn red cells are broken down by macrophages; hemoglobin splits into heme and globin.
  • Iron is salvaged: stored as ferritin, carried by transferrin, reused for new hemoglobin.
  • Leftover heme becomes bilirubin, sent to the liver and excreted in bile.
The body hoards iron and recycles it; only the pigment is thrown away.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Regulation

Leukopoiesis and CSFs

Mechanism
White-cell control

Key points

  • White cells are produced on demand under colony-stimulating factors (CSFs) and interleukins.
  • G-CSF (granulocytes), M-CSF (monocytes), GM-CSF (both), IL-3 (multiple lines).
  • Clinical: G-CSF (filgrastim) rebuilds neutrophils after chemotherapy.
Infection raises CSFs, and CSFs raise the white-cell output to match the threat.

Part 3

Hemostasis

Stopping bleeding, step by step.

Hemostasis

How bleeding stops

Core
Three ideas

Key points

  • 1 Vasoconstriction: the injured vessel squeezes shut to slow flow.
  • 2 Platelet plug: platelets stick to the wound and to each other.
  • 3 Coagulation: a fibrin mesh hardens the plug into a clot.
Narrow the pipe, plug the hole, then cement the plug.

Hemostasis

Coagulation, the cascade

Mechanism
Teach it

A way to picture it

  • Think of it as falling dominoes: each clotting factor is a domino lying flat (inactive). The injury tips the first one, and each fall knocks over the next.
  • Two on-ramps, one highway: the extrinsic pathway (tissue factor, fast) and the intrinsic pathway (contact, slower) both merge into the common pathway.
  • The cascade amplifies: a tiny trigger becomes a big clot in seconds because each step activates many of the next.
  • It always ends the same way: thrombin turns fibrinogen into fibrin. Calcium and vitamin K are required.
One line to leave them with: two roads merge into one, and the destination is always fibrin. Walk the stepper beside it.
  1. Injurythe vessel wall tears and hidden factors are exposed
  2. Two pathways fireextrinsic (tissue factor, fast) and intrinsic (contact, slower)
  3. They meet at Factor Xboth roads converge on the common pathway
  4. Prothrombin → thrombinthe prothrombin activator forms, calcium required
  5. Fibrinogen → fibrinthrombin snips fibrinogen into sticky fibrin threads
  6. Fibrin meshthreads weave a net that traps cells: the clot is set

Hemostasis

Coagulation pathways (reference)

Mechanism
Pathways

Key points

  • Intrinsic: triggered inside the vessel by contact with damage; slower.
  • Extrinsic: triggered by tissue factor from injured tissue; faster.
  • Common: both activate Factor X, which drives prothrombin to thrombin, then fibrinogen to fibrin.
  • Clinical tie-in: PT tests the extrinsic road, PTT the intrinsic road.
PT checks the fast road (extrinsic); PTT checks the long road (intrinsic).

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Hemostasis

Clot retraction and fibrinolysis

Mechanism
Cleanup

Key points

  • Clot retraction: platelet actin and myosin contract, pulling the wound edges together.
  • Fibrinolysis: once healed, plasmin digests the fibrin and dissolves the clot (tPA activates plasmin).
  • Anticlotting factors keep the clot from spreading beyond the injury.
The clot tightens to heal, then plasmin cleans it up when the job is done.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Clinical

Anticoagulant and antiplatelet drugs

Clinical
Pharmacology

Key points

  • Antiplatelets (aspirin) block platelet aggregation.
  • Anticoagulants (heparin, warfarin) slow the cascade; warfarin blocks vitamin K.
  • Used to prevent thrombosis in at-risk patients, balanced against bleeding risk.
Every clot-slowing drug trades clot risk for bleeding risk.

Clinical

Coagulation lab tests

Clinical
Monitoring

Key points

  • PT (prothrombin time): the extrinsic and common pathways; INR standardizes it.
  • PTT: the intrinsic and common pathways.
  • Bleeding time: platelet function.
PT for the extrinsic road, PTT for the intrinsic, bleeding time for platelets.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Clinical

Clotting disorders

Clinical
When it goes wrong

Key points

  • Too little clotting: hemophilia (missing a factor) or thrombocytopenia; bleeding.
  • Too much clotting: thrombosis; a clot that breaks free is an embolus.
  • Read each as a broken step in spasm, plug, or cascade.
Bleeding or clotting: too little or too much of one step in hemostasis.

Part 4

Transfusion

Matching blood, and what happens when it is wrong.

Immunohematology

Transfusion compatibility

Core
Matching

Key points

  • Red cells carry antigens; plasma carries antibodies against the antigens a person lacks.
  • A safe transfusion never gives an antigen the recipient has antibodies against.
  • O negative is the universal donor; AB positive is the universal recipient.
Match the antigen to the antibody, or the immune system attacks the donor cells.

Clinical

Hemolytic transfusion reaction

Clinical
Mismatch

Key points

  • A mismatched unit: recipient antibodies bind donor antigens.
  • Result: agglutination and hemolysis of donor cells.
  • Free hemoglobin can damage the kidneys; cross-matching prevents it.
The wrong unit clumps and bursts the donor cells within minutes.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Clinical

Hemolytic disease of the newborn

Clinical
Rh sensitization

Key points

  • An Rh-negative mother is sensitized by an Rh-positive fetus and makes anti-Rh antibodies.
  • In a later Rh-positive pregnancy those antibodies cross the placenta and destroy fetal red cells.
  • Prevention: anti-D immunoglobulin (RhIg).
The danger is the next Rh-positive pregnancy; RhIg prevents sensitization.

Wrap-up

Key takeaways

Review
  • Blood transports gases: hemoglobin carries almost all the oxygen; most CO2 rides as bicarbonate.
  • The kidney senses low oxygen and releases EPO to make red cells; CSFs control white-cell production; iron is recycled.
  • Hemostasis is spasm, plug, and coagulation: two pathways (intrinsic and extrinsic) merge into a common pathway that makes fibrin.
  • The clot retracts to heal, then plasmin dissolves it; drugs and lab tests target these steps.
  • Transfusion safety is antigen-antibody matching; mismatch causes a transfusion reaction, and Rh mismatch causes HDN.
