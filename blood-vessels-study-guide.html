1. The vessel wall: three tunics

Histology
  1. Name the three tunics of a vessel wall from inside to outside, and the main tissue of each. Recall
  2. What lines the tunica intima, and why does a smooth lining matter? Apply
  3. On a slide, how do you tell an artery from a vein in the same field (wall thickness, lumen shape, lumen contents)? Apply
  4. Which tunic is thickest in a muscular artery, and why does that fit its job? Reason
  5. What are the vasa vasorum and the nervi vasorum, and which vessels need them? Recall
  6. Where do the internal and external elastic membranes sit, and in which vessels are they seen? Apply

2. Arteries: elastic to arteriole

Arteries
  1. Contrast elastic (conducting) arteries, muscular (distributing) arteries, and arterioles by size and wall makeup. Recall
  2. Why do large arteries near the heart need so much elastic tissue? Apply
  3. How do arterioles control blood flow into a capillary bed? Reason

3. Capillaries

Exchange
  1. Name the three capillary types and give an example location of each. Recall
  2. Rank continuous, fenestrated, and sinusoid capillaries by leakiness, and explain why. Apply
  3. Why are sinusoids found in the liver, spleen, and bone marrow? Reason
  4. Define perfusion and microcirculation. Recall
  5. How do continuous capillaries form the blood-brain barrier? Apply
  6. Describe a capillary bed: metarteriole, precapillary sphincters, thoroughfare channel, and the vascular shunt. Apply

4. Veins and venous return

Veins
  1. How does a vein's wall differ from a comparable artery's? Recall
  2. What three mechanisms move blood back to the heart against gravity (valves, muscle pump, respiratory pump)? Apply
  3. Why do veins act as blood reservoirs (capacitance vessels)? Reason
  4. Where is the venous reserve, and how does venoconstriction (driven by the vasomotor center) mobilize it? Apply
  5. What is diapedesis, and at which vessels does it occur? Recall

5. Vessel disorders

Clinical
  1. What is atherosclerosis, and how does it change the vessel wall and lumen? Recall
  2. Define an aneurysm and explain why it is dangerous. Apply
  3. How do faulty valves lead to varicose veins, and why are leg veins most affected? Reason

6. Fetal circulation and shunts

Development
  1. Name the three fetal shunts and the adult remnant of each. Recall
  2. Trace how oxygenated blood from the placenta reaches the fetal heart and bypasses the liver and lungs. Apply
  3. Why must these shunts close at birth, and what happens if one stays open? Reason

7. Region: head, neck, upper limb, thorax

Regional
  1. Name the three branches of the aortic arch and what each supplies. Recall
  2. List the branches of the external carotid artery and name its two terminal branches. Recall
  3. Trace an artery from the subclavian to the deep palmar arch, naming each segment. Apply
  4. Trace a drop of blood from the cephalic vein back to the superior vena cava. Apply
  5. Why does the right common carotid arise from the brachiocephalic trunk while the left comes straight off the arch? Reason

8. Region: abdomen and lower limb

Regional
  1. Name the three unpaired (midline) branches of the abdominal aorta and the gut region each supplies. Recall
  2. List the three branches of the celiac trunk. Recall
  3. Trace an artery from the external iliac to the dorsalis pedis, naming each segment. Apply
  4. Which veins form the hepatic portal vein, and why does gut blood pass through the liver before reaching the IVC? Reason

9. Region: pelvis, renal, reproductive

Regional
  1. Name the two divisions of the internal iliac artery and several organs the anterior division supplies. Recall
  2. Where do the renal and gonadal arteries arise, and what does each supply? Recall
  3. Explain the venous asymmetry: where does the right gonadal vein drain versus the left? Apply
  4. Why might a left-sided varicocele be more common, given that drainage pattern? Reason
