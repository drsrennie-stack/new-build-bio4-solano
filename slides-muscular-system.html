BIO 004 · Human Anatomy · Week 4

The Muscular System

Anatomy, tissue, and microanatomy. How skeletal muscle is built, from the whole muscle down to the sarcomere, and how it attaches and moves bone.

Part 1 · Overview

How skeletal muscle produces movement

Function

Muscle moves the body by pulling, never by pushing.

  • A muscle exerts force on tendons, which pull on bones or other structures.
  • Most muscles cross at least one joint and attach to the articulating bones of that joint.
  • The belly (body) is the fleshy, contractile portion of the muscle.
Four jobs of muscle tissue: produce movement, maintain posture, stabilize joints, and generate heat.

Part 1 · Attachments

Origin and insertion

Anatomy

Two bones, one moves

  • Origin: the tendon attachment to the stationary (or less movable) bone. In a limb, the origin is proximal.
  • Insertion: the tendon attachment to the movable bone, pulled toward the origin; usually distal.
  • Action: the main movement produced by the contraction.
Analogy, a spring on a door: origin on the frame, insertion on the door, action is the door closing.

Part 1 · Levers

Bones are levers, joints are fulcrums

Biomechanics

Two forces on every lever

  • Effort: the force of muscle contraction, applied at the insertion.
  • Load (resistance): the weight of the body part moved plus anything it carries.
  • Movement happens when effort exceeds the load.
  • Mechanical advantage: load close to the fulcrum, small effort moves a heavy load. Farther out, much more effort is needed (chewing with back teeth vs front).
Example, biceps flexion: fulcrum at the elbow, load the forearm plus object, effort the biceps pulling up.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Part 1 · Lever classes

First, second, and third class

Classification

By the order of fulcrum, effort, and load

  • First class: fulcrum between effort and load (seesaw; the head on the vertebral column).
  • Second class: load between fulcrum and effort (wheelbarrow; standing on your toes).
  • Third class: effort between fulcrum and load (forceps; the elbow). Most common in the body.
Third-class levers trade force for speed and range of motion, which is why they dominate the limbs.

Part 1 · Coordination

Agonists and antagonists

Teamwork

Muscles work in opposing pairs

  • Skeletal muscles are arranged in antagonistic pairs across joints (flexor/extensor, abductor/adductor, pronator/supinator).
  • Agonist (prime mover): contracts to cause the action.
  • Antagonist: stretches and yields to the prime mover.
A muscle can be prime mover in one action and antagonist in another; role depends on the movement.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Part 1 · Coordination

Synergists, fixators, and compartments

Teamwork

Helpers and stabilizers

  • Synergists: prevent unwanted movement at intermediate joints or assist the prime mover.
  • Fixators: stabilize the origin so the prime mover works efficiently, steadying the proximal limb while the distal end moves.
  • Compartment: a group of muscles with their shared vessels and nerves and a common function (upper limb flexor/anterior and extensor/posterior compartments).
Naming clue: muscles are named by direction, size, shape, action, number of origins, location, and origin/insertion.

Part 2

Muscle Tissue

Three kinds of muscle: skeletal, cardiac, and smooth. Compare them by striations, control, and location.

Part 2 · Skeletal

Skeletal muscle tissue

Histology

Striated and voluntary

  • Location: skeletal muscle organs covering the bony skeleton.
  • Longest muscle cells; striated (visible striations); voluntary, under conscious control.
  • Cells are long, cylindrical, and multinucleate, with nuclei just under the sarcolemma.
Remember skeletal muscle by two words: striated and voluntary.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Part 2 · Cardiac

Cardiac muscle tissue

Histology

Striated and involuntary

  • Location: the bulk of the heart wall.
  • Striated but involuntary; contracts on its own at a steady rate, with the nervous system adjusting the pace.
  • Cells joined by intercalated discs, which hold two junctions: desmosomes and gap junctions.
Remember cardiac muscle by three words: striated, involuntary, intercalated discs.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Part 2 · Smooth

Smooth muscle tissue

Histology

No striations, involuntary

  • Location: walls of hollow visceral organs (stomach, bladder, airways, blood vessels).
  • Role: force fluids and materials through internal channels.
  • Cells are spindle-shaped with a single flat nucleus; no striations; slow, sustained, involuntary contractions.
Smooth muscle: elongated spindle cells, no stripes, slow and steady.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Part 2 · Properties

Four properties of muscle tissue

Concepts
  • Excitability (responsiveness): receives and responds to a stimulus by changing its membrane potential.
  • Contractility: shortens forcibly when stimulated, the property that sets muscle apart.
  • Extensibility: can stretch beyond its resting length.
  • Elasticity: recoils to its resting length after stretching.

Part 3

Gross Anatomy of a Muscle

The nerve and blood supply and the three connective-tissue sheaths that wrap it, from outside in.

Part 3 · Supply

Nerve and blood supply

Anatomy

One nerve, one artery, one or more veins

  • Each muscle usually gets one nerve, one artery, and one or more veins, entering and branching through the connective-tissue sheath.
  • Every muscle fiber has its own nerve ending controlling it.
  • Rich blood supply for high energy and oxygen demand; capillaries are long and winding so they straighten as the muscle stretches.
The connective-tissue sheaths are the highway that carries these vessels and nerves into the muscle.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Part 3 · Sheaths

Connective-tissue sheaths, outside in

Anatomy

Epimysium, perimysium, endomysium

  • Epimysium: dense irregular CT around the whole muscle; can blend with deep fascia.
  • Perimysium: dense irregular CT around each fascicle (a bundle of fibers).
  • Endomysium: fine areolar CT around each individual muscle fiber.
All three sheaths are continuous with the tendon, so they transmit the pull to the bone.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Part 3 · Attachments

Direct and indirect attachments

Anatomy

How muscle grips bone

  • Direct (fleshy): the epimysium fuses to the periosteum of bone or the perichondrium of cartilage.
  • Indirect: CT wrappings continue as a tendon (rope-like) or aponeurosis (sheet-like) that anchors to bone, cartilage, or fascia.
  • Indirect is most common: tendons are small and durable, conserve space, and pass over joints easily.
When a muscle contracts, the insertion (movable bone) is drawn toward the origin (fixed bone).

Part 4

Skeletal Muscle Microanatomy

Inside one fiber: myofibrils, the sarcomere, the filaments, and the membranes that trigger contraction.

Part 4 · The fiber

Inside one muscle fiber

Cell biology

A very large, specialized cell

  • Long, cylindrical, with multiple nuclei under the sarcolemma; about 10 times the size of a typical cell.
  • Sarcoplasm holds many glycosomes (stored glycogen) for ATP and myoglobin (red pigment) that stores oxygen.
  • Specialized organelles: myofibrils, sarcoplasmic reticulum, and T tubules.
Sarco = flesh: sarcoplasm is the cytoplasm, sarcolemma is the plasma membrane.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Part 4 · Myofibrils

Myofibrils and the striation bands

Histology

Rod-like, densely packed

  • Myofibrils fill about 80% of the fiber volume; they carry the contractile sarcomeres.
  • Striations come from alternating A bands (dark) and I bands (light).
  • Within the A band: the lighter H zone, bisected by the M line (myomesin). The Z disc splits each I band.
Landmarks to memorize: A band, I band, H zone, M line, Z disc.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Part 4 · Sarcomere

The sarcomere

Functional unit

The smallest contractile unit

  • The sarcomere is the functional (contractile) unit of skeletal muscle.
  • It is the segment of a myofibril between two Z discs.
Everything about contraction is measured across one sarcomere, Z disc to Z disc.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Part 4 · Filaments

Thick, thin, and elastic filaments

Molecules

Myofilaments in the sarcomere

  • Thick filaments (myosin): centrally placed, span the A band, joined at the M line.
  • Thin filaments (actin): span the I band and partway into the A band, anchored at the Z disc by alpha-actinin.
  • Elastic filament (titin/desmin): runs from the Z disc and links myofibrils, providing recoil.
Thick = myosin, thin = actin, elastic = titin. Learn which band each occupies.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Part 4 · Myosin & actin

Myosin, actin, and the regulatory proteins

Molecules

The motor and its track

  • Myosin: rod-like tail with two globular heads that form cross-bridges and swivel to generate force; about 300 molecules per thick filament.
  • Actin: thin filament with active sites for the myosin heads.
  • Tropomyosin: blocks the myosin-binding sites on relaxed actin. Troponin (TnI, TnT, TnC): TnC binds calcium and moves tropomyosin off the sites.
Calcium binds troponin, tropomyosin shifts, and myosin heads can grab actin. That is the switch.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Part 4 · Structure

Structural proteins of the myofibril

Molecules
  • Titin: the elastic filament from Z disc to M line; holds thick filaments in place and springs the muscle back after stretch.
  • Dystrophin: links thin filaments to the sarcolemma.
  • Nebulin, myomesin, C proteins: bind filaments and sarcomeres together and keep them aligned.

Part 4 · SR

The sarcoplasmic reticulum

Membranes

The calcium store

  • A specialized smooth ER forming an interconnecting tubular sleeve around each myofibril.
  • Runs longitudinally and ends in terminal cisterns at the A band, I band junction.
  • Function: stores and releases calcium on demand, the "go" signal for contraction.
No calcium release, no contraction. The SR controls when the sarcomere fires.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Part 4 · T tubules

T tubules and the triad

Membranes

Carrying the signal inward

  • T tubules are extensions of the sarcolemma that dive deep into the fiber.
  • A triad is one T tubule flanked by two terminal cisterns of the SR.
  • T-tubule proteins act as voltage sensors; SR proteins are the gated calcium channels.
The triad makes sure every myofibril in the fiber contracts at the same instant.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Part 4 · Sliding filament

What changes when a muscle shortens

Concept

The sliding filament model

  • The I bands shorten.
  • The distance between Z discs shortens.
  • The H zones disappear.
  • The A bands move closer together, but their length does not change.
Filaments slide past each other; they do not shrink. The A band length is your check.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Wrap-up

Key takeaways

Review
  • Muscle pulls on tendons to move bone; origin is fixed, insertion moves toward it.
  • Bones are levers and joints are fulcrums; most body levers are third class.
  • Three tissues: skeletal (striated, voluntary), cardiac (striated, involuntary, intercalated discs), smooth (no striations, involuntary).
  • A fiber is wrapped by epimysium, perimysium, endomysium and packed with myofibrils.
  • The sarcomere (Z to Z) is the contractile unit; myosin and actin slide, triggered by calcium from the SR via the triad.
