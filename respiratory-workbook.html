Overview and divisions 2 questions

01 DOK 1 · Recall

List the major functions of the respiratory system.

Reveal answer

Gas exchange (bringing oxygen in, taking carbon dioxide out), regulation of blood pH (through CO2 levels), voice production (through the larynx), olfaction (through the nasal epithelium), and air conditioning (warming, humidifying, and filtering incoming air).

02 DOK 1 · Recall

Distinguish the upper and lower respiratory tract by structures and the conducting vs respiratory zones.

Reveal answer

Upper respiratory tract: nose, nasal cavity, pharynx, larynx (often included in upper). Lower respiratory tract: trachea, bronchi, bronchioles, alveoli. Functionally: the conducting zone (nose through terminal bronchioles) only moves air; the respiratory zone (respiratory bronchioles, alveolar ducts, alveolar sacs, alveoli) is where gas exchange happens.

The upper airway 3 questions

03 DOK 2 · Apply

Three things happen to inhaled air in the nasal cavity. What are they, and what features make each possible?

Reveal answer

Air is warmed (by close passage over the rich vasculature of the conchae), humidified (by mucus from the epithelial lining), and filtered (large particles trapped by nasal hairs and small particles caught in mucus moved by cilia). The conchae (turbinates) create turbulence that maximizes contact with the lining.

04 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the paranasal sinuses and what they do.

Reveal answer

Frontal, maxillary, ethmoid, and sphenoid sinuses, named for the bones that house them. They lighten the skull, produce mucus that drains into the nasal cavity, and resonate during voice production.

05 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the three regions of the pharynx and what passes through each.

Reveal answer

Nasopharynx (behind the nasal cavity): air only. Oropharynx (behind the mouth): air and food. Laryngopharynx (behind and below the larynx): air and food, and the path divides at the bottom into the larynx (air) and esophagus (food).

The larynx 3 questions

06 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the major cartilages of the larynx and what each does.

Reveal answer

Thyroid cartilage: the large anterior cartilage forming the Adam's apple. Cricoid cartilage: a complete ring just below the thyroid, the only complete tracheal-level ring. Epiglottis: a leaf-shaped elastic cartilage that folds over the laryngeal opening during swallowing to prevent aspiration. Paired arytenoid, corniculate, and cuneiform cartilages support the vocal apparatus.

07 DOK 1 · Recall

What are the vocal cords (true vocal folds), what are they made of, and what do they do?

Reveal answer

The vocal folds are paired bands of mucosa stretched across the laryngeal cavity, containing a band of elastic ligament and underlying muscle. Air forced between them makes them vibrate, producing sound. Their tension and the size of the opening (rima glottidis) are adjusted by laryngeal muscles to change pitch and volume.

08 DOK 2 · Apply

How does the larynx protect the lower airway during swallowing?

Reveal answer

During swallowing the larynx is pulled superiorly and the epiglottis folds down over the laryngeal opening, directing food into the esophagus instead of the trachea. The vocal folds also close, providing a backup seal.

The lower airway 5 questions

09 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the trachea's structure, including the cartilage that supports it.

Reveal answer

The trachea is a flexible tube about 10 to 12 cm long descending from the larynx to T5, where it splits into the two main bronchi. Its wall is supported by 16 to 20 C-shaped hyaline cartilage rings; the open part of each C faces posteriorly toward the esophagus. The trachealis muscle bridges the gap, allowing the esophagus to expand during swallowing.

10 DOK 1 · Recall

What lines the trachea, and what is the mucociliary escalator?

Reveal answer

Pseudostratified ciliated columnar epithelium with goblet cells lines the trachea. Goblet cells secrete mucus that traps inhaled debris. Cilia beat upward in coordinated waves, sweeping the mucus toward the throat where it is swallowed or coughed out. The whole arrangement is called the mucociliary escalator.

11 DOK 1 · Recall

Trace the airway from the trachea to the alveolus, naming each level.

Reveal answer

Trachea → right and left main (primary) bronchi → lobar (secondary) bronchi (3 right, 2 left, one per lobe) → segmental (tertiary) bronchi → smaller bronchi → terminal bronchioles (end of conducting zone) → respiratory bronchioles → alveolar ducts → alveolar sacs → alveoli (respiratory zone).

12 DOK 2 · Apply

Why does an inhaled foreign object tend to lodge in the right main bronchus more often than the left?

Reveal answer

The right main bronchus is wider, shorter, and more vertical than the left, which has to angle around the heart. Inhaled objects fall straight down the trachea and continue down the more vertical right bronchus more easily.

13 DOK 2 · Apply

Bronchioles do not have cartilage in their walls. What do they have instead, and why does it matter?

Reveal answer

They have smooth muscle. The smooth muscle constricts (bronchoconstriction) or relaxes (bronchodilation) in response to autonomic signals and irritants. Without cartilage, the smooth muscle's diameter is what sets airflow resistance; this is why asthma (smooth muscle spasm) and emphysema (loss of elastic recoil supporting bronchioles) cause airflow problems primarily at this level.

Alveoli and gas exchange 4 questions

14 DOK 1 · Recall

What is an alveolus, and what kind of epithelium lines it?

Reveal answer

An alveolus is a tiny air sac at the end of the airway. Its wall is mostly simple squamous epithelium (Type I alveolar cells), giving the thinnest possible barrier for gas diffusion. Each alveolus is wrapped in a dense network of capillaries.

15 DOK 1 · Recall

What are Type I and Type II alveolar cells, and what does each do?

Reveal answer

Type I alveolar cells are squamous and form the gas exchange surface. Type II alveolar cells are cuboidal and produce surfactant, a phospholipid film that reduces surface tension inside the alveolus and prevents it from collapsing.

16 DOK 2 · Apply

What is the respiratory (blood-air) barrier, and what cell layers and structures must oxygen cross to reach the blood?

Reveal answer

The respiratory barrier is the very thin sheet across which oxygen and carbon dioxide diffuse. From the alveolar air to the blood, oxygen crosses: surfactant film, Type I alveolar cell membrane, the fused basement membranes of the alveolus and capillary, and the capillary endothelial cell. Less than a micrometer thick in healthy lungs, which is what permits rapid diffusion.

17 DOK 3 · Reason

Premature infants often lack surfactant. Predict the clinical consequence and explain it using alveolar anatomy.

Reveal answer

Without surfactant, surface tension inside the alveoli pulls them closed at end-expiration; the alveoli collapse and resist reopening. The infant has to do enormous work just to take each breath. The clinical condition is infant respiratory distress syndrome (IRDS), treated with synthetic surfactant and supportive ventilation.

Lungs, pleurae, and breathing 5 questions

18 DOK 1 · Recall

How many lobes does each lung have, and what divides them?

Reveal answer

The right lung has three lobes (superior, middle, inferior) divided by the oblique and horizontal fissures. The left lung has two lobes (superior, inferior) divided by the oblique fissure. The left has less room because the heart pushes into its medial side (the cardiac notch).

19 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the pleurae of the lungs.

Reveal answer

Two layers, like the pericardium. The parietal pleura lines the inside of the chest wall, the upper surface of the diaphragm, and the sides of the mediastinum. The visceral pleura wraps the lung itself. The narrow space between them is the pleural cavity, containing a thin film of serous fluid.

20 DOK 2 · Apply

What happens during quiet inspiration in terms of muscle action and thoracic volume?

Reveal answer

The diaphragm contracts and flattens downward; the external intercostal muscles contract, lifting the ribs and pulling the sternum forward. Thoracic volume increases. Intrapleural pressure becomes more negative, the lungs are stretched outward, alveolar pressure briefly drops below atmospheric pressure, and air flows in.

21 DOK 2 · Apply

How does quiet expiration happen?

Reveal answer

Mostly passively. The diaphragm and external intercostals relax. The elastic recoil of the lungs and chest wall returns them to their resting volume. Thoracic volume decreases, alveolar pressure briefly exceeds atmospheric pressure, and air flows out.

22 DOK 3 · Reason

Air entering the pleural cavity through a chest wound causes the lung on that side to collapse. Using what you know about pleural anatomy, explain why.

Reveal answer

The negative pressure in the pleural cavity is what keeps the lung stretched out against the chest wall. When air enters and the cavity equalizes with atmospheric pressure, that hold is lost. The lung's natural elastic recoil pulls it inward, and the chest wall springs outward; the lung deflates.
