Articulations and Joints

Morning stiffness that lasts two hours, swollen knuckles, and a synovium that has turned on itself.

Patient case

Priya is 41, a software engineer who has had morning stiffness in her hands lasting more than an hour for the past three months. She used to be able to open jars and play piano; now her hands feel "locked" until late morning.

On exam the MCP and PIP joints of both hands are warm, swollen, and tender, and the symptoms are strikingly symmetric. She also notes pain in both wrists and the balls of both feet (MTP joints). She is fatigued. Labs come back with elevated CRP, positive rheumatoid factor and positive anti-CCP antibodies. Hand X-rays show early periarticular erosions.

RA is a disease of the synovial joint. To understand why Priya's small joints are being destroyed while her spine and DIPs are spared, you need to know what a synovial joint actually is, what holds it together, and what makes one go wrong.

1 Classification

Three ways joints can be built

Joints (articulations) are classified by what holds the bones together. There are three options:

Fibrous jointsBones held by dense connective tissue. No joint cavity. Little to no movement. Sutures of the skull, syndesmoses (tibiofibular), gomphoses (teeth in sockets).
Cartilaginous jointsBones held by cartilage. No joint cavity. Slight movement. Synchondroses (hyaline, like the epiphyseal plate) and symphyses (fibrocartilage, like the pubic symphysis and intervertebral discs).
Synovial jointsHave a joint cavity filled with synovial fluid. Free movement. Most joints of the limbs. This is what RA attacks.

Joints can also be classified functionally: synarthroses (no motion), amphiarthroses (slight motion), diarthroses (free motion). Synovial joints are diarthroses; that mobility is exactly what makes them clinically interesting and clinically vulnerable.

2 Synovial structure

Six features that define every synovial joint

Articular (hyaline) cartilageCaps the ends of the bones. Smooth, glassy, low-friction. No nerves, no blood vessels.
Joint (articular) capsuleTwo-layered envelope around the joint. Outer fibrous layer (continuous with periosteum) and inner synovial membrane.
Synovial membraneInner lining of the capsule. Produces synovial fluid. This is the tissue RA attacks.
Joint cavityPotential space between the articular cartilages, filled with synovial fluid.
Synovial fluidViscous, slippery, hyaluronic-acid rich. Lubricates and nourishes the avascular articular cartilage.
Reinforcing ligamentsCapsular thickenings or separate bands that stabilize the joint and limit unwanted motion.
Where RA strikes. Activated immune cells infiltrate the synovial membrane. The membrane proliferates into an inflammatory mass called pannus. Pannus grows over the articular cartilage, releases enzymes that destroy it, and erodes the underlying bone. The classic X-ray finding of periarticular erosions is bone being chewed up by pannus where it spreads over the cartilage margins.
3 Accessory structures

Add-ons that protect or improve specific joints

MenisciFibrocartilage pads (knee, TMJ, AC joint). Improve fit, distribute load, absorb shock.
LabrumFibrocartilage rim that deepens a socket (glenoid labrum, acetabular labrum). Improves stability.
BursaeSmall synovial-fluid-filled sacs where tendons cross bone or ligaments. Reduce friction. Inflammation = bursitis.
Tendon sheathsElongated bursae wrapping tendons in narrow tunnels (wrist, ankle).
Fat padsCushion the joint where there are spaces (e.g., infrapatellar fat pad).
LigamentsBone-to-bone connectors. Inside the capsule (intracapsular, like ACL), outside it (extracapsular, like MCL), or thickenings of it.
4 Movements & six types

What synovial joints can do, by shape

Synovial-joint movements come in three categories: gliding (sliding flat surfaces), angular (flexion, extension, abduction, adduction, circumduction), and rotational (turning around the long axis). The joint's shape determines which motions it allows.

Factors affecting ROM: shape of articulating surfaces, ligament tightness, muscle bulk, age, prior injury, joint disease. RA reduces ROM by destroying cartilage, stretching ligaments, and triggering muscle spasm.

5 Shoulder & knee

The two large joints worth knowing in detail

The shoulder (glenohumeral) joint

Ball-and-socket. The most mobile joint in the body, and the least stable. Shallow glenoid receives the much larger humeral head. Stability comes from the glenoid labrum (deepens the socket), the joint capsule (loose to allow ROM), glenohumeral ligaments (capsular thickenings), and the rotator cuff: four muscles (supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, subscapularis — SITS) that surround the joint and actively hold the head in the glenoid.

The knee joint

The largest synovial joint in the body. Modified hinge: primarily flexion and extension, with small rotation when flexed. Stability from cruciate ligaments (ACL prevents anterior tibial translation, PCL prevents posterior), collateral ligaments (MCL medial, LCL lateral), menisci (medial and lateral), and the quadriceps tendon-patella-patellar tendon mechanism.

6 Common disorders

When joints go wrong

Osteoarthritis (OA)"Wear and tear." Mechanical loss of articular cartilage over years. Affects weight-bearing joints (hips, knees) and DIPs. NOT immune-mediated.
Rheumatoid arthritis (RA)Autoimmune. Synovium attacks the joint. Small symmetric joints. Morning stiffness > 1 hour. Spares DIPs.
GoutMonosodium urate crystals deposit in joints. Acute monoarticular, classically big toe MTP (podagra). Sudden, exquisite pain.
Septic arthritisJoint infection. Single hot swollen joint with fever. Emergency: requires drainage.
BursitisInflamed bursa from overuse or trauma. Olecranon, prepatellar, subacromial. Local swelling, pain on pressure.
SprainStretch or tear of a ligament. Inversion ankle sprain (lateral ligament complex) is the most common.

Returning to Priya

Why these joints, why symmetric, why the DIPs are spared

RA's distribution is anatomical, not random. Once you understand what synovial membrane is and where the largest amount of it lives, the pattern is predictable.

"Morning stiffness > 1 hour"
Inflammatory fluid accumulates in synovial joints overnight when they aren't moving. Movement re-circulates the fluid and disperses inflammatory mediators. RA stiffness lasts > 1 hr; osteoarthritis < 30 min.
"Symmetric distribution"
RA is a systemic autoimmune disease. The same antibodies and immune cells circulate to both sides equally, attacking matched synovial joints.
"MCPs, PIPs, wrists, MTPs"
These are small condyloid and saddle synovial joints with a relatively high ratio of synovial membrane to bone. More target tissue per joint = more inflammation per joint.
"DIPs spared"
DIPs have less synovium and a different structural arrangement. They are classically attacked by OA, not RA. Knowing this pattern distinguishes the two at the bedside.
"Periarticular erosions on X-ray"
Pannus (proliferative inflamed synovium) grows over the cartilage margin and into the underlying bone, eroding both. The erosions appear at the joint edges where the synovium starts.
"Why anti-CCP matters"
Anti-cyclic citrullinated peptide antibodies are highly specific for RA, often positive years before symptoms, and predict more aggressive disease. Combined with RF, very supportive of the diagnosis.

Synovium, cartilage, pannus, erosion. Priya's joints are showing you in clinic what a textbook synovial joint diagram never can. When you go back to the prework, the synovial joint cutaway will land differently — it's where her disease lives.
