General 2 questions

01 DOK 1 · Recall

What is a nerve plexus, and why does the body form them?

Reveal answer

A nerve plexus is a network where adjacent spinal nerves intermingle and exchange fibers before forming the peripheral nerves that travel to specific targets. Plexuses let each peripheral nerve carry fibers from several spinal levels, so injury to a single spinal nerve does not completely paralyze a limb.

02 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the four major spinal nerve plexuses and the spinal levels each comes from.

Reveal answer

Cervical plexus (C1-C4, with some C5), brachial plexus (C5-T1), lumbar plexus (L1-L4), sacral plexus (L4-S4). The thoracic nerves (T1-T11) do not form a plexus; they run individually as intercostal nerves.

Cervical plexus 1 question

03 DOK 1 · Recall

Name two important branches of the cervical plexus.

Reveal answer

Phrenic nerve (C3-C5): the only motor supply to the diaphragm; essential for breathing. Branches to skin of the anterior neck and shoulder.

Brachial plexus 3 questions

04 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the basic organization of the brachial plexus from spinal nerves outward.

Reveal answer

Five roots (C5-T1) → three trunks (upper, middle, lower) → six divisions (anterior and posterior of each trunk) → three cords (lateral, posterior, medial) → five major terminal branches. Mnemonic for the sequence: Roots, Trunks, Divisions, Cords, Branches (Real Texans Drink Cold Beer).

05 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the five terminal branches of the brachial plexus and what each supplies.

Reveal answer

Musculocutaneous: anterior arm muscles (biceps brachii, brachialis), sensation to lateral forearm. Axillary: deltoid and teres minor, sensation over the deltoid. Radial: posterior arm and forearm muscles (triceps, extensors), sensation to posterior arm and hand. Median: most flexor muscles of the forearm, thenar muscles, sensation to lateral palm. Ulnar: medial flexors of the forearm, most hand intrinsic muscles, sensation to medial hand.

06 DOK 3 · Reason

A 'Saturday night palsy' is radial nerve injury from prolonged compression (e.g., sleeping with the arm draped over a chair). Predict the resulting deficit.

Reveal answer

The radial nerve supplies the extensors of the wrist and fingers. Injury causes wrist drop: the patient cannot extend the wrist or fingers, so the hand hangs limp at the wrist. Sensation over the dorsum of the hand may also be reduced.

Lumbar plexus 1 question

07 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the two largest branches of the lumbar plexus and what each supplies.

Reveal answer

Femoral nerve (L2-L4): anterior thigh muscles (quadriceps, sartorius), sensation to anterior thigh and medial leg (via saphenous branch). Obturator nerve (L2-L4): medial thigh muscles (adductors), sensation to medial thigh.

Sacral plexus 3 questions

08 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the sciatic nerve.

Reveal answer

The largest nerve in the body, originating from L4-S3. It exits the pelvis through the greater sciatic foramen, descends through the buttock and posterior thigh, and at about the knee splits into its two terminal branches: the tibial nerve and the common fibular (peroneal) nerve.

09 DOK 1 · Recall

What do the tibial and common fibular nerves supply?

Reveal answer

Tibial: posterior leg muscles (calf), plantar foot muscles, sensation to most of the sole of the foot. Common fibular: anterior and lateral leg muscles (dorsiflexors and evertors of the foot), sensation to anterolateral leg and dorsum of the foot. The common fibular wraps around the head of the fibula, where it is vulnerable to compression.

10 DOK 3 · Reason

Common fibular nerve injury at the fibular head causes foot drop. Predict the gait abnormality and the sensory loss.

Reveal answer

Without dorsiflexors of the foot, the foot hangs limp; the patient cannot lift the toes during the swing phase of walking, and to keep the toes from dragging they lift the affected leg higher than normal (a 'steppage gait'). Sensation is reduced over the anterolateral leg and the dorsum of the foot.
