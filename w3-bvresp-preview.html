core Great vessels bvn-great-vessels 5 recall · 1 mcq · 0 stretch

bvn-great-vessels-r1DOK 1recalllab

What is the aorta?

A. The largest artery, carrying oxygen-rich blood from the left ventricle to the body.

bvn-great-vessels-r2DOK 1recalllab

What does the pulmonary trunk carry, and to where?

A. Oxygen-poor blood from the right ventricle to the lungs, through the right and left pulmonary arteries.

bvn-great-vessels-r3DOK 1recalllab

How many pulmonary veins are there, and what do they return, and to where?

A. Four; they return oxygen-rich blood from the lungs to the left atrium.

bvn-great-vessels-r4DOK 2recalllab

The pulmonary arteries carry oxygen-poor blood and the pulmonary veins carry oxygen-rich blood. Why is this the reverse of the usual rule?

A. Vessel names come from the direction of flow, not oxygen content, and the pulmonary circuit reverses the usual oxygen pattern.

bvn-great-vessels-r5DOK 3recalllab

Trace, in order, the great vessels a drop of blood passes as it goes from the right ventricle to the left atrium.

A. Pulmonary trunk, pulmonary arteries, the lungs, pulmonary veins, then the left atrium.

bvn-great-vessels-q1DOK 1mcq

The pulmonary veins carry blood to which heart chamber?

A. Right atrium

B. Right ventricle

C. Left atrium

D. Left ventricle

The four pulmonary veins return oxygen-rich blood from the lungs to the left atrium. The venae cavae feed the right atrium, and the ventricles are pumping chambers.

core Vessel wall tunics bvn-vessel-tunics 5 recall · 1 mcq · 0 stretch

bvn-vessel-tunics-r1DOK 1recalllab

Name the three tunics of a blood vessel wall, from inner to outer.

A. Tunica intima, tunica media, tunica externa.

bvn-vessel-tunics-r2DOK 1recalllab

What tissue forms the tunica intima?

A. A lining of simple squamous endothelium on a basement membrane.

bvn-vessel-tunics-r3DOK 1recalllab

What does the tunica media contain, and what does it control?

A. Smooth muscle and elastic fibers; its smooth muscle sets the vessel's diameter.

bvn-vessel-tunics-r4DOK 2recalllab

A capillary wall is made of only one tunic. Which one, and why only that layer?

A. The tunica intima only, because exchange needs the thinnest possible wall.

bvn-vessel-tunics-r5DOK 3recalllab

A vessel sample shows a thick middle layer packed with smooth muscle and elastic fibers. Is it more likely an artery or a vein, and why?

A. An artery, because arteries carry high-pressure blood and have a thick tunica media.

bvn-vessel-tunics-q1DOK 1mcq

Which tunic is the only layer a capillary has?

A. Tunica intima

B. Tunica media

C. Tunica externa

D. Tunica adventitia

Capillaries have only the tunica intima, the thin endothelial layer, because exchange needs the thinnest wall. The media and externa are present only in arteries and veins.

core The five vessel types bvn-vessel-types 5 recall · 1 mcq · 0 stretch

bvn-vessel-types-r1DOK 1recalllab

List the five vessel types in the order blood passes through them, artery to vein.

A. Arteries, arterioles, capillaries, venules, veins.

bvn-vessel-types-r2DOK 1recalllab

Which vessels carry blood away from the heart, and which carry it toward the heart?

A. Arteries carry blood away from the heart; veins carry blood toward the heart.

bvn-vessel-types-r3DOK 1recalllab

What is the job of a capillary?

A. It is the exchange vessel, where gases and nutrients cross between blood and tissue.

bvn-vessel-types-r4DOK 2recalllab

Arterioles have walls of mostly smooth muscle. What does that let them do?

A. Adjust how much blood enters a capillary bed.

bvn-vessel-types-r5DOK 3recalllab

Blood is drawn from a vein, not an artery. Give two structural reasons a vein is the safer choice.

A. A vein lies closer to the surface with a thinner wall, and it runs at low pressure, so it is easy to enter and slow to bleed.

bvn-vessel-types-q1DOK 2mcq

Which vessel adjusts how much blood enters a capillary bed?

A. An elastic artery

B. An arteriole

C. A venule

D. A large vein

Arterioles have walls of mostly smooth muscle and act as gatekeepers to the capillary beds. Elastic arteries cushion pressure, and venules and veins return blood.

core Respiratory tracts and zones resp-zones 5 recall · 1 mcq · 0 stretch

resp-zones-r1DOK 1recalllecture

What does the upper respiratory tract include?

A. The nose, nasal cavity, paranasal sinuses, and pharynx.

resp-zones-r2DOK 1recalllecture

What does the lower respiratory tract include?

A. The larynx, trachea, bronchi, and lungs.

resp-zones-r3DOK 1recalllecture

What is the conducting zone?

A. The passages that only carry air, from the nose to the terminal bronchioles.

resp-zones-r4DOK 2recalllecture

What is the respiratory zone, and where does it begin?

A. The structures where gas exchange happens, the respiratory bronchioles, alveolar ducts, and alveoli; it begins just past the terminal bronchioles.

resp-zones-r5DOK 3recalllecture

A structure carries air but has no alveoli in its walls. Is it in the conducting or respiratory zone?

A. The conducting zone, which only carries air and has no alveoli.

resp-zones-q1DOK 2mcq

Which structure is the last part of the conducting zone, just before gas exchange?

A. Terminal bronchiole

B. Respiratory bronchiole

C. Alveolar duct

D. Alveolus

The terminal bronchiole is the last conducting airway. The respiratory bronchiole, alveolar duct, and alveolus belong to the respiratory zone where gases cross.

What is the larynx?

What is the epiglottis, and what does it do?

What is the glottis?

Food 'went down the wrong pipe.' Which laryngeal structure normally prevents this, and how?

A. The epiglottis, which folds down over the laryngeal opening during swallowing.

Which structure folds over the airway during swallowing?

A. Epiglottis

B. Thyroid cartilage

C. Cricoid cartilage

D. Arytenoid cartilage

The epiglottis folds down to keep food out of the airway. The thyroid and cricoid cartilages form the laryngeal framework, and the arytenoids move the vocal cords.

core Trachea and bronchial tree resp-tree 5 recall · 1 mcq · 0 stretch

resp-tree-r1DOK 1recalllab

What holds the trachea permanently open?

A. C-shaped rings of cartilage.

resp-tree-r2DOK 1recalllab

What is the carina?

A. The internal ridge where the trachea forks into the two main bronchi.

resp-tree-r3DOK 1recalllab

Order the bronchial tree from the trachea to the terminal bronchioles.

A. Trachea, main (primary) bronchi, lobar (secondary) bronchi, segmental (tertiary) bronchi, bronchioles, terminal bronchioles.

resp-tree-r4DOK 2recalllab

How do bronchioles differ from bronchi in their walls?

A. Bronchioles have smooth muscle walls and no cartilage.

resp-tree-r5DOK 3recalllab

A tumor blocks a tertiary bronchus. Which unit of the lung loses its air supply?

A. One bronchopulmonary segment, since a segmental (tertiary) bronchus supplies one segment.

resp-tree-q1DOK 1mcq

What keeps the trachea permanently open?

A. Smooth muscle alone

B. C-shaped cartilage rings

C. Bony rings

D. Elastic ligaments

C-shaped cartilage rings hold the trachea open while letting the esophagus expand behind it. Bronchioles, by contrast, rely on smooth muscle and have no cartilage.

core Lungs and pleurae resp-lungs-pleura 5 recall · 0 mcq · 0 stretch

resp-lungs-pleura-r1DOK 1recalllab

How many lobes does the right lung have, and the left?

A. The right lung has three lobes; the left has two.

resp-lungs-pleura-r2DOK 1recalllab

What is the cardiac notch, and which lung has it?

A. An indentation in the left lung that makes room for the heart.

resp-lungs-pleura-r3DOK 1recalllab

What is the hilum of the lung?

A. The indentation on the medial surface where the bronchus and vessels enter and leave.

resp-lungs-pleura-r4DOK 2recalllab

Compare the parietal and visceral pleura.

A. The parietal pleura lines the thoracic wall; the visceral pleura covers the outer surface of the lung.

resp-lungs-pleura-r5DOK 3recalllab

Why does the left lung have two lobes and a cardiac notch while the right has three?

A. The left lung gives up room for the heart, which sits to the left of the midline.

What is a portal system?

A. An arrangement where blood passes through two capillary beds in series before returning to the heart, as in the hepatic portal system.

What is an anastomosis?

A. A place where two vessels join, giving blood an alternate route if one path is blocked.

high Vessel disorders bvn-vessel-disorders 3 recall · 1 mcq · 1 stretch

bvn-vessel-disorders-r1DOK 1recalllecture

What is atherosclerosis?

A. A disease in which fatty plaques (atheromas) build up within the walls of arteries, narrowing the lumen.

bvn-vessel-disorders-r2DOK 2recalllecture

How do arterial disorders differ from venous disorders in what tends to go wrong?

A. Arterial disorders tend to narrow the channel or weaken the wall; venous disorders tend to involve failed valves and pooled or clotted blood.

bvn-vessel-disorders-r3DOK 3recalllecture

Explain anatomically why a deep vein thrombosis in the leg can become a pulmonary embolism.

A. If the clot breaks free it travels up the venae cavae, through the right heart, and lodges in a lung artery, following the normal venous return.

bvn-vessel-disorders-q1DOK 2mcq

A clot from a deep leg vein lodges in a lung artery. What is this called?

A. Aneurysm

B. Pulmonary embolism

C. Atherosclerosis

D. Varicose vein

A pulmonary embolism is a clot, often from a deep vein thrombosis, that lodges in a lung artery. An aneurysm is a wall bulge, atherosclerosis is plaque, and varicose veins are failed venous valves.

bvn-vessel-disorders-s1DOK 3stretch

One patient has crushing chest pain, another has calf pain when walking, a third has sudden one-sided weakness. Name the single artery-wall change behind all three, and the disease it causes in each vessel.

A. Atherosclerosis. In a coronary artery it causes a heart attack, in a leg artery peripheral artery disease, and in a brain artery a stroke.

high Alveolar structure resp-histo 3 recall · 1 mcq · 0 stretch

resp-histo-r1DOK 1recalllab

What are alveoli?

A. The tiny air sacs that are the site of gas exchange.

resp-histo-r2DOK 2recalllab

Compare type I and type II alveolar cells.

A. Type I cells are thin and flat and form most of the wall where gases cross; type II cells secrete surfactant that keeps the alveoli from collapsing.

resp-histo-r3DOK 2recalllab

What is the respiratory membrane?

A. The thin barrier between the alveolar air and the blood, across which gases cross.

resp-histo-q1DOK 2mcq

Which alveolar cell secretes surfactant?

A. Type I alveolar cell

B. Alveolar macrophage

C. Type II alveolar cell

D. Endothelial cell

Type II alveolar cells secrete surfactant, which lowers surface tension so alveoli do not collapse. Type I cells form the thin gas-exchange wall, and macrophages clear debris.
