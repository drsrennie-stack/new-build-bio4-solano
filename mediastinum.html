Overview, what is the mediastinum

The mediastinum is the central compartment of the thoracic cavity, the region between the two pleural sacs that house the lungs. It is not an organ, it is a space, and it carries almost everything in the thorax that is not lung.

  • Mediastinumthe central compartment of the thoracic cavity, flanked on each side by the pleural cavities
  • Positionbetween the two lungs, extending from the thoracic inlet above to the diaphragm below
  • What it containsheart, great vessels, trachea, esophagus, thymus, thoracic duct, nerves of the thorax, lymph nodes, and supporting connective tissue
  • What it does not containthe lungs themselves; the lungs sit in the pleural cavities lateral to the mediastinum
  • Why it mattersa dense, tight space with no room to spare; a mass here compresses neighbors quickly

Surface landmarks you can palpate

Three surface landmarks let you find mediastinal structures from the outside. They are the bridge between the body in front of you and the anatomy on the imaging screen.

  • Jugular notchthe dip at the superior border of the manubrium; marks the level of T2/T3 and the thoracic inlet
  • Sternal angle of Louisthe palpable ridge where the manubrium meets the body of the sternum; lies at the T4/T5 disc; the single most useful surface landmark in the chest
  • Xiphisternal jointthe joint between the body of the sternum and the xiphoid process; lies at about T9 and marks the level of the superior surface of the liver

The sternal angle is where almost everything important happens at once: the trachea bifurcates, the aortic arch starts and ends, the azygos vein arches over the right main bronchus, and the superior and inferior mediastinum meet. Know this level cold.

The six borders

The mediastinum is bounded on all sides. Each border is a real anatomical structure, and each one tells you what a mass here can invade.

  • Superiorsuperior thoracic aperture (thoracic inlet)
  • Inferiordiaphragm
  • Anteriorsternum and costal cartilages
  • Posteriorbodies of the thoracic vertebrae T1 to T12
  • Right lateralright mediastinal pleura
  • Left lateralleft mediastinal pleura

The four compartments

Use the sternal angle plane to divide the mediastinum into a superior compartment and an inferior compartment, then divide the inferior into three. Where a mass lives in this map is the first step in naming it.

The four mediastinal compartments compared
Compartment Boundaries Key contents
Superior thoracic inlet above to the sternal angle (T4/T5 plane) below trachea, esophagus, aortic arch and its three branches, brachiocephalic veins, thymus, thoracic duct, vagus and phrenic nerves, recurrent laryngeal nerves
Anterior (inferior) sternum in front to the pericardium behind thymus remnant, lymph nodes, internal thoracic vessels, fat
Middle (inferior) the pericardial sac and its contents heart, ascending aorta, SVC, pulmonary trunk and pulmonary veins, main bronchi, phrenic nerves on the lateral pericardium
Posterior (inferior) pericardium in front to the vertebral bodies behind esophagus, descending thoracic aorta, azygos and hemiazygos veins, thoracic duct, sympathetic trunks, vagus nerves

How to use this map

When you see a mediastinal mass on imaging, the first question is always: which compartment? Anterior is most often a Thymoma, Teratoma or germ cell tumor, Terrible lymphoma, or Thyroid goiter, the four T's. Middle is most often lymphoma, vascular pathology, or a bronchogenic cyst. Posterior is most often a neurogenic tumor.

Contents, by category

The mediastinum is densely packed. The structures inside it serve the whole body and travel through a region with no elasticity. There is nowhere to go when something expands.

Vascular

  • Ascending aortaarises from the left ventricle behind the pulmonary trunk; gives off the right and left coronary arteries
  • Aortic archarches over the left main bronchus; the left recurrent laryngeal nerve loops under it
  • Aortic arch branchesbrachiocephalic trunk, left common carotid, left subclavian, in that order
  • Descending thoracic aortaruns posterior, to the left of the vertebral column; gives off intercostal, bronchial, and esophageal branches
  • Superior vena cavaformed by the union of the brachiocephalic veins; returns blood from head, neck, upper limbs to the right atrium
  • Brachiocephalic veinsright and left; the left crosses the midline in the superior mediastinum and is the vein commonly compressed in upper SVC syndrome
  • Pulmonary trunk and pulmonary veinspulmonary trunk leaves the right ventricle and divides into right and left pulmonary arteries; four pulmonary veins return oxygenated blood to the left atrium
  • Azygos veinascends on the right side of the vertebral column; arches over the right main bronchus and empties into the SVC; collateral when the IVC is obstructed
  • Hemiazygos and accessory hemiazygosleft-sided counterparts that cross to drain into the azygos

Airway and digestive

  • Tracheaenters the superior mediastinum from the neck; ~10 to 12 cm long; bifurcates at the carina at the level of the sternal angle (T4/T5)
  • Carinathe internal ridge at the tracheal bifurcation; a sensitive cough trigger point
  • Right main bronchuswider, shorter, more vertical than the left; aspirated objects favor it
  • Left main bronchusnarrower, longer, more horizontal; passes under the aortic arch
  • Esophagusdescends behind the trachea, passes posterior to the left atrium, and exits through the diaphragm at T10
  • Esophageal constrictionsthree normal narrowings: at the cricopharyngeus (C6), at the aortic arch and left main bronchus crossing, and at the diaphragmatic hiatus (T10)

Neural

  • Vagus nerves (CN X)enter the thorax beside the carotid sheath; give off recurrent laryngeal branches; continue posteriorly along the esophagus as the esophageal plexus
  • Left recurrent laryngeal nervebranches from the left vagus, loops under the aortic arch lateral to the ligamentum arteriosum, and ascends in the tracheoesophageal groove; vulnerable to any aortic, left atrial, or anterior mediastinal mass
  • Right recurrent laryngeal nerveloops under the right subclavian artery in the neck, not in the thorax; usually spared in mediastinal masses
  • Phrenic nerves (C3 to C5)descend along the lateral pericardium, anterior to the lung root; sole motor supply to the diaphragm; compression causes a paralyzed hemidiaphragm
  • Sympathetic trunksparavertebral chains running along the heads of the ribs in the posterior mediastinum; give off cardiac, pulmonary, and splanchnic branches

Lymphatic and endocrine

  • Thoracic ductthe largest lymphatic vessel; ascends from the cisterna chyli through the aortic hiatus, crosses to the left at about T5, empties at the junction of the left subclavian and left internal jugular veins
  • Mediastinal lymph nodesparatracheal, tracheobronchial, bronchopulmonary (hilar), subcarinal; drain the lungs, heart, and esophagus; central to lung cancer staging
  • Thymusanterior mediastinum; prominent in childhood, fatty remnant in adults; site of T-lymphocyte maturation; thymomas associate with myasthenia gravis
  • Ectopic parathyroidin a minority of patients, an inferior parathyroid gland descends with the thymus into the anterior mediastinum; a missed parathyroid here is a classic surgical pitfall

Cross-section at T4/T5, the high-yield level

Cut the thorax transversely at the sternal angle and you see almost every mediastinal player in one view. Identify each structure from anterior to posterior, then left to right.

  1. Sternum (manubriosternal joint)most anterior; the bone you palpate as the sternal angle
  2. Thymus or thymic remnantjust deep to the sternum in the anterior mediastinum
  3. Ascending aorta and SVCascending aorta near midline, SVC to its right at this level
  4. Trachea bifurcating at the carinainto right and left main bronchi; key landmark for this cut
  5. Azygos veinarching forward over the right main bronchus to enter the SVC
  6. Esophagusjust posterior to the trachea, slightly left of midline
  7. Descending aortato the left of the vertebral body
  8. Thoracic ductcrosses behind the esophagus from right to left around this level
  9. Vertebral body of T4 or T5most posterior; the back wall of the mediastinum

Imaging the mediastinum

Anatomy is one half. Reading it on a study is the other. The mediastinal silhouette on chest X-ray is your first look; CT is your detailed map.

  • Mediastinal silhouettethe central white shadow on a frontal chest X-ray; widening suggests mass, hemorrhage, or dissection
  • Right paratracheal stripea thin vertical line on the right of the trachea on chest X-ray; thickening suggests right paratracheal nodes
  • Aortic knobthe rounded shadow of the aortic arch on the left of the upper mediastinum; abnormal contour suggests aneurysm
  • Mediastinal wideningon a PA chest X-ray, mediastinum > 8 cm at the aortic knob raises concern for aortic dissection or trauma
  • CT with contrastthe workhorse for a mediastinal mass; defines compartment, vascular involvement, lymph node burden, and tumor margins
  • PET-CTadds metabolic data; staging for lymphoma and lung cancer
  • MRIreserved for neurogenic tumors (relationship to spinal cord) and selected vascular questions

Tying it back to the patient

Our patient's CT showed a central thoracic mass. The next imaging questions are: which compartment, what is the vascular and airway involvement, and which lymph node stations are enlarged. The compartment narrows the differential; the involvement determines whether the operating room or the chemotherapy clinic is the next stop.

Clinical correlation, returning to our patient

A mass in the mediastinum, what gets compressed

Our patient has four symptoms. Each maps to a specific structure that travels through the mediastinum. This is anatomy under pressure.

  • Hoarsenessleft recurrent laryngeal nerve compressed where it loops under the aortic arch; vocal cord paresis on the left
  • Dysphagiaesophageal compression in the posterior mediastinum; solids before liquids
  • Shortness of breathtracheal or main-bronchus compression, or phrenic nerve involvement producing a paralyzed hemidiaphragm
  • Chest painmediastinal pleura, pericardium, or direct invasion of adjacent chest-wall structures

Two named syndromes worth knowing. SVC syndrome is compression of the superior vena cava: facial and upper-extremity edema, distended neck veins, dyspnea, often a plethoric face. Horner syndrome (ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis) suggests involvement of the sympathetic chain by an apical or posterior mediastinal mass, classically a Pancoast tumor extending superiorly.

The symptom pattern points to the compartment. Hoarseness plus dysphagia together strongly suggest a superior or posterior mass touching both the recurrent laryngeal nerve and the esophagus. Lymphoma, aortic aneurysm, and esophageal malignancy all belong on this differential.

Differentials by compartment

A mediastinal mass is named by the compartment it lives in. Pair the location with patient age, B symptoms, tumor markers, and imaging features to narrow it.

Common mediastinal masses by compartment
Compartment Most common pathology Helpful clues
Anterior Thymoma, Teratoma and other germ cell tumors, Terrible lymphoma, Thyroid (retrosternal goiter) Thymoma associates with myasthenia gravis; germ cell tumors often elevate AFP or beta-hCG; lymphoma may present with B symptoms; goiter is contiguous with the cervical thyroid on CT
Middle Lymphoma (especially Hodgkin), bronchogenic cyst, lymph node enlargement, vascular pathology (aortic aneurysm) Hilar nodes plus mediastinal nodes suggest lymphoma; smooth fluid-density lesion near carina suggests bronchogenic cyst; pulsatile mass with a high-density rim suggests aneurysm
Posterior Neurogenic tumors (schwannoma, neurofibroma, ganglioneuroma), neuroenteric and esophageal duplication cysts Paravertebral location; possible scalloping of vertebral bodies on imaging; MRI assesses spinal cord involvement
Superior Retrosternal goiter, thymoma, lymphoma, aortic aneurysm The most dangerous compartment because of structure density; consider SVC syndrome and recurrent laryngeal nerve involvement

Self-check, apply the anatomy

Answer in your own words. Then click Reveal to compare.

  • Why hoarseness from a left-sided thoracic mass and not a right-sided oneThe left recurrent laryngeal nerve loops under the aortic arch, deep within the thorax, while the right one loops around the right subclavian artery in the neck. Left-sided masses near the aortic arch reach the nerve; equivalent right-sided masses generally do not.
  • Why a mass in the anterior mediastinum that also causes muscle weakness suggests thymomaThymomas associate with myasthenia gravis through autoantibodies against the acetylcholine receptor. Anterior mediastinal mass plus fatigable weakness is thymoma until proven otherwise.
  • Why an aspirated coin lodges on the right more often than the leftThe right main bronchus is wider, shorter, and more vertical than the left, so gravity and airflow favor the right side.
  • Why widened mediastinum after blunt trauma is taken seriouslyIt raises concern for traumatic aortic injury, classically at the aortic isthmus where the aorta is tethered by the ligamentum arteriosum. This is a surgical emergency.
  • Why elevation of one hemidiaphragm on chest X-ray pairs with shortness of breathThe phrenic nerve, the sole motor supply to the diaphragm, has been compromised. A mediastinal mass along the lateral pericardium can paralyze the hemidiaphragm on that side.
