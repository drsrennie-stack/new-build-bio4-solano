Part 1

What Blood Is

Functions, composition, and the physical properties of the tissue.

Overview

Functions of blood

Core
Homeostasis

Key points

  • Transport: oxygen and carbon dioxide, nutrients, wastes, and hormones.
  • Regulation: body temperature, pH, and fluid balance.
  • Protection: clotting limits blood loss; leukocytes and antibodies fight infection.
Three jobs: transport, regulation, protection.

Composition

Components of blood

Core
Connective tissue

Key points

  • Blood is a fluid connective tissue: cells suspended in a fluid matrix.
  • Formed elements (cells and fragments): about 45%.
  • Plasma (the extracellular matrix): about 55%.
45% cells, 55% plasma: a tissue you can pour.

Properties

General characteristics

Core
Physical

Key points

  • Volume: 7 to 8% of body weight, about 5 L in an adult.
  • Temperature about 38°C, slightly warmer than core; pH 7.35 to 7.45.
  • Viscosity about 5 times water, from proteins and cells.
  • Color: bright red when oxygen-rich, dark red when oxygen-poor.
A warm, slightly alkaline, viscous tissue.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Red cell fraction

Hematocrit

Core
Measurement

Key points

  • Hematocrit: the percentage of whole blood that is erythrocytes.
  • In a spun sample: plasma on top, a thin buffy coat of leukocytes and platelets, packed red cells below.
  • Typical values: men about 42 to 52%, women about 37 to 47%.
Spin the tube and the red-cell column is the hematocrit.

Matrix

Plasma and its proteins

Core
The fluid

Key points

  • Plasma is 91 to 92% water, plus dissolved solutes.
  • Albumin (60% of proteins): carrier, pH buffer, and oncotic pressure that holds fluid in vessels.
  • Globulins (35%): alpha and beta transport proteins; gamma globulins are antibodies.
  • Fibrinogen (4%): converts to fibrin for clotting.
  • Non-protein solutes: nutrients, wastes, electrolytes, and dissolved gases.
Mostly water, but the proteins (albumin, globulins, fibrinogen) do the work.

Part 2

Red Cells

Structure, hemoglobin, turnover, and formation.

Red cells

Erythrocytes

Core
Histology

Key points

  • Biconcave disc, about 7.5 µm; thinner at the center.
  • The shape gives about 30% more surface area than a sphere, plus flexibility for capillaries.
  • Anucleate: no nucleus and no organelles, ejected at maturity.
  • Consequences: cannot divide or make proteins; fixed lifespan about 120 days.
Every feature trades cell machinery for hemoglobin space.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Red cells

Hemoglobin

Mechanism
Mechanism

Key points

  • Quaternary protein: four subunits, two alpha and two beta chains.
  • Four heme groups, each an iron-containing ring; iron is Fe2+ (ferrous), required to bind oxygen.
  • Normal: men 13 to 18 g/dL, women 12 to 16 g/dL.
  • Forms: oxyhemoglobin, deoxyhemoglobin, carboxyhemoglobin (CO, toxic), methemoglobin (Fe3+, cannot carry O2).
  • Gas transport: O2 about 98.5% bound; CO2 about 70% as bicarbonate, 20% on globin, 10% dissolved.
Four hemes, four Fe2+ irons; change the iron or bind CO and transport fails.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Red cells

RBC turnover

Mechanism
Lifecycle

Key points

  • Lifespan about 120 days.
  • Worn cells are removed by macrophages in the spleen, liver, and marrow (phagocytosis).
  • Hemoglobin is split into heme and globin for recycling.
Old red cells are eaten and taken apart for parts.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Red cells

Iron recycling and bilirubin

Mechanism
Mechanism

Key points

  • Globin returns to amino acids.
  • Heme splits into iron and protoporphyrin; iron is stored as ferritin, carried by transferrin, and reused for new hemoglobin.
  • Protoporphyrin becomes biliverdin then bilirubin, carried by albumin to the liver, conjugated, and excreted in bile.
Iron is salvaged; the leftover pigment becomes bilirubin. Excess bilirubin causes jaundice.

Formation

Hematopoiesis and erythropoiesis

Mechanism
Development

Key points

  • All formed elements arise from the hemocytoblast, splitting into myeloid and lymphoid lines.
  • Erythropoiesis: proerythroblast, erythroblast, normoblast (ejects nucleus), reticulocyte, mature red cell, about 15 to 16 days.
  • Reticulocytes are 1 to 2% of red cells; a rise means active production, a fall means marrow suppression.
One stem cell, a 15-day line, with the reticulocyte count as the marrow's report card.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Formation

Control of erythropoiesis

Mechanism
EPO

Key points

  • Erythropoietin (EPO): a hormone, about 90% from the kidney and 10% from the liver.
  • Stimulus: tissue hypoxia (low oxygen).
  • Target: proerythroblasts in the marrow; a negative-feedback loop.
  • More EPO to more red cells to more oxygen to less EPO.
The kidney senses low oxygen and releases EPO to make more red cells.

Part 3

White Cells and Platelets

The five leukocytes, how they move, and platelets.

White cells

Leukocytes and cell lines

Core
Histology

Key points

  • Granulocytes (visible granules): neutrophils (60 to 70%), eosinophils (2 to 4%), basophils (<1%).
  • Agranulocytes: lymphocytes (20 to 30%) and monocytes (2 to 8%).
  • Lymphocytes include B cells, T cells, and NK cells.
  • Neutrophils are the first responders to bacteria; monocytes become macrophages.
Abundance order N, L, M, E, B (Never Let Monkeys Eat Bananas).

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

White cells

How leukocytes move

Mechanism
Mechanism

Key points

  • Diapedesis: squeezing between endothelial cells to leave the vessel.
  • Chemotaxis: directed movement toward chemical signals.
  • Amoeboid movement: crawling with pseudopodia in tissue.
  • Only about 2% of leukocytes are in the blood; about 98% work in tissues.
Blood is the highway, not the destination.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Clinical

Disorders of white blood cells

Clinical
Clinical

Key points

  • Leukopenia: too few white cells (<4,000/µL); weakened defense.
  • Leukocytosis: too many (>11,000/µL); often infection.
  • Leukemia: malignant proliferation of abnormal white cells that crowds the marrow.
Too few, too many, or cancerous: the three ways white-cell counts go wrong.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Platelets

Platelets and thrombopoiesis

Core
Fragments

Key points

  • Platelets are cell fragments shed from megakaryocytes.
  • Size 2 to 4 µm, lifespan 8 to 10 days, count 150,000 to 400,000/µL.
  • Alpha granules hold clotting proteins; dense granules hold ADP, calcium, serotonin.
  • The megakaryocyte becomes polyploid (up to 64N), then its cytoplasm fragments into platelets.
Not cells but packets of clotting machinery, shed from a giant parent cell.

Part 4

Hemostasis

How bleeding stops, in five phases.

Hemostasis

The phases of hemostasis

Mechanism
Overview

Key points

  • Phase I Vasoconstriction: the injured vessel narrows (endothelin, thromboxane).
  • Phase II Platelet plug: platelets adhere to exposed collagen, activate, and aggregate.
  • Phase III Coagulation: pathways converge to turn fibrinogen into a fibrin clot.
Narrow, plug, clot: the first three steps that seal a breach.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Hemostasis

Coagulation pathways

Mechanism
Mechanism

Key points

  • Intrinsic pathway: triggered by contact with damage inside the vessel; slower.
  • Extrinsic pathway: triggered by tissue factor from injured tissue; faster.
  • Both reach a common pathway: prothrombin to thrombin, then fibrinogen to fibrin.
  • Calcium and vitamin K are required cofactors.
Every path ends the same way: thrombin turns fibrinogen into fibrin.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Hemostasis

Clot retraction and fibrinolysis

Mechanism
Mechanism

Key points

  • Phase IV Clot retraction: platelet actin and myosin pull the wound edges together.
  • Phase V Fibrinolysis: plasmin digests the fibrin once healing is done (tPA activates it).
The clot tightens to heal, then plasmin removes it when the job is done.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Clinical

Coagulation lab tests

Clinical
Clinical

Key points

  • PT (prothrombin time): tests the extrinsic pathway.
  • PTT (partial thromboplastin time): tests the intrinsic pathway.
  • Bleeding time: tests platelet function.
  • INR standardizes PT for monitoring anticoagulants.
PT for extrinsic, PTT for intrinsic, bleeding time for platelets.

Part 5

Blood Groups and Clinical

Typing, compatibility, and the disorders.

Blood groups

Human blood groups

Core
Antigens

Key points

  • Blood type is set by antigens (agglutinogens), glycoproteins on the red-cell membrane.
  • ABO: A (N-acetylgalactosamine), B (galactose), AB (both), O (neither).
  • Antigens are the markers the immune system reads as self or foreign.
Antigens on the cell surface name the blood type.

Blood groups

Antigens and antibodies

Core
Terminology

Key points

  • Antibodies (agglutinins) in plasma target the antigens a person lacks.
  • Type A has anti-B; type B has anti-A; type O has both; type AB has neither.
  • ABO antibodies are absent at birth and develop over the first months of life.
  • A mismatch causes agglutination, the clumping behind a transfusion reaction.
Antigens on the cell, antibodies in the plasma; a mismatch clumps the cells.

Blood groups

ABO and Rh interpretation

Core
Typing

Key points

  • Typing mixes blood with anti-A, anti-B, and anti-D sera and reads which clump.
  • Rh: Rh-positive carries the D antigen (about 85%); Rh-negative lacks it.
  • Rh antibodies are not naturally present; they form only after sensitization.
Clumping with a known antiserum tells you which antigen is present.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Blood groups

Universal donor and recipient

Core
Compatibility

Key points

  • Universal donor: O negative. No A, B, or Rh antigens, so nothing for a recipient's antibodies to attack.
  • Universal recipient: AB positive. No anti-A or anti-B antibodies, so it accepts any ABO type.
  • Compatibility rule: never give antigens the recipient has antibodies against.
O negative gives to anyone; AB positive receives from anyone.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Clinical

Hemolytic disease of the newborn

Clinical
Rh incompatibility

Key points

  • An Rh-negative mother carries an Rh-positive fetus.
  • At delivery, fetal red cells cross the placenta and the mother makes anti-Rh antibodies (sensitization).
  • In a later Rh-positive pregnancy, those antibodies cross the placenta and destroy fetal red cells.
  • Prevention: anti-D immunoglobulin (RhIg / RhoGAM) given to the mother.
The risk is to the next Rh-positive pregnancy; RhIg prevents sensitization.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Clinical

Hemolytic transfusion reaction

Clinical
Clinical

Key points

  • A mismatched transfusion: the recipient's antibodies bind donor antigens.
  • Result: agglutination and hemolysis of the donor cells.
  • Consequences include blocked vessels and kidney damage from released hemoglobin.
  • Prevented by cross-matching before transfusion.
Mismatched antigens and antibodies clump and burst the donor cells; cross-match first.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Clinical

Anemia

Clinical
Red-cell disorders

Key points

  • Anemia: reduced oxygen-carrying capacity; fatigue and pallor.
  • By size: microcytic (iron deficiency), normocytic (chronic disease, blood loss), macrocytic (B12 or folate).
  • By cause: blood loss, decreased production, or increased destruction.
Read anemia two ways: what the cells look like, and why they are low.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Clinical

Sickle cell and thalassemia

Clinical
Hemoglobin disorders

Key points

  • Sickle cell: a single amino-acid change (Glu to Val) in beta-globin; HbS polymerizes when deoxygenated, sickling the cell (lifespan 10 to 20 days).
  • Thalassemia: reduced alpha or beta globin synthesis; imbalanced chains and ineffective erythropoiesis.
  • Both are genetic and both distort or destroy red cells.
Change the hemoglobin gene, change the cell, change the outcome.

Dr. Rennie, lecture figure

Clinical

Erythrocytosis

Clinical
Red-cell disorders

Key points

  • Erythrocytosis / polycythemia: too many red cells.
  • Raises blood viscosity, slowing flow and straining the heart.
  • Polycythemia vera is the primary marrow form.
Too many red cells thicken the blood and overwork the heart.

Wrap-up

Key takeaways

Review
  • Blood is a fluid connective tissue, 45% formed elements and 55% plasma; warm, slightly alkaline, viscous.
  • Red cells are biconcave and anucleate, packed with hemoglobin (2 alpha, 2 beta, four Fe2+ hemes); worn cells recycle iron and make bilirubin.
  • Formation runs from the hemocytoblast; EPO from the kidney drives red cells.
  • The five leukocytes defend, platelets clot, and hemostasis is spasm, plug, clot, retraction, fibrinolysis.
  • Blood groups are set by ABO and Rh antigens; Rh mismatch causes hemolytic disease of the newborn, prevented by RhIg.
