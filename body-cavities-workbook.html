Directional terms and body planes 9 questions

01 DOK 1 · Recall

Define anatomical position. Why does every directional term assume it?

Reveal answer

Anatomical position is standing upright, eyes forward, feet flat and slightly apart, arms at the sides with palms facing forward and thumbs pointing laterally. Every directional term is defined relative to this position so that the words mean the same thing no matter how the body happens to be lying or moving.

02 DOK 1 · Recall

Compare anterior and posterior, and give the synonyms used for each in human anatomy.

Reveal answer

Anterior means toward the front of the body; ventral is the synonym. Posterior means toward the back of the body; dorsal is the synonym. In quadrupeds (animals on four legs) ventral and dorsal are the preferred terms; in humans, anterior and posterior are more common.

03 DOK 1 · Recall

Compare medial and lateral. Give one example pair that uses each.

Reveal answer

Medial means toward the midline of the body. Lateral means away from the midline. The sternum is medial to the nipple. The pinky finger is medial to the thumb (when in anatomical position with palms forward).

04 DOK 1 · Recall

Compare superior and inferior. What synonym is sometimes used in place of each?

Reveal answer

Superior means toward the head; cranial or cephalic are synonyms. Inferior means toward the feet; caudal is the synonym. The heart is superior to the diaphragm; the bladder is inferior to the stomach.

05 DOK 2 · Apply

Proximal and distal apply primarily to the limbs. Define each, and explain why they are different from superior and inferior.

Reveal answer

Proximal means closer to the trunk or to the point of attachment of a limb. Distal means farther from the trunk or point of attachment. They are limb-relative, not body-relative, so they remain correct even when the limb is moved. The wrist is distal to the elbow whether the arm is at the side or raised overhead; the same is not true of superior and inferior.

06 DOK 1 · Recall

Compare superficial and deep. Give one example pair.

Reveal answer

Superficial means closer to the body surface. Deep means farther from the surface, toward the interior. The skin is superficial to the muscle beneath it; the femur is deep to the quadriceps.

07 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the midline of the body, and what is meant by ipsilateral versus contralateral?

Reveal answer

The midline is the vertical line that divides the body into right and left halves. Ipsilateral means on the same side as a reference structure; contralateral means on the opposite side. The right kidney is ipsilateral to the right ureter; the left kidney is contralateral to the right ureter.

08 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the three primary anatomical planes and describe how each one cuts the body.

Reveal answer

The sagittal plane cuts vertically into right and left portions (midsagittal at the midline, parasagittal off the midline). The frontal (coronal) plane cuts vertically into anterior and posterior portions. The transverse (horizontal) plane cuts horizontally into superior and inferior portions. An oblique plane cuts at any angle other than these three.

09 DOK 1 · Recall

Distinguish midsagittal from parasagittal sections.

Reveal answer

Midsagittal (median) section is right on the midline and divides the body into equal right and left halves. Parasagittal section is parallel to the midline but offset, so it divides the body into unequal right and left portions.

The body cavities 8 questions

10 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the two main body cavities and the subdivisions of each.

Reveal answer

The dorsal (posterior) body cavity contains the cranial cavity and the vertebral (spinal) cavity. The ventral (anterior) body cavity contains the thoracic cavity above the diaphragm and the abdominopelvic cavity below it.

11 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the cranial cavity, what surrounds it, and what does it contain?

Reveal answer

The cranial cavity is the space inside the skull. Its walls are formed by the cranial bones. It contains the brain and its membranes (the meninges) and the surrounding cerebrospinal fluid.

12 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the vertebral cavity, what surrounds it, and what does it contain?

Reveal answer

The vertebral cavity is the canal that runs through the vertebral column. Its walls are formed by the stacked vertebrae and their ligaments. It contains the spinal cord, its meninges, and surrounding cerebrospinal fluid.

13 DOK 2 · Apply

Are the cranial and vertebral cavities continuous? How would you explain that connection structurally?

Reveal answer

Yes. The cranial cavity opens to the vertebral cavity through the foramen magnum at the base of the skull. The brain and spinal cord, their meninges, and cerebrospinal fluid are continuous between the two spaces. Structurally and functionally the dorsal cavity is one continuous compartment.

14 DOK 1 · Recall

What does the thoracic cavity contain, and how is it subdivided?

Reveal answer

The thoracic cavity contains the heart, the lungs, the great vessels, the trachea and esophagus, and the thymus. It is subdivided into two lateral pleural cavities (each containing one lung) and a central mediastinum (containing the heart and the other midline structures).

15 DOK 1 · Recall

What does the abdominopelvic cavity contain, and how is it subdivided?

Reveal answer

The abdominopelvic cavity contains most of the digestive organs, the spleen, the kidneys and ureters, the urinary bladder and reproductive organs, and the abdominal great vessels. It is divided by the pelvic brim into the abdominal cavity (superior, holding the stomach, liver, intestines, and most of the urinary system) and the pelvic cavity (inferior, holding the bladder, rectum, and reproductive organs).

16 DOK 1 · Recall

What separates the thoracic cavity from the abdominopelvic cavity, and what kind of structure is it?

Reveal answer

The diaphragm, a dome-shaped skeletal muscle. It is the boundary between the thoracic cavity above and the abdominopelvic cavity below. Its contraction during breathing flattens the dome and increases thoracic volume.

17 DOK 1 · Recall

Name three smaller body cavities outside the dorsal and ventral cavities.

Reveal answer

The oral cavity (mouth), the nasal cavity (interior of the nose), the orbital cavities (eye sockets), the middle ear cavities, and the synovial cavities (inside freely movable joints). These open to the outside (oral, nasal) or sit inside specific structures (joints, middle ear).

Serous membranes 5 questions

18 DOK 1 · Recall

What is a serous membrane, what is it made of, and what does it do?

Reveal answer

A serous membrane is a thin double-layered membrane that lines a closed body cavity and covers the organs in that cavity. It is built from a simple squamous epithelium (mesothelium) over a thin connective tissue layer. It secretes serous fluid into the space between its two layers, which lubricates the surfaces so the organ and the cavity wall slide against each other with little friction.

19 DOK 1 · Recall

Compare the parietal and visceral layers of a serous membrane.

Reveal answer

The parietal layer lines the wall of the body cavity. The visceral layer covers the organ inside the cavity. The two layers are continuous with each other where the organ meets the wall; the narrow space between them holds serous fluid.

20 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the three pairs of serous membranes in the body and where each pair sits.

Reveal answer

The pleurae line the pleural cavities and cover the lungs. The pericardium lines the pericardial cavity and covers the heart. The peritoneum lines the abdominopelvic cavity and covers most of the abdominopelvic organs.

21 DOK 2 · Apply

Why does a serous cavity need fluid between the two layers, and what happens if too much accumulates?

Reveal answer

Serous fluid lubricates the layers so the organ can slide against the cavity wall during normal movement (breathing, heartbeat, peristalsis) with minimal friction. If too much fluid accumulates (effusion), the organ is compressed: pleural effusion can collapse parts of a lung; pericardial effusion can prevent the heart from filling properly (cardiac tamponade); peritoneal fluid accumulation (ascites) distends the abdomen.

22 DOK 2 · Apply

Trace the pleura from the lung surface outward and name each part.

Reveal answer

Starting at the lung surface: visceral pleura wraps the lung itself. The pleural cavity is the narrow space outside the visceral pleura that contains serous fluid. The parietal pleura lines the inside of the chest wall, the upper surface of the diaphragm, and the sides of the mediastinum. Visceral and parietal pleurae are continuous with each other at the hilum where vessels and bronchi enter the lung.

The mediastinum 3 questions

23 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the mediastinum, what are its boundaries, and what does it contain?

Reveal answer

The mediastinum is the central compartment of the thoracic cavity, between the two pleural cavities. Its boundaries are the sternum anteriorly, the vertebral column posteriorly, the two pleural cavities laterally, the thoracic inlet superiorly, and the diaphragm inferiorly. It contains the heart and its pericardium, the great vessels, the trachea, the esophagus, the thymus, and various nerves.

24 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the compartments of the mediastinum and what each contains.

Reveal answer

Superior mediastinum (above the level of the sternal angle): great vessels, trachea, esophagus, thymus, thoracic duct. Inferior mediastinum is divided into three compartments. Anterior mediastinum (in front of the pericardium): thymus remnants, lymph nodes, connective tissue. Middle mediastinum: the heart and its pericardium, the roots of the great vessels, the main bronchi. Posterior mediastinum (behind the pericardium): esophagus, descending thoracic aorta, thoracic duct, sympathetic trunks, and azygos system of veins.

25 DOK 2 · Apply

Why is the mediastinum a high-stakes region for a chest wound or trauma?

Reveal answer

It contains the heart, the great vessels, the trachea, the esophagus, and major nerves all packed together in a small space. A penetrating injury can damage any combination of those structures at once, and the cardiovascular and respiratory consequences can be immediate and severe.

The peritoneum 6 questions

26 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the peritoneum, and how do its two layers relate to abdominal organs?

Reveal answer

The peritoneum is the serous membrane of the abdominopelvic cavity. Its parietal layer lines the abdominal wall and the inferior surface of the diaphragm. Its visceral layer covers most of the abdominal organs. The peritoneal cavity is the narrow space between the layers and holds serous (peritoneal) fluid.

27 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare intraperitoneal and retroperitoneal organs by their relationship to the peritoneum.

Reveal answer

Intraperitoneal organs are surrounded by visceral peritoneum and suspended within the peritoneal cavity by a mesentery (a double layer of peritoneum that carries vessels and nerves). Retroperitoneal organs sit behind the peritoneum, against the posterior abdominal wall, with peritoneum covering only their anterior surface.

28 DOK 1 · Recall

What is a mesentery, what does it do, and give two examples.

Reveal answer

A mesentery is a double layer of peritoneum that suspends an intraperitoneal organ from the body wall. It anchors the organ in place and carries blood vessels, lymphatics, and nerves to and from the organ. Examples: the mesentery proper supports the small intestine, the mesocolon supports portions of the large intestine.

29 DOK 1 · Recall

What does the SADPUCKER mnemonic stand for? List the structures it names.

Reveal answer

SADPUCKER lists retroperitoneal structures: Suprarenal (adrenal) glands, Aorta and inferior vena cava, Duodenum (most of it, except the first part), Pancreas (except the tail), Ureters, Colon (ascending and descending), Kidneys, Esophagus (lower thoracic to abdominal portion), Rectum (lower portion).

30 DOK 1 · Recall

Name several intraperitoneal organs.

Reveal answer

Stomach, most of the duodenum's first part, jejunum, ileum, cecum and appendix, transverse colon, sigmoid colon, liver, gallbladder, spleen, and the tail of the pancreas. The female internal reproductive organs (uterus, uterine tubes, ovaries) are partly intraperitoneal.

31 DOK 3 · Reason

Peritonitis is inflammation of the peritoneum, often from a ruptured abdominal organ. Using what you know about the peritoneal cavity, predict why peritonitis can become a body-wide emergency so quickly.

Reveal answer

The peritoneal cavity is one continuous space lined by a thin serous membrane with a huge surface area. Bacteria or chemicals from a ruptured organ spread freely through that space, contact a vast absorptive surface, and can enter the bloodstream rapidly. The result is widespread infection or shock within hours. The continuity that lets the abdomen flex and breathe is the same continuity that lets contamination spread fast.

Abdominopelvic regions (nine) 5 questions

32 DOK 1 · Recall

Why divide the abdomen into nine regions, and what kind of grid produces them?

Reveal answer

The nine-region grid gives anatomists and clinicians a precise way to localize pain, masses, organs, and surgical sites. The grid is built from four planes: two horizontal (the subcostal plane just below the ribs and the transtubercular plane across the iliac tubercles) and two vertical (the midclavicular planes running down from the midpoints of the clavicles).

33 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the nine abdominopelvic regions in order from upper right across, middle row, and lower row.

Reveal answer

Top row (right to left): right hypochondriac, epigastric, left hypochondriac. Middle row: right lumbar (right lateral), umbilical, left lumbar (left lateral). Bottom row: right iliac (right inguinal), hypogastric (pubic), left iliac (left inguinal).

34 DOK 2 · Apply

Name one major organ found in each of the three top-row regions: right hypochondriac, epigastric, left hypochondriac.

Reveal answer

Right hypochondriac: liver (right lobe) and gallbladder. Epigastric: stomach and proximal duodenum, pancreas. Left hypochondriac: spleen and stomach (fundus and body).

35 DOK 2 · Apply

Name one major organ found in each middle-row region: right lumbar, umbilical, left lumbar.

Reveal answer

Right lumbar: ascending colon and right kidney. Umbilical: small intestine (mostly jejunum and ileum), transverse colon, parts of the pancreas. Left lumbar: descending colon and left kidney.

36 DOK 2 · Apply

Name one major organ found in each bottom-row region: right iliac, hypogastric, left iliac.

Reveal answer

Right iliac: cecum and appendix. Hypogastric: urinary bladder (when full), uterus (in females), sigmoid colon. Left iliac: sigmoid colon.

Abdominopelvic quadrants (four) 7 questions

37 DOK 1 · Recall

What two planes form the four abdominopelvic quadrants, and where do they cross?

Reveal answer

One transverse plane through the umbilicus and one vertical plane through the midline. They cross at the umbilicus.

38 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the four abdominal quadrants.

Reveal answer

Right upper quadrant (RUQ), left upper quadrant (LUQ), right lower quadrant (RLQ), left lower quadrant (LLQ). Right and left are the patient's right and left, not the examiner's.

39 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the major organs found in the right upper quadrant.

Reveal answer

Most of the liver and the gallbladder, the right kidney (in the back), the duodenum, the head of the pancreas, parts of the ascending and transverse colon. Pain in the RUQ classically suggests gallbladder or liver disease.

40 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the major organs found in the left upper quadrant.

Reveal answer

Stomach, spleen, left lobe of the liver, body and tail of the pancreas, left kidney (in the back), parts of the transverse and descending colon.

41 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the major organs found in the right lower quadrant.

Reveal answer

Cecum, appendix, ascending colon (lower portion), right ovary and uterine tube (in females), right ureter. Appendicitis classically produces pain that localizes to the RLQ.

42 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the major organs found in the left lower quadrant.

Reveal answer

Sigmoid colon, descending colon (lower portion), left ovary and uterine tube (in females), left ureter.

43 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare the quadrant system and the nine-region system. When is each one used?

Reveal answer

Quadrants are simpler (four areas) and are favored for quick clinical localization (where is the pain?). Nine regions are more precise (smaller areas, more specific organ overlap) and are favored for detailed anatomy teaching and finer clinical description.

Clinical reasoning 4 questions

44 DOK 3 · Reason

A patient describes sharp pain in the right upper quadrant after a fatty meal. Using regional anatomy, list two organs you would consider first and explain why each fits the pain pattern.

Reveal answer

The gallbladder is the strongest candidate. It sits in the RUQ and releases bile in response to fatty meals; a stone blocking its outflow produces sharp pain that classically follows fat ingestion. The liver is also in the RUQ and can cause RUQ pain when inflamed or distended, though the fatty-meal trigger is less characteristic.

45 DOK 3 · Reason

A patient develops abdominal pain that starts around the umbilicus and over the next few hours migrates to the right lower quadrant. What is the most likely cause, and which abdominal region is involved?

Reveal answer

Acute appendicitis is the classic explanation. Early visceral pain from the appendix is poorly localized and felt near the umbilicus (umbilical region); as the inflammation progresses to involve the parietal peritoneum overlying the appendix, the pain sharpens and localizes to the RLQ (right iliac region), where the appendix actually sits.

46 DOK 3 · Reason

A patient who fell on the left side of the abdomen develops left upper quadrant pain that also radiates to the left shoulder. Predict which organ has likely been injured and why the pain refers where it does.

Reveal answer

The spleen, which sits in the LUQ, is the likely injury. The diaphragm overlies the spleen and shares innervation with the shoulder (the phrenic nerve, C3-C5). Blood under the diaphragm irritates these nerve fibers, and the brain refers the pain to the dermatomes those nerves also serve, which include the shoulder. This is called Kehr's sign.

47 DOK 3 · Reason

A patient is stabbed in the chest. Air enters the pleural cavity. Predict what happens to the lung on that side and why.

Reveal answer

The lung on the injured side collapses (pneumothorax). The pleural cavity normally holds a tiny film of serous fluid at a slight negative pressure, which keeps the visceral pleura (and the lung) pulled tight against the parietal pleura. When air enters the cavity, that negative pressure is lost, the lung's natural elastic recoil pulls it inward, and it deflates.
