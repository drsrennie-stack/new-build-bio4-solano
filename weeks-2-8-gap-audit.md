# Weeks 2 to 8 Notes-to-Bank Gap Audit

Date: June 19, 2026. Source of truth: `BIO004-Week2-Notes.pdf` through `BIO004-Week8-Notes.pdf`. Checked against the full spaced-recall bank (`course-content.js` + `dok3-explain-why.js`, 1,410 cards) plus the Week 1 gap cards.

## Method

Same as Week 1: pull every defined term from each week's notes tables, search the entire bank for each, classify as tested in that week's topics, tested only elsewhere, or absent everywhere, then manually confirm the absences (checking wording variants so a concept taught with different phrasing is not falsely flagged). A concept counts as "delivered" if any activity reaches it. Per your call, surface and identification terms (bone landmarks, named ligaments, individual brain structures) are assigned to Match-up and the lab sprints, not cards.

## Headline

Coverage is excellent across most of the term. **Weeks 3, 4, 6, and 8 have no card-worthy gaps.** Week 5's only flag is a clinical aside. The real gaps concentrate in two weeks, and one of them matters a lot.

## Card-worthy gaps to close

### Week 2, joint movements (the most important gap in the whole course)

The Articulations topic tests flexion, extension, abduction, rotation, and depression. It is **missing twelve standard movements** your notes teach: adduction, circumduction, pronation, supination, dorsiflexion, plantar flexion, inversion, eversion, elevation, protraction, retraction, and opposition. These are practical-exam staples and the clearest "study the bank and still get caught" risk in the course. This is the one I would close first. It also pairs naturally with Match-up (movement to definition) and labeling.

### Week 2, bone microanatomy

Bone histology covers osteons and concentric lamellae but not **circumferential lamellae** or **interstitial lamellae**, both defined in your notes. Two or three cards.

### Week 2, ligament classification

**Extracapsular versus intracapsular ligaments** is taught as a classification but not tested. One or two cards. (The individually named ligaments, glenohumeral, coracohumeral, patellar retinacula, are identification items for the lab sprints, not cards.)

### Week 7, basal nuclei

The Brain topic covers the primary motor cortex, Broca, and Wernicke areas, but the **basal nuclei (putamen, globus pallidus) and their role in movement** are absent. This is a major brain concept, card-worthy and also lab material. Three or four cards.

### Week 7, functional cortical areas

Missing from the Brain topic: **premotor cortex, supplementary motor area, prefrontal cortex, and the primary gustatory cortex.** The primary motor and language areas are present, so this rounds out the functional map. Four or five cards.

## Deliver through lab and Match-up, not cards

These are taught in the notes as structures to identify, so they belong to the lab sprints and a future Match-up, where you said identification material is better placed: skull landmarks (external and internal acoustic meatus, parietal eminence), the interosseous membrane, the named shoulder and knee ligaments, the massa intermedia, and the olives of the medulla.

## Optional clinical asides

Mentioned in the notes as context, not core testable facts: rheumatism (Week 2) and colorectal cancer (Week 5). Add cards only if you test clinical asides.

## Tally and recommendation

Card-worthy gaps across Weeks 2 to 8 close with roughly 25 to 35 new cards, the bulk of them the Week 2 movements. Combined with the 8 Week 1 cards already drafted, the bank would then be complete against your notes for everything cards are the right vehicle for, with identification terms routed to lab and Match-up.

## Limitation

This audit finds concepts named in the notes but not tested. It does not yet judge depth of testing for concepts that are present. That is a separate pass if you want it.
