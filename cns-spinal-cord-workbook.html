Gross anatomy 3 questions

01 DOK 1 · Recall

Where does the spinal cord begin and end in the adult, and why does it end where it does?

Reveal answer

It begins at the foramen magnum, continuous with the medulla. It ends as the conus medullaris at about L1-L2 in the adult. It ends higher than the vertebral column because the cord grows more slowly than the vertebral column during late development, so the cord 'rises' relative to the bones. Below the conus, only nerve roots continue.

02 DOK 1 · Recall

What are the cervical and lumbar enlargements, and why are they there?

Reveal answer

Cervical enlargement (C4-T1): wider region of cord where the nerves to the upper limbs originate. Lumbar (lumbosacral) enlargement (T11-S1): wider region where the nerves to the lower limbs originate. More neurons are packed in to serve the limb territories.

03 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the cauda equina, and what is the filum terminale?

Reveal answer

The cauda equina ('horse's tail') is the bundle of lumbar, sacral, and coccygeal nerve roots that descend below the conus medullaris within the vertebral canal before exiting at their respective intervertebral foramina. The filum terminale is a thin strand of pia mater that extends from the conus and anchors the cord to the coccyx.

Internal structure 3 questions

04 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the internal structure of the spinal cord in cross section.

Reveal answer

Central H-shaped or butterfly-shaped gray matter, surrounded by white matter. Gray matter has dorsal (posterior) horns (sensory) and ventral (anterior) horns (motor); in the thoracic and upper lumbar regions, lateral horns are also present (autonomic). White matter is organized into columns (funiculi): dorsal, lateral, and ventral. The central canal runs through the middle, carrying CSF.

05 DOK 1 · Recall

Compare dorsal and ventral roots of a spinal nerve.

Reveal answer

Dorsal (posterior) root: carries sensory (afferent) axons into the spinal cord. The dorsal root ganglion houses the cell bodies of these sensory neurons. Ventral (anterior) root: carries motor (efferent) axons out of the spinal cord, with cell bodies in the ventral horn. The two roots merge to form a spinal nerve just lateral to the cord.

06 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare ascending and descending tracts in the spinal cord.

Reveal answer

Ascending tracts (sensory) carry signals from the spinal cord up to the brain. Examples: dorsal columns (fine touch, vibration, proprioception), spinothalamic tract (pain and temperature). Descending tracts (motor) carry signals from the brain down. Example: corticospinal tract (voluntary motor control). Most tracts cross the midline somewhere along their path, which is why each side of the brain controls and feels the opposite side of the body.

Spinal nerves and clinical 3 questions

07 DOK 1 · Recall

How many spinal nerves are there, and how are they numbered?

Reveal answer

31 pairs: 8 cervical (C1-C8), 12 thoracic (T1-T12), 5 lumbar (L1-L5), 5 sacral (S1-S5), 1 coccygeal. Cervical nerves are named for the vertebra below them (except C8, which exits between C7 and T1). The other regions are named for the vertebra above them.

08 DOK 1 · Recall

What is a dermatome?

Reveal answer

The area of skin supplied by sensory fibers from a single spinal nerve. Dermatomes form bands on the trunk and longitudinal strips on the limbs. Mapping which dermatome is affected by sensory loss helps localize a spinal nerve problem.

09 DOK 3 · Reason

A lumbar puncture inserts a needle between L3 and L4 (or L4 and L5) into the subarachnoid space. Using cord anatomy, explain why this level is safe.

Reveal answer

The spinal cord ends at about L1-L2 in adults. Below that level the vertebral canal contains only the cauda equina (loose nerve roots floating in CSF). A needle passing in at L3-L4 enters the subarachnoid space below the end of the cord, and the loose roots tend to drift out of the way of the needle. There is no cord tissue to damage at this level.
