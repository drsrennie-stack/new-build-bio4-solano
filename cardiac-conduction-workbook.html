The conduction system structures 6 questions

01 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the components of the cardiac conduction system in order from where the signal starts to where it ends.

Reveal answer

Sinoatrial (SA) node → atrial myocardium → atrioventricular (AV) node → AV bundle (bundle of His) → right and left bundle branches → Purkinje fibers → ventricular myocardium.

02 DOK 1 · Recall

Where is the SA node, and what is its role?

Reveal answer

The SA node is a cluster of specialized cardiac muscle cells in the wall of the right atrium near the entrance of the superior vena cava. It is the heart's natural pacemaker, generating impulses on its own about 60 to 100 times per minute under normal autonomic balance.

03 DOK 1 · Recall

Where is the AV node, and what is its key feature?

Reveal answer

The AV node sits in the interatrial septum near the tricuspid valve. It receives the signal from the atria and briefly delays it (about 0.1 second). The delay lets the atria finish contracting before the ventricles start, so the atria can finish filling the ventricles.

04 DOK 1 · Recall

Where is the AV bundle (bundle of His), and what does it do?

Reveal answer

The AV bundle continues from the AV node and travels through the interventricular septum. It splits into right and left bundle branches that descend toward the apex. It is the only normal electrical pathway from atria to ventricles, because the cardiac fibrous skeleton insulates the rest of the path.

05 DOK 1 · Recall

What are Purkinje fibers, and what do they do?

Reveal answer

Purkinje fibers are specialized large-diameter conducting cardiac muscle fibers. They branch from the right and left bundle branches and spread the signal rapidly through the ventricular walls, starting at the apex. They make ventricular contraction quick and coordinated.

06 DOK 2 · Apply

Why do the ventricles begin contracting at the apex and squeeze upward toward the base?

Reveal answer

The Purkinje fibers deliver the signal first to the apex. Contraction begins there and rolls upward, which efficiently pushes blood toward the great vessels that exit at the base of the heart. The geometry maximizes the volume of blood ejected per beat.

The autonomic input 2 questions

07 DOK 1 · Recall

How do the sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous systems influence the heart?

Reveal answer

The sympathetic system (via cardiac accelerator nerves) speeds up the SA node, increases AV node conduction, and increases force of contraction. The parasympathetic system (via the vagus nerve) slows the SA node and reduces AV node conduction. The two systems counterbalance each other and adjust heart rate moment to moment.

08 DOK 2 · Apply

The heart will beat even when all autonomic nerves are cut. What does that tell you about the conduction system?

Reveal answer

The conduction system is intrinsic: the SA node generates its own impulses without needing input from the brain. Autonomic nerves only modulate the rate and force. Even an isolated heart beats on its own, which is what makes heart transplantation possible.

The ECG 3 questions

09 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the three waves of a normal ECG and what each represents.

Reveal answer

P wave: atrial depolarization (the electrical signal sweeping across the atria, which precedes atrial contraction). QRS complex: ventricular depolarization (the signal sweeping across the ventricles, which precedes ventricular contraction). T wave: ventricular repolarization (the ventricles resetting after contraction).

10 DOK 2 · Apply

Why does the ECG not show a separate wave for atrial repolarization?

Reveal answer

Atrial repolarization happens at about the same time as ventricular depolarization, and the much larger QRS complex masks it. The atrial repolarization wave exists but is buried inside the QRS.

11 DOK 2 · Apply

What does the PR interval on an ECG represent, and what would lengthen it?

Reveal answer

The PR interval is the time from the start of the P wave to the start of the QRS. It represents the time the signal takes to travel from the SA node through the atria, through the AV node delay, and into the ventricles. AV node disease lengthens this interval (first-degree AV block).

Clinical reasoning 3 questions

12 DOK 3 · Reason

If the SA node fails, the heart usually keeps beating, but more slowly. Why?

Reveal answer

Other parts of the conduction system can act as backup pacemakers (escape rhythms). The AV node fires at about 40 to 60 beats per minute on its own; ventricular cells fire at about 20 to 40. Whichever site is fastest takes over. The heart slows down but does not stop, which is why complete SA failure is survivable long enough for treatment.

13 DOK 3 · Reason

In complete (third-degree) AV block, no signal gets from the atria to the ventricles. Predict what happens to the heartbeat.

Reveal answer

The atria continue at their SA-paced rate. The ventricles fall back on a ventricular escape rhythm, typically 20 to 40 beats per minute, independent of the atria. The two beat asynchronously. Output drops, the patient may feel weak or faint, and a pacemaker is typically needed.

14 DOK 3 · Reason

A myocardial infarction affecting the interventricular septum can damage the bundle branches. Predict what kind of ECG abnormality might appear.

Reveal answer

A bundle branch block. If the right bundle branch is damaged, the right ventricle depolarizes more slowly than the left, broadening and distorting the QRS in a recognizable pattern. The same applies if the left bundle is damaged. Bundle branch blocks are clues that the septum has been injured.
