BIO 004 · Human Anatomy · Week 5

The GI Tract, a Journey

One trip from the mouth to the anus. At every stop we name the anatomy, look at the cells and histology, and say what the region does. The accessory organs join the route as tributaries.

Stop 0 · Orientation

Two groups, one continuous tube

The plan

How the system is built

  • Alimentary canal: the continuous muscular tube food travels through, mouth to anus, about 16 to 23 feet in life.
  • Accessory organs: teeth, tongue, salivary glands, liver, gallbladder, and pancreas; they sit alongside and empty secretions in through ducts.
  • Lumen: the open interior, continuous with the outside world at the mouth and anus, so food in the tube is technically still outside the body.
  • Order of the canal: mouth, most of the pharynx, esophagus, stomach, small intestine, large intestine.
Nutrients only enter the body once they are absorbed across the wall. The whole journey is about getting them there.

Stop 0 · The wall

The four layers you travel through

Histology

From the lumen outward

  • Mucosa: epithelium in contact with food, lamina propria beneath, and a thin muscularis mucosae.
  • Submucosa: areolar connective tissue with blood and lymphatic vessels and the submucosal (Meissner) plexus.
  • Muscularis: usually inner circular and outer longitudinal smooth muscle, with the myenteric (Auerbach) plexus between them.
  • Serosa: the visceral peritoneum; replaced by a fibrous adventitia on the esophagus.
  • The two plexuses form the enteric nervous system: submucosal controls secretion, myenteric controls motility.
Same four layers the whole way, retuned at each stop. A problem that erodes from mucosa to serosa can perforate into the peritoneal cavity.

Stop 0 · The lining

The epithelium changes with the job

Cells

Where the tube does abrasive work it is armored; where it absorbs it is thin.

  • Stratified squamous (protection): mouth, pharynx, esophagus, and anal canal.
  • Simple columnar (secretion and absorption): stomach and intestines.
  • Scattered through the lining: goblet cells secrete mucus, and enteroendocrine cells secrete hormones into the lamina propria.
  • The lamina propria holds MALT (mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue); the largest clusters are Peyer’s patches in the distal ileum.
  • Epithelium is replaced fast, from a couple of days in the mouth to about a week in the gut.

Stop 1

The Mouth

Ingestion, mechanical breakdown, and the first secretions.

Stop 1 · Mouth

Where the journey begins

Anatomy · Cells · Function

Anatomy

  • Oral cavity bounded by cheeks, lips, palate, and tongue; the hard palate (maxillae, palatine bones) and muscular soft palate with the uvula.
  • Tongue of skeletal muscle with papillae carrying taste buds; anchored by the lingual frenulum.
  • Teeth cut and grind; tonsils (palatine, lingual) guard the entry.

Cells and function

  • Stratified squamous epithelium for abrasion.
  • Salivary glands begin chemical digestion (salivary amylase); teeth and tongue do mechanical digestion and form the bolus.
The mouth ingests, chews, and mixes food with saliva, then the tongue pushes the bolus back to be swallowed.

Stop 2 · Pharynx & esophagus

The swallow and the slide down

Anatomy · Histology · Function

Anatomy

  • Pharynx, three regions: nasopharynx, oropharynx, laryngopharynx; the soft palate and uvula close the nasopharynx during a swallow.
  • Esophagus, a collapsible muscular tube about 10 inches long; passes the diaphragm at the esophageal hiatus.
  • Upper and lower esophageal sphincters guard each end; the lower is also called the cardiac sphincter.

Histology and function

  • Stratified squamous lining; the outer layer is a fibrous adventitia, not serosa.
  • No digestion here; peristalsis carries the bolus to the stomach.
A weak lower esophageal sphincter lets acid reflux back up, the heartburn of GERD.

Stop 3

The Stomach

A muscular mixing chamber and holding reservoir below the diaphragm.

Stop 3 · Stomach

Regions, curvatures, and three muscle layers

Anatomy

Anatomy

  • Four regions: cardia (at the esophagus), fundus (domed, upper left), body (central), and pylorus (antrum, canal, pyloric sphincter to the duodenum).
  • Greater curvature (convex, lateral) and lesser curvature (concave, medial).
  • Rugae: folds of the lining that flatten as the stomach fills.
  • Muscularis has three layers, an inner oblique added to the usual circular and longitudinal, for vigorous churning.
The extra oblique layer is the structural clue that the stomach does more than push, it churns.

Stop 3 · Stomach lining

Gastric pits, glands, and their cells

Cells · Histology

From pit to gland

  • Surface is simple columnar mucous cells; gastric pits lead down into gastric glands.
  • Parietal cells: secrete hydrochloric acid (activates pepsin, kills microbes) and intrinsic factor (for B12 absorption).
  • Chief cells: secrete pepsinogen, the inactive form of the protein-digesting enzyme pepsin.
  • Mucous neck cells: secrete acidic mucus. Enteroendocrine G cells: secrete gastrin.
The mucosal barrier, a thick bicarbonate-rich mucus plus tight junctions plus fast cell turnover, keeps the stomach from digesting itself. Break it and you get an ulcer.

Stop 3 · Stomach

What the stomach does

Function

A holding tank that churns food into liquid and starts protein digestion.

  • Mechanical: mixing waves churn food with gastric juice into chyme.
  • Chemical: HCl and pepsin begin protein digestion; little absorption occurs (some water, alcohol, aspirin).
  • Gastric emptying: the pyloric sphincter releases small squirts of chyme into the duodenum so the small intestine is not overwhelmed.
  • The one truly essential job: making intrinsic factor for vitamin B12 absorption.

Stop 4

The Small Intestine

The long, coiled workhorse where most digestion finishes and nearly all absorption happens.

Stop 4 · Small intestine

Three regions and the ducts that feed it

Anatomy

Anatomy

  • Duodenum, about 12 inches, C-curve around the head of the pancreas; mostly retroperitoneal. Bile and pancreatic juice enter at the major duodenal papilla from the hepatopancreatic ampulla, guarded by the sphincter of Oddi.
  • Jejunum, about 3 feet, the main site of absorption.
  • Ileum, about 6 feet, joins the large intestine at the ileocecal sphincter; rich in Peyer’s patches.
The duodenum is the junction where the accessory tributaries (liver and pancreas) deliver into the canal.

Stop 4 · Small intestine lining

Three ways the surface is amplified

Cells · Histology

Surface area, x600

  • Circular folds (plicae circulares): permanent ridges of mucosa and submucosa that spiral the chyme.
  • Villi: finger-like mucosal projections; each holds a capillary bed and a lacteal (lymphatic capillary) for fats.
  • Microvilli: the brush border on absorptive cells, carrying enzymes that finish digestion.

Cells

  • Absorptive (enterocytes) and goblet cells line the surface; Paneth cells (lysozyme) and enteroendocrine cells sit in the crypts (intestinal glands).
Folds, villi, and microvilli stack three magnifications of surface area so absorption can keep up.

Stop 4 · Small intestine

Where nutrients enter the body

Function

Most digestion finishes here, and nearly all absorption happens here.

  • Segmentation mixes chyme with bile and pancreatic and intestinal juices, then migrating motility complexes move it along.
  • Sugars and amino acids enter the blood capillary of the villus, bound for the hepatic portal vein and liver.
  • Fats enter the lacteal and travel by the lymphatic system.
  • Brush-border enzymes finish carbohydrate and protein digestion right at the cell surface.

Stop 5

The Large Intestine

The wide, final stretch that reclaims water, houses the flora, and forms and stores feces.

Stop 5 · Large intestine

Cecum to anus, and its signature muscle bands

Anatomy

Anatomy

  • Cecum with the vermiform appendix (lymphatic tissue), below the ileocecal valve.
  • Colon: ascending, transverse, descending, sigmoid (with right colic and left colic flexures).
  • Rectum (rectal valves) then the anal canal with internal (smooth, involuntary) and external (skeletal, voluntary) anal sphincters.
  • Three signatures: teniae coli (three muscle bands), haustra (the pouches they make), and omental appendices (fat tags).
The teniae coli are shorter than the colon, so they gather it into haustra, the puckered look.

Stop 5 · Large intestine

Lining and final jobs

Cells · Histology · Function

Lining

  • Simple columnar epithelium of absorptive cells and abundant goblet cells; no villi and no circular folds; deep intestinal glands.
  • Goblet mucus eases the passage of feces and shields the wall from bacterial acids and gases.

Function

  • Absorbs the remaining water and salts, turning liquid residue into semisolid feces.
  • Bacterial flora ferment leftover carbohydrate and synthesize vitamins (K, some B).
  • Mass movements drive feces to the rectum; the defecation reflex empties it.
No villi, lots of goblet cells: the lining is built for lubrication and water recovery, not nutrient uptake.

Tributaries

The accessory organs that empty into the route

Anatomy · Function

They join the canal through ducts

  • Salivary glands (parotid, submandibular, sublingual) deliver saliva into the mouth to moisten and begin starch digestion.
  • Liver makes bile; the gallbladder stores and concentrates it. Bile travels hepatic ducts to the common bile duct to the duodenum.
  • Pancreas is exocrine (acini make enzyme-rich pancreatic juice) and endocrine (islets make hormones); its duct joins the bile duct at the ampulla.
  • Bile and pancreatic juice both enter at the major duodenal papilla, the small intestine.
A gallstone stuck at the shared ampulla can block bile and pancreatic juice at once, one way it triggers pancreatitis.

The route

Trace a bite from end to end

Sequence

One direction, each region in turn.

  • 1 Mouth taken in, chewed, mixed with saliva.
  • 2 Pharynx the swallow passes the oropharynx and laryngopharynx.
  • 3 Esophagus peristalsis carries the bolus to the stomach.
  • 4 Stomach churned and held as it becomes chyme.
  • 5 Small intestine duodenum, jejunum, ileum: most absorption.
  • 6 Large intestine cecum, colon, rectum, anal canal: water reclaimed, feces formed.
  • 7 Anus feces eliminated.

Wrap-up

Key takeaways

Review
  • Two groups: a continuous canal and the accessory organs that empty in.
  • The same four-layer wall is retuned at every stop; the epithelium armors the ends and thins for absorption in the middle.
  • Stomach churns and starts protein digestion; its cells (parietal, chief, G) make HCl, pepsinogen, and gastrin.
  • Small intestine finishes digestion and absorbs, amplified by folds, villi, and microvilli; fats go to lacteals.
  • Large intestine reclaims water, houses the flora, and forms feces.
