The integument overview 3 questions

01 DOK 1 · Recall

List the major functions of the integumentary system.

Reveal answer

Protection (barrier against pathogens, mechanical trauma, UV, water loss), temperature regulation (sweat, blood flow in skin), sensation (touch, pressure, pain, temperature receptors), excretion (sweat removes some salts and waste), vitamin D synthesis (in the deep epidermis with UV light), and to a lesser extent immune surveillance.

02 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the major layers of the integument from outside to inside.

Reveal answer

Epidermis (outer, stratified squamous epithelium, avascular). Dermis (deep, dense irregular connective tissue, vascular and innervated). Hypodermis or subcutaneous layer (deepest, mostly adipose connective tissue). Anatomists usually call the hypodermis 'subcutaneous tissue' and treat it as separate from skin proper, even though the three layers are continuous.

03 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare thick skin and thin skin by where each is found and how their epidermal layers differ.

Reveal answer

Thick skin is found only on the palms of the hands and the soles of the feet. It has all five epidermal strata, including the stratum lucidum, and a much thicker stratum corneum to resist wear. Thin skin covers the rest of the body. It lacks the stratum lucidum and has a thinner stratum corneum, and most thin skin also carries hair follicles (thick skin does not).

The epidermis: layers 7 questions

04 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the strata of the epidermis from deep to superficial.

Reveal answer

From deep to superficial: stratum basale (germinativum), stratum spinosum, stratum granulosum, stratum lucidum (thick skin only), stratum corneum.

05 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the stratum basale, including the cells found there.

Reveal answer

The stratum basale (germinativum) is the deepest epidermal layer, a single row of cuboidal to columnar keratinocytes attached to the basement membrane. It is the actively dividing layer that produces all the keratinocytes above it. It also contains scattered melanocytes (pigment cells) and Merkel cells (touch receptors).

06 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the stratum spinosum and explain its name.

Reveal answer

The stratum spinosum sits above the basale; it is many cell layers thick, made of keratinocytes with spiny-looking projections in tissue preparations. The 'spines' are where neighboring cells join by abundant desmosomes; tissue processing shrinks the cells and pulls the desmosomes into a spiky appearance. The layer also contains Langerhans (dendritic) cells, which are antigen-presenting immune cells.

07 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the stratum granulosum and what happens to keratinocytes there.

Reveal answer

The stratum granulosum is three to five layers of flattened keratinocytes filled with keratohyalin granules and lamellar (membrane-coating) granules. The keratohyalin contributes to keratin formation; the lamellar granules release lipids that waterproof the skin. Above this layer most keratinocytes die: they are too far from the dermal blood supply to survive.

08 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the stratum lucidum and where it occurs.

Reveal answer

The stratum lucidum is a thin, clear layer of flat, dead keratinocytes packed with keratin precursor. It is found only in thick skin (palms and soles) and gives that skin its extra wear-resistance.

09 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the stratum corneum.

Reveal answer

The stratum corneum is the outer layer of dead, flattened, fully keratinized cells (squames). It is many layers thick and forms the body's principal mechanical and water-loss barrier. Squames are continuously shed from the surface as new cells push up from below.

10 DOK 2 · Apply

Trace the life of a keratinocyte from its origin to its loss from the body.

Reveal answer

A keratinocyte is born when a cell in the stratum basale divides. As cells push it upward, it passes through the stratum spinosum (acquiring desmosomes and shape), the stratum granulosum (filling with keratin precursor and lipids), the stratum lucidum (only in thick skin), and finally the stratum corneum, where it is a dead, flat, keratin-filled squame. It is shed from the surface roughly a month after birth. The cycle is continuous and steady.

The epidermis: cell types 5 questions

11 DOK 1 · Recall

What is a keratinocyte and what does it produce?

Reveal answer

A keratinocyte is the most common cell type in the epidermis (about 90%). It produces keratin, a tough fibrous protein that gives the epidermis its mechanical strength and resistance to abrasion. Keratinocytes also release defensive peptides and signaling molecules.

12 DOK 1 · Recall

What is a melanocyte, where is it found, and what does it do?

Reveal answer

A melanocyte is a pigment cell scattered through the stratum basale. It synthesizes melanin in organelles called melanosomes and transfers melanosomes to neighboring keratinocytes. Melanin gives skin its color and shields the DNA of basal keratinocytes from UV damage.

13 DOK 1 · Recall

What is a Langerhans (dendritic) cell, where is it found, and what does it do?

Reveal answer

A Langerhans cell is a dendritic immune cell found mostly in the stratum spinosum. It captures antigens that penetrate the epidermis (microbes, foreign proteins) and presents them to T cells. It is the resident antigen-presenting cell of the skin.

14 DOK 1 · Recall

What is a Merkel cell, where is it found, and what does it do?

Reveal answer

A Merkel cell sits in the stratum basale, especially in highly sensitive skin (fingertips, lips). Each Merkel cell is closely associated with a sensory nerve ending (a tactile disc). Together they detect light, sustained touch.

15 DOK 2 · Compare

Three factors shape natural skin color. Name them and explain how each contributes.

Reveal answer

Melanin (made by melanocytes) is the main pigment; more melanin gives darker skin. Carotene, a yellow-orange pigment from certain foods, accumulates in the stratum corneum and the hypodermis and tints skin yellow-orange. Hemoglobin in the blood of dermal capillaries gives skin its pinkish hue, especially in fair-skinned individuals where less melanin masks it.

The dermis and hypodermis 5 questions

16 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the two layers of the dermis and the kind of connective tissue in each.

Reveal answer

The papillary layer is the thin, superficial layer of areolar connective tissue. It forms the dermal papillae that interlock with the epidermis. The reticular layer is the deeper, thicker layer of dense irregular connective tissue. It carries most of the larger vessels, nerves, and accessory structures.

17 DOK 2 · Apply

What are dermal papillae, what do they create at the skin surface, and what do they do for the avascular epidermis?

Reveal answer

Dermal papillae are finger-like projections of the papillary dermis up into the epidermis. They interlock the two layers (resisting shearing forces). In thick skin, they create the surface ridges we see as fingerprints. They also carry capillary loops that bring blood close enough to the epidermis to feed the basal keratinocytes by diffusion.

18 DOK 1 · Recall

List the structures that live in the dermis.

Reveal answer

Blood and lymphatic vessels; sensory nerves and receptors (Meissner corpuscles in the papillary layer, Pacinian corpuscles deep in the reticular layer); hair follicles; sebaceous glands; sweat glands; arrector pili muscles. The dermis is where the working machinery of the skin lives.

19 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the hypodermis, what is it made of, and what does it do?

Reveal answer

The hypodermis (subcutaneous tissue, superficial fascia) sits deep to the dermis. It is mostly adipose tissue with some areolar connective tissue. It anchors the skin to underlying structures, insulates the body, cushions deeper tissues, and stores energy. It is not part of the skin proper but is functionally integrated with it.

20 DOK 2 · Apply

Stretch marks (striae) appear in skin that has been rapidly stretched. Which layer is responsible, and why do the marks remain even after the stretch is over?

Reveal answer

The dermis is responsible. Rapid stretching tears the collagen and elastin of the reticular dermis. The tears heal as fibrous scars, which look thinner and paler than surrounding skin. Because dermal scarring does not return to normal collagen organization, the marks persist.

Hair 4 questions

21 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the major parts of a hair and its follicle.

Reveal answer

Hair shaft: the visible part above the skin surface. Hair root: the part embedded in the follicle, below the surface. Hair bulb: the expanded base of the root, surrounding the hair papilla. Hair papilla: a small projection of dermis carrying blood vessels into the bulb. Matrix: actively dividing keratinocytes in the bulb that produce the hair. Follicle: the sheath of epidermis and dermis that holds the root.

22 DOK 1 · Recall

What is hair made of and how does its keratin differ from that of the skin?

Reveal answer

Hair is built from keratinocytes that fill with hard keratin and die as they are pushed upward. Hard (hair) keratin is more compact, more sulfur-rich, and tougher than the soft keratin in the epidermal stratum corneum. The same general protein family, but optimized differently for the two jobs.

23 DOK 1 · Recall

What determines hair color, and what produces gray hair?

Reveal answer

Hair color is determined by melanin made by melanocytes in the hair matrix and transferred to keratinocytes there, just as in the skin. Different ratios of eumelanin (brown to black) and pheomelanin (red to yellow) produce the range of hair colors. Gray hair appears when matrix melanocytes lose activity or die: less melanin in the keratinocytes, so the keratin appears its native pale color.

24 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the arrector pili, what kind of structure is it, and what does it do?

Reveal answer

The arrector pili is a small bundle of smooth muscle attached at one end to the hair follicle and at the other to the papillary dermis. When it contracts, it pulls the hair upright. The result is goosebumps and the appearance of fuller hair, and historically (in furry mammals) it created insulation and made the animal look bigger.

Nails 2 questions

25 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the major parts of a fingernail.

Reveal answer

Nail plate: the hard, visible portion. Free edge: the part that extends beyond the fingertip. Nail body: the attached part of the plate. Nail root: the part of the plate hidden under the proximal nail fold. Nail bed: the epidermis under the body and root. Nail matrix: the proximal part of the nail bed where new nail tissue is produced. Eponychium (cuticle): a strip of stratum corneum over the proximal nail fold. Hyponychium: the thickened epidermis under the free edge. Lunula: the white half-moon at the base, where the underlying matrix shows through.

26 DOK 1 · Recall

Where do nails grow from, and what tissue makes them?

Reveal answer

Nails grow from the nail matrix, which sits at the proximal end of the nail bed under the proximal nail fold. The matrix is a thickened bed of dividing keratinocytes. As new cells are made and keratinize, they push older nail forward, so the nail grows distally from a steady source at the proximal end.

Skin glands 4 questions

27 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe sebaceous glands: where they sit, what they secrete, by what mechanism, and onto what.

Reveal answer

Sebaceous glands sit in the dermis. Most empty into hair follicles, so their product reaches the surface along the hair shaft; a few open directly onto skin. They secrete sebum (an oily mixture of lipids, broken-down cells, and metabolic by-products). They release by holocrine secretion: cells fill with sebum and rupture, releasing both product and cellular debris. Sebum lubricates the skin and hair and slows water loss.

28 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe eccrine sweat glands and what they do.

Reveal answer

Eccrine (merocrine) sweat glands are distributed over almost the entire body, especially the palms, soles, and forehead. They are coiled tubular glands in the dermis that open directly onto the skin surface through a duct. They secrete a watery sweat by merocrine release (exocytosis). Their main job is thermoregulation: evaporating sweat cools the body.

29 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe apocrine sweat glands and how they differ from eccrine glands.

Reveal answer

Apocrine sweat glands are larger and concentrated in the axillary and anogenital regions. They release their secretion into hair follicles, not directly onto the surface. Their sweat is thicker, oilier, and contains proteins and lipids; bacterial breakdown of these molecules on the skin produces body odor. They activate at puberty. Despite the name they are now thought to use a merocrine mechanism in most cases, but the older 'apocrine' name has stuck.

30 DOK 1 · Recall

What are ceruminous and mammary glands, and what kind of gland is each?

Reveal answer

Ceruminous glands are modified apocrine glands in the external auditory canal that produce cerumen (earwax), which traps debris and discourages microorganisms. Mammary glands are modified apocrine glands of the breast that produce milk during lactation.

Burns and clinical anatomy 5 questions

31 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe a first-degree (superficial) burn.

Reveal answer

A first-degree burn damages only the epidermis. The skin is red, painful, and slightly swollen, with no blister formation. Healing takes a few days and leaves no scar. A typical sunburn is a first-degree burn.

32 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe a second-degree (partial-thickness) burn.

Reveal answer

A second-degree burn damages the epidermis and part of the dermis. Blisters form because plasma collects between the damaged layers. The skin is red, painful, and weeping. Because some dermal accessory structures (hair follicles, glands) survive, the epidermis can regrow from them and the burn typically heals without major scarring, though it takes weeks.

33 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe a third-degree (full-thickness) burn.

Reveal answer

A third-degree burn destroys the epidermis and the entire dermis, and often reaches the hypodermis or below. The wound is leathery, dry, and white, black, or red, and it is paradoxically not painful at the burn site because the sensory nerves of the dermis have been destroyed. The epidermis cannot regenerate from the wound itself because the source structures are gone; grafting is required. Scarring is typical.

34 DOK 3 · Reason

A patient with extensive burns is at high risk for fluid loss and infection within the first day. Using what you know about the layers of the integument, explain why.

Reveal answer

The intact epidermis is the body's main barrier against water loss and pathogen entry. When that barrier is destroyed over a large area, the dermal capillaries are exposed and dump fluid (plasma) freely; the body can lose liters per day. At the same time, the route into the body is wide open for skin and environmental microorganisms. Both consequences trace directly to the epidermal barrier being gone.

35 DOK 2 · Apply

People with darker skin have lower rates of UV-induced skin cancer on average. Using what you know about melanin and where melanocytes sit, explain why.

Reveal answer

Melanocytes in the stratum basale transfer melanin granules to the basal keratinocytes. The granules sit like umbrellas over the nuclei, shielding the DNA from UV photons. With more melanin, more UV is absorbed before it reaches DNA, so fewer DNA mutations accumulate over time. Fewer mutations in basal keratinocytes (and in melanocytes themselves) means fewer skin cancers, on average.
