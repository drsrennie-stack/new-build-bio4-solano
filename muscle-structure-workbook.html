Levels of organization 3 questions

01 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the levels of skeletal muscle organization from whole muscle down to the contractile protein.

Reveal answer

Whole muscle (e.g., biceps) → fascicles (bundles of fibers) → muscle fibers (single muscle cells) → myofibrils (long protein cylinders within a fiber) → sarcomeres (the repeating contractile units) → myofilaments (the actin and myosin proteins themselves).

02 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the three connective tissue wrappings of skeletal muscle and what each surrounds.

Reveal answer

Endomysium: thin layer of areolar CT around each individual muscle fiber. Perimysium: denser CT around each fascicle. Epimysium: tough dense irregular CT covering the entire muscle. All three converge at the ends as the muscle's tendon, anchoring it to bone.

03 DOK 1 · Recall

Distinguish tendon from aponeurosis.

Reveal answer

Both are dense regular connective tissue extensions of muscle wrappings that attach muscle to bone or other structures. A tendon is rope-like (cord-shaped); an aponeurosis is broad and flat (sheet-shaped). The Achilles is a tendon; the broad flat attachment of an abdominal muscle is an aponeurosis.

Muscle fiber anatomy 4 questions

04 DOK 1 · Recall

List the distinctive features of a skeletal muscle fiber.

Reveal answer

Long cylindrical multinucleated cell; nuclei pushed to the periphery just under the sarcolemma; striated due to organized myofilaments; filled with myofibrils that run the length of the fiber; surrounded by sarcoplasmic reticulum and traversed by T tubules.

05 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the sarcolemma, and what is unusual about it compared to a typical cell membrane?

Reveal answer

The sarcolemma is the plasma membrane of a muscle fiber. It is excitable: it can propagate action potentials like a neuron. T tubules are invaginations of the sarcolemma that carry the action potential deep into the fiber.

06 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the sarcoplasmic reticulum, and what does it store?

Reveal answer

The sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR) is the specialized smooth ER of muscle. It wraps around each myofibril. It stores and releases calcium ions, which trigger contraction when released into the cytosol.

07 DOK 1 · Recall

What is a triad in a muscle fiber, and what does it accomplish?

Reveal answer

A triad is a T tubule flanked by two terminal cisternae of the sarcoplasmic reticulum. When an action potential travels down a T tubule, the triad arrangement triggers the adjacent SR to release calcium, coupling membrane excitation to contraction quickly throughout the fiber.

Sarcomere and myofilaments 5 questions

08 DOK 1 · Recall

What is a sarcomere, and what defines its boundaries?

Reveal answer

A sarcomere is the fundamental contractile unit of a skeletal or cardiac muscle fiber. It is the segment of a myofibril between two Z discs. Sarcomeres are arranged end to end along each myofibril.

09 DOK 1 · Recall

Compare thick and thin filaments by protein and where in the sarcomere each sits.

Reveal answer

Thick filaments are made of myosin. They sit in the middle of the sarcomere, anchored to the M line. Thin filaments are made primarily of actin (plus tropomyosin and troponin). They are anchored to the Z disc at each end and project toward the M line, overlapping with thick filaments in the middle.

10 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the bands and lines of a sarcomere and what each one represents.

Reveal answer

Z disc: boundary of the sarcomere; anchors thin filaments. I band: light area containing only thin filaments; spans the Z disc. A band: dark area, the length of the thick filaments; contains the entire myosin filament. H zone: lighter region in the middle of the A band where only thick filaments sit (no overlap). M line: dark line in the center of the H zone; anchors thick filaments. During contraction, the I band and H zone shorten; the A band stays the same length.

11 DOK 1 · Recall

What are tropomyosin and troponin, and what do they do?

Reveal answer

Both are regulatory proteins on the thin filament. Tropomyosin is a long strand that lies along the actin filament and covers the myosin-binding sites on actin when the muscle is at rest. Troponin is a three-subunit complex bound to tropomyosin. When calcium binds troponin, the troponin-tropomyosin complex shifts and uncovers the binding sites, allowing myosin to bind actin and contraction to occur.

12 DOK 2 · Apply

What is the basic idea of the sliding filament theory, expressed without molecular detail?

Reveal answer

The thick and thin filaments do not change length during contraction. Instead, the thin filaments slide past the thick filaments toward the center of each sarcomere, pulling the Z discs closer together. The sarcomere shortens, and so does the entire fiber.

Neuromuscular junction 3 questions

13 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the neuromuscular junction, and what are its three main components?

Reveal answer

The neuromuscular junction (NMJ) is the synapse between a motor neuron and a skeletal muscle fiber. Its three components are: the presynaptic terminal of the motor axon (containing vesicles of acetylcholine), the synaptic cleft (the narrow gap between cell and fiber), and the motor end plate (a specialized region of sarcolemma with many folds and acetylcholine receptors).

14 DOK 1 · Recall

What is a motor unit?

Reveal answer

A motor unit is one motor neuron plus all of the muscle fibers it innervates. Motor units are the functional unit of skeletal muscle. Small motor units (one neuron with few fibers, e.g., in the eye) allow fine control. Large motor units (one neuron with many fibers, e.g., in postural muscles) allow strong force.

15 DOK 1 · Recall

What neurotransmitter is released at the NMJ, and what receptor binds it on the muscle side?

Reveal answer

Acetylcholine (ACh). It binds nicotinic ACh receptors on the motor end plate, which open ion channels and initiate the action potential in the muscle fiber that triggers contraction.

Three muscle types compared 2 questions

16 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare skeletal, cardiac, and smooth muscle by striations, number of nuclei per cell, control, and one distinctive feature of each.

Reveal answer

Skeletal: striated, multinucleated, voluntary, attached to bone; distinctive feature is conscious control via motor neurons. Cardiac: striated, usually one nucleus (sometimes two), involuntary, only in the heart; distinctive feature is intercalated discs that join branching cells. Smooth: non-striated, one nucleus, involuntary, walls of hollow organs and vessels; distinctive feature is spindle-shaped cells with no sarcomeres.

17 DOK 2 · Apply

Smooth muscle contracts even though it has no sarcomeres. What arrangement of contractile proteins makes that possible?

Reveal answer

Smooth muscle has actin and myosin organized in diagonal bundles attached at dense bodies on the membrane and within the cell. When these bundles contract, the cell shortens and twists rather than shortening along a single axis. The result is contraction without the striped appearance of organized sarcomeres.
