Cartilage as a tissue 5 questions

01 DOK 1 · Recall

List the general features of cartilage as a tissue: cells, matrix, vascularity, and innervation.

Reveal answer

Cells: chondroblasts (immature, build matrix) and chondrocytes (mature, sit in lacunae). Matrix: firm but flexible, dominated by collagen and proteoglycans, rich in chondroitin sulfate. Avascular: no blood vessels run through cartilage. Not innervated: no nerves. Both features mean cartilage heals slowly because nutrients arrive only by diffusion.

02 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare the three cartilage types by distinguishing feature and one typical location.

Reveal answer

Hyaline cartilage is most common, has fine collagen in a glassy matrix; found on the ends of bones, in costal cartilages, the nose, the trachea, the larynx. Elastic cartilage is flexible with abundant elastic fibers; found in the external ear and the epiglottis. Fibrocartilage is tough with thick collagen bundles for shock absorption; found in the intervertebral discs, the knee menisci, and the pubic symphysis.

03 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the perichondrium, what is it made of, and what does it do for the cartilage it covers?

Reveal answer

The perichondrium is a dense irregular connective tissue covering on most cartilage. It supplies nutrients to the avascular cartilage by diffusion, holds the cartilage in place, and contains the cells (chondroblasts at its inner surface) that can build new cartilage.

04 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare appositional and interstitial growth of cartilage.

Reveal answer

Appositional growth occurs at the surface: chondroblasts in the perichondrium deposit new matrix on the outside, so the cartilage grows wider. Interstitial growth occurs inside: chondrocytes already trapped in lacunae divide and produce more matrix, so the cartilage grows from within. Most cartilage uses both, especially during development.

05 DOK 1 · Recall

Distinguish chondroblasts from chondrocytes.

Reveal answer

Chondroblasts are immature cartilage cells that actively secrete matrix; they live near the surface (at the perichondrium) where new cartilage is being added. Once a chondroblast is trapped within the matrix it has produced, it is called a chondrocyte; chondrocytes sit in spaces called lacunae and maintain the matrix.

Bone classification 5 questions

06 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the five categories of bones by shape and give one example of each.

Reveal answer

Long bones (longer than wide): femur, humerus, phalanges. Short bones (roughly equal in dimensions): carpals, tarsals. Flat bones (thin, broad, often curved): cranial bones, ribs, sternum, scapula. Irregular bones (complex shapes that don't fit the others): vertebrae, facial bones. Sesamoid bones (small bones embedded in tendons): the patella is the largest example.

07 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the major parts of a long bone in order from one end to the other.

Reveal answer

From end to end: the proximal epiphysis (the expanded end), the metaphysis (the region just under the epiphysis where the growth plate sat), the diaphysis (the long shaft), the distal metaphysis, and the distal epiphysis. Articular cartilage covers the ends; the medullary cavity lies inside the diaphysis; the periosteum covers the outer surface; the endosteum lines the marrow cavities.

08 DOK 1 · Recall

Distinguish the epiphyseal plate from the epiphyseal line.

Reveal answer

The epiphyseal plate is the hyaline cartilage growth plate present during childhood and adolescence. It is where the long bone grows in length. The epiphyseal line is the bony scar that remains after the plate has ossified at the end of growth. Plate during growth; line afterward.

09 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the periosteum and what it does.

Reveal answer

The periosteum is a tough double-layered membrane that covers the outer surface of bone (except where bone is covered by articular cartilage). The outer fibrous layer is dense irregular connective tissue; the inner cellular layer contains osteogenic cells. It anchors tendons and ligaments to bone, carries vessels into the bone, and provides cells for growth and repair.

10 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the endosteum and where does it sit?

Reveal answer

The endosteum is a delicate membrane that lines the inner surfaces of bone: the medullary cavity, the central canals of osteons, and the spaces in spongy bone. It also carries osteogenic cells and is involved in growth and remodeling from the inside.

Bone microanatomy 7 questions

11 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the osteon, the structural unit of compact bone, including its named parts.

Reveal answer

An osteon (Haversian system) is a cylindrical unit running parallel to the long axis of the bone. At its center is the central (Haversian) canal, which carries blood vessels and nerves. Around the canal sit concentric rings (lamellae) of bone matrix. Lacunae between the lamellae house osteocytes. Tiny channels called canaliculi connect lacuna to lacuna and lacuna to the central canal, so osteocytes can exchange nutrients and waste.

12 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare Haversian and Volkmann (perforating) canals.

Reveal answer

Haversian (central) canals run parallel to the long axis of the bone, one through the center of each osteon. Volkmann (perforating) canals run perpendicular to them, connecting one Haversian canal to another and to the periosteum. Together they form an interconnected network of vessels that feeds the entire bone.

13 DOK 1 · Recall

What are lamellae in bone, and what is the difference between concentric, interstitial, and circumferential lamellae?

Reveal answer

Lamellae are layers of mineralized bone matrix arranged in rings. Concentric lamellae form the rings around the central canal of an osteon. Interstitial lamellae are leftover fragments of older osteons that have been partially remodeled, sitting between intact osteons. Circumferential lamellae wrap around the outer and inner surfaces of the entire bone, just inside the periosteum and just outside the endosteum.

14 DOK 1 · Recall

What are trabeculae, where do you find them, and how are they organized?

Reveal answer

Trabeculae are the thin bony struts of spongy (cancellous) bone. They form an open lattice that gives spongy bone its sponge-like look. Trabeculae are not random: they align along the lines of mechanical stress on the bone, making spongy bone strong while keeping it light.

15 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare compact and spongy bone by structure, location within a bone, and functional role.

Reveal answer

Compact bone is dense, built from packed osteons, and forms the strong outer shell of every bone and the walls of the diaphysis. Spongy bone is an open lattice of trabeculae, lines the interior of every bone, and fills the epiphyses of long bones. Compact resists stress; spongy lightens the bone and houses red bone marrow.

16 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the four bone cells and what each one does.

Reveal answer

Osteogenic (osteoprogenitor) cells are stem cells in the periosteum and endosteum; they divide and give rise to osteoblasts. Osteoblasts secrete the bone matrix (osteoid) and start its mineralization. Osteocytes are mature osteoblasts trapped in lacunae; they maintain the matrix and sense mechanical load. Osteoclasts are large multinucleated cells that resorb bone matrix to release calcium or remodel the bone.

17 DOK 1 · Recall

Osteoclasts have an unusual origin compared with the other bone cells. What is it?

Reveal answer

Osteoclasts come from the fusion of monocytes (a type of white blood cell) and are part of the macrophage lineage. The other bone cells come from mesenchymal stem cells. So bone-building cells share an origin with connective tissue; bone-resorbing cells share an origin with immune cells.

Bone formation and growth 5 questions

18 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare intramembranous and endochondral ossification by what each starts from and which bones each forms.

Reveal answer

Intramembranous ossification turns a sheet of mesenchymal connective tissue directly into bone. It forms the flat bones of the skull, the clavicles, and the mandible. Endochondral ossification builds a hyaline cartilage model first and then replaces it with bone. It forms most other bones of the body, including all long bones.

19 DOK 2 · Apply

In broad strokes, what happens during endochondral ossification of a long bone?

Reveal answer

A hyaline cartilage model of the bone forms first. A primary ossification center appears in the middle of the diaphysis: cartilage there is calcified, chondrocytes die, blood vessels invade, and osteoblasts deposit bone matrix on the cartilage scaffold. Ossification spreads toward the ends of the bone. Secondary ossification centers appear later in each epiphysis. Cartilage remains only as the articular cartilage on the ends and as the epiphyseal plate between epiphysis and diaphysis until growth ends.

20 DOK 2 · Compare

How does a long bone grow in length, and how does it grow in width?

Reveal answer

In length: at the epiphyseal plate, by interstitial growth of cartilage on the epiphysis side and ossification on the diaphysis side. When the plate ossifies and becomes the epiphyseal line, growth in length stops. In width: by appositional growth on the bone surface, where osteoblasts in the periosteum add new bone on the outside while osteoclasts inside resorb bone to enlarge the medullary cavity.

21 DOK 2 · Apply

Name the zones of the epiphyseal plate from the epiphysis toward the diaphysis, and state what is happening in each.

Reveal answer

From the epiphysis end toward the diaphysis: (1) Resting (reserve) zone: anchors the plate to the epiphysis; chondrocytes are quiet. (2) Proliferation zone: chondrocytes divide, line up in columns, and push the epiphysis away from the diaphysis. (3) Hypertrophy zone: chondrocytes enlarge and the surrounding matrix thins. (4) Calcification zone: the thinned matrix calcifies and chondrocytes die. (5) Ossification zone: blood vessels invade, osteoclasts clear remnants, osteoblasts deposit new bone on the cartilage scaffold.

22 DOK 2 · Apply

At the end of long bone growth, what happens to the epiphyseal plate, and how does this show on an X-ray?

Reveal answer

The plate is fully replaced by bone, leaving a thin bony remnant called the epiphyseal line. On an X-ray, a wide open plate appears as a dark band of cartilage between two pieces of bone; a closed plate appears as a thin bright line, or disappears completely. Radiologists use plate appearance to estimate skeletal age.

Clinical reasoning and skeletal imaging 4 questions

23 DOK 3 · Reason

A child's long bone fractures through the epiphyseal plate. Predict why this injury is more concerning than a similar break in an adult bone.

Reveal answer

The epiphyseal plate is the engine of length growth. Damage to the cartilage in the plate, or to its blood supply, can permanently stop or distort growth in that bone. The result can be a limb that is shorter than its mate or that grows in a crooked direction. The same fracture in an adult (whose plate has long since closed) does not have this consequence because there is no longer any plate to disturb.

24 DOK 2 · Apply

On an X-ray of a child's knee, you can see a dark band of cartilage in each long bone near the joint. What is that, and what would it look like in an adult X-ray?

Reveal answer

The dark band is the epiphyseal plate, still active and still mostly cartilage, so it does not block the X-ray. In an adult, the plate has fully ossified, so there is no longer a dark band: only a thin bright line (the epiphyseal line) or no line at all.

25 DOK 3 · Reason

Osteoporosis is a disease in which osteoclast activity outpaces osteoblast activity. Using what you know about bone remodeling, predict the structural consequences in compact bone and in spongy bone.

Reveal answer

In compact bone, the dense outer cortex thins as more matrix is removed than is replaced. In spongy bone, trabeculae become thinner and some are removed entirely, so the lattice loses connections. The result is bone that is more fragile in both regions, fracturing more easily under loads it once handled (vertebrae compress, the femoral neck breaks).

26 DOK 2 · Apply

Vitamin D is needed to absorb calcium from the gut. Using what you know about bone matrix, predict what happens to a bone when vitamin D is severely deficient in a growing child versus an adult.

Reveal answer

Bone matrix has two parts: organic (osteoid, mostly collagen) and inorganic (mineral, mostly calcium phosphate). Without calcium, the matrix laid down by osteoblasts cannot mineralize properly. In a growing child, the result is rickets: soft, weak bones that deform under the child's weight (bowed legs, deformed chest). In an adult, the result is osteomalacia: soft, weak bones that produce pain and easy fracture, but without the dramatic deformities of growing bones because the adult skeleton is no longer being shaped by growth.
