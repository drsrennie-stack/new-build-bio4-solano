Major regions 1 question

01 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the four major regions of the brain.

Reveal answer

Cerebrum (two hemispheres, the largest part), diencephalon (deep midline structures including thalamus and hypothalamus), brainstem (midbrain, pons, medulla — covered separately), and cerebellum (two hemispheres beneath the posterior cerebrum).

Cerebrum 6 questions

02 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the five lobes of the cerebral cortex.

Reveal answer

Frontal, parietal, temporal, occipital, and insula (deep, hidden beneath the lateral sulcus). The first four are visible on the surface; the insula is buried.

03 DOK 1 · Recall

What are gyri and sulci?

Reveal answer

Gyri are the ridges of the folded cerebral cortex. Sulci are the grooves between them. A deeper sulcus is called a fissure. Folding multiplies cortical surface area within the limited space of the cranial cavity.

04 DOK 1 · Recall

Name three important sulci or fissures of the cerebrum.

Reveal answer

Longitudinal fissure (separates the two hemispheres). Central sulcus (separates frontal and parietal lobes; runs vertically). Lateral sulcus (separates the temporal lobe from the frontal and parietal lobes).

05 DOK 2 · Apply

Name and locate four functional regions of the cerebral cortex.

Reveal answer

Primary motor cortex: precentral gyrus (frontal lobe, just anterior to the central sulcus); voluntary motor control. Primary somatosensory cortex: postcentral gyrus (parietal lobe, just posterior to the central sulcus); body sensation. Primary visual cortex: occipital lobe; vision. Primary auditory cortex: superior temporal gyrus; hearing. Broca's area (left frontal) for speech production and Wernicke's area (left temporal-parietal) for language comprehension are usually in the dominant (typically left) hemisphere.

06 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the corpus callosum?

Reveal answer

A massive band of white matter (about 200 million axons) connecting the two cerebral hemispheres beneath the longitudinal fissure. It allows the hemispheres to communicate and coordinate.

07 DOK 1 · Recall

What are the basal ganglia, and what do they do in general terms?

Reveal answer

Subcortical clusters of gray matter deep within the cerebrum (caudate nucleus, putamen, globus pallidus, and functionally the substantia nigra and subthalamic nucleus). They modulate motor control, helping initiate and smooth voluntary movement. Damage produces movement disorders like Parkinson disease and Huntington disease.

Diencephalon 3 questions

08 DOK 1 · Recall

Where is the thalamus, and what does it do?

Reveal answer

A pair of egg-shaped masses of gray matter on either side of the third ventricle. It is the major relay station: almost all sensory input (except smell) passes through the thalamus on its way to the cortex, and many motor pathways relay here as well.

09 DOK 1 · Recall

Where is the hypothalamus, and what does it do?

Reveal answer

Below the thalamus, forming the floor and walls of the third ventricle. It is the master controller of homeostasis: regulates body temperature, hunger, thirst, sleep, autonomic function, and links the nervous and endocrine systems through the pituitary.

10 DOK 1 · Recall

Where is the pineal gland in relation to the diencephalon?

Reveal answer

It projects from the posterior end of the diencephalon (epithalamus), above the midbrain. It secretes melatonin.

Cerebellum and ventricles 3 questions

11 DOK 1 · Recall

Where is the cerebellum, and what does it do?

Reveal answer

Inferior to the occipital lobes, posterior to the brainstem, in the posterior cranial fossa. It coordinates voluntary movement, posture, and balance, and contributes to motor learning. It does not initiate movement but smooths and tunes it.

12 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the four ventricles of the brain.

Reveal answer

Two lateral ventricles (one in each cerebral hemisphere), the third ventricle (midline between the two thalami), and the fourth ventricle (between the brainstem and cerebellum, continuous below with the central canal of the spinal cord).

13 DOK 2 · Apply

Trace the flow of cerebrospinal fluid through the ventricles.

Reveal answer

CSF is made by choroid plexus in each ventricle, mostly in the lateral ventricles. From the lateral ventricles it passes through the interventricular foramina (of Monro) into the third ventricle, then through the cerebral aqueduct into the fourth ventricle. From the fourth ventricle it exits through three openings (two lateral apertures and one median) into the subarachnoid space, where it bathes the brain and spinal cord. It is reabsorbed into the dural venous sinuses through arachnoid granulations.
