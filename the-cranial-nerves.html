The Cranial Nerves

Sudden right-sided facial droop including the forehead. A tear that won't close the eye. One peripheral nerve in trouble.

Patient case

Anna is 35. She woke this morning with the right side of her face drooping. She cannot close her right eye fully, cannot raise her right eyebrow, cannot smile on the right. Food tastes different on the front of her tongue. No weakness in her arm or leg; no slurred speech otherwise; no other deficits.

On exam she has complete right-sided facial paralysis INCLUDING the forehead, mild hyperacusis on the right (sounds louder), reduced taste on the anterior two-thirds of the right tongue. The diagnosis is Bell's palsy — an idiopathic peripheral palsy of cranial nerve VII (often viral-mediated inflammation).

The 12 cranial nerves come directly off the brain or brainstem (not the spinal cord) and serve the structures of the head and neck (plus some thoracic/abdominal viscera via CN X). Anna's palsy is one nerve in isolation, but every finding maps to a specific branch of CN VII.

Guiding question What do each of the 12 cranial nerves do, and how does a single peripheral CN VII lesion produce ipsilateral facial paralysis, loss of taste, and hyperacusis at the same time?

3 CN VII detail

Why Anna has the combination of findings she has

Cranial nerve VII has multiple branches before it reaches the face. Damage at different points produces different combinations:

Motor branches to facial musclesFive branches at the parotid (temporal, zygomatic, buccal, mandibular, cervical). Innervate all muscles of facial expression.
Chorda tympaniCarries taste from anterior 2/3 of tongue + parasympathetic to submandibular and sublingual glands.
Greater petrosal nerveParasympathetic to lacrimal gland.
Nerve to stapediusInnervates the stapedius muscle that dampens loud sounds. Loss = hyperacusis (sounds too loud).
4 UMN vs LMN facial weakness

The forehead is the key

Why forehead involvement = peripheral (LMN) lesion. The forehead motor cortex sends fibers to BOTH facial nerve nuclei (bilateral cortical input). The lower face is controlled by only the CONTRALATERAL cortex. A cortical (UMN) stroke produces lower-face weakness on the contralateral side but spares the forehead (because the other hemisphere still drives forehead). A peripheral nerve (LMN) lesion takes out everything that nerve does — including the forehead. Anna can't wrinkle her right forehead → peripheral lesion, not a stroke.
5 Other classic CN findings

Patterns worth memorizing

CN III palsy"Down and out" eye, ptosis, dilated pupil. Causes: posterior communicating artery aneurysm, diabetic microvascular ischemia.
CN VI palsyCannot abduct eye → horizontal diplopia. Can be from raised intracranial pressure (long course).
Trigeminal neuralgiaLightning-like facial pain in V2 or V3 distribution. Often vascular compression.
Acoustic neuromaTumor on CN VIII at the cerebellopontine angle. Hearing loss + tinnitus + balance issues. Can compress CN VII (facial weakness) and CN V (facial numbness).
6 Functional grouping

Three special-sense, six motor, three mixed

Special senses: I (smell), II (vision), VIII (hearing/balance). Pure motor: III, IV, VI, XI, XII. Plus IX (mostly mixed but sometimes considered motor-heavy). Mixed (sensory + motor): V, VII, IX, X.

Returning to Anna

One nerve, multiple branches, all the findings

Anna's case is a peripheral CN VII palsy — distinguished from a stroke by the forehead involvement.

"Why forehead is involved"
Peripheral lesion of CN VII. Takes out the entire nerve including the temporal branch to forehead muscles. UMN stroke would spare forehead.
"Eye won't close"
CN VII innervates orbicularis oculi (eye closure). Loss → lagophthalmos (can't close eye) → corneal exposure → ulceration risk. Critical to lubricate and tape closed at night.
"Loss of taste anterior 2/3 of tongue"
Chorda tympani branch of CN VII carries taste from anterior 2/3. Posterior 1/3 is CN IX (unaffected).
"Hyperacusis"
Loss of CN VII branch to stapedius. Stapedius normally dampens loud sounds by tensing the ossicles. Without it, sounds feel uncomfortably loud.
"Likely cause"
Bell's palsy: idiopathic, often associated with herpes simplex reactivation in the geniculate ganglion. Inflammation swells the nerve in the tight bony facial canal, leading to compressive demyelination.
"Prognosis"
~70% recover fully without treatment; oral corticosteroids started within 72 hours improve outcomes. Most recovery happens within 3-6 months. Some have residual weakness.

Forehead, taste, stapedius, lacrimation. Anna's exam is a CN VII anatomy lesson.
