Joint classifications 4 questions

01 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the three structural classifications of joints and what tissue holds each one together.

Reveal answer

Fibrous joints are held together by dense connective tissue (collagen); no joint cavity. Cartilaginous joints are united by cartilage (hyaline or fibrocartilage); no joint cavity. Synovial joints have a joint cavity filled with synovial fluid, held together by a fibrous capsule and ligaments.

02 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the three functional classifications of joints and how much movement each allows.

Reveal answer

Synarthroses are immovable. Amphiarthroses are slightly movable. Diarthroses are freely movable. Most synovial joints are diarthroses; most fibrous and cartilaginous joints are synarthroses or amphiarthroses.

03 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the three types of fibrous joints with an example of each.

Reveal answer

Sutures are short fibers between flat bones of the skull. Syndesmoses are longer fibers connecting two bones, like the distal tibiofibular joint or the interosseous membrane of the forearm. Gomphoses are peg-in-socket fibrous joints, the only example being a tooth in its socket.

04 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the two types of cartilaginous joints and give an example of each.

Reveal answer

Synchondroses are joined by hyaline cartilage; the epiphyseal plate of a growing bone and the costal cartilages of the first rib are examples. Symphyses are joined by fibrocartilage and have a small amount of give; the intervertebral discs and the pubic symphysis are examples.

Synovial joint anatomy 6 questions

05 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the parts of a typical synovial joint.

Reveal answer

Articular cartilage (hyaline) on the bone ends; joint (synovial) cavity between them; articular capsule with an outer fibrous layer and inner synovial membrane; synovial fluid in the cavity; reinforcing ligaments; and often accessory structures such as menisci, bursae, and tendon sheaths.

06 DOK 1 · Recall

What is synovial fluid, what produces it, and what does it do?

Reveal answer

Synovial fluid is a viscous, egg-white-like fluid produced by the synovial membrane. It lubricates the joint, nourishes the avascular articular cartilage, and absorbs shock.

07 DOK 1 · Recall

What is articular cartilage, what kind of tissue is it, and why does joint damage to it heal poorly?

Reveal answer

Articular cartilage is hyaline cartilage that covers the ends of bones at synovial joints. Because cartilage is avascular, nutrients arrive only by diffusion from synovial fluid. Damage heals slowly and often incompletely, which is why osteoarthritis progresses over years.

08 DOK 1 · Recall

What are menisci, where are they found, and what do they do?

Reveal answer

Menisci are crescent-shaped fibrocartilage pads inside certain synovial joints. The knee has medial and lateral menisci. They improve the fit between articulating bones, cushion impact, and stabilize the joint.

09 DOK 1 · Recall

What is a bursa, where is it found, and what does it do?

Reveal answer

A bursa is a flattened fibrous sac lined with synovial membrane and filled with synovial fluid. Bursae sit where ligaments, muscles, skin, or tendons rub against bone. They reduce friction during movement. Common sites include the shoulder, elbow, hip, and knee.

10 DOK 1 · Recall

What is a tendon sheath, and how is it like a bursa?

Reveal answer

A tendon sheath is an elongated bursa wrapped around a tendon, especially where tendons cross joints in narrow channels (wrist, ankle, fingers). It allows the tendon to slide with minimal friction.

Six types of synovial joints 6 questions

11 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe a plane (gliding) joint and give one example.

Reveal answer

A plane joint has flat or only slightly curved articular surfaces that slide across each other. Movement is non-axial (no axis of rotation), only side-to-side or back-and-forth gliding. Examples: intercarpal and intertarsal joints, acromioclavicular joint.

12 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe a hinge joint and give two examples.

Reveal answer

A hinge joint has a convex surface that fits into a concave one, allowing motion in a single plane (flexion and extension only). Uniaxial. Examples: elbow (humerus-ulna), interphalangeal joints, the knee (mostly hinge, with limited rotation).

13 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe a pivot joint and give two examples.

Reveal answer

A pivot joint has a rounded bone rotating within a ring formed by a second bone and a ligament. Uniaxial; allows rotation only. Examples: the proximal radioulnar joint (rotation of the radius in pronation/supination) and the atlantoaxial joint (rotation of the head).

14 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe a condyloid (ellipsoid) joint and give an example.

Reveal answer

A condyloid joint has an oval convex surface fitting into an oval concave one. Biaxial: allows flexion/extension and abduction/adduction, but not rotation. Examples: metacarpophalangeal (knuckle) joints, the wrist (radiocarpal) joint.

15 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe a saddle joint and give the classic example.

Reveal answer

A saddle joint has each surface shaped like a saddle, concave in one direction and convex in the other; the surfaces fit together at right angles. Biaxial with the added benefit of opposition. Classic example: the carpometacarpal joint of the thumb, which gives the thumb its opposable motion.

16 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe a ball-and-socket joint and give the two main examples.

Reveal answer

A ball-and-socket joint has a spherical head fitting into a cup-shaped socket. Multiaxial: flexion/extension, abduction/adduction, rotation, and circumduction. Examples: the shoulder (glenohumeral) joint and the hip joint.

Joint movements 8 questions

17 DOK 1 · Recall

Define flexion and extension, and give an example.

Reveal answer

Flexion decreases the angle between two bones at a joint (bending). Extension increases the angle (straightening). Bending the elbow is flexion; straightening it is extension. Hyperextension is extension beyond anatomical position.

18 DOK 1 · Recall

Define abduction and adduction.

Reveal answer

Abduction moves a limb away from the midline of the body. Adduction moves it toward the midline. Lifting your arm out to the side is shoulder abduction; bringing it back is adduction.

19 DOK 1 · Recall

Define medial and lateral rotation.

Reveal answer

Medial (internal) rotation turns the anterior surface of a limb toward the midline. Lateral (external) rotation turns it away from the midline. The shoulder and hip both rotate medially and laterally.

20 DOK 1 · Recall

Define circumduction.

Reveal answer

Circumduction is the cone-shaped movement of a limb in which the distal end traces a circle while the proximal end stays fixed. It combines flexion, abduction, extension, and adduction in sequence. Possible at ball-and-socket and condyloid joints.

21 DOK 1 · Recall

Define pronation and supination of the forearm.

Reveal answer

Supination turns the forearm so the palm faces anteriorly (anatomical position). Pronation turns the forearm so the palm faces posteriorly. The radius rotates across the ulna at the proximal and distal radioulnar joints.

22 DOK 1 · Recall

Define dorsiflexion and plantar flexion of the ankle.

Reveal answer

Dorsiflexion lifts the foot at the ankle so the toes move toward the shin. Plantar flexion presses the foot down so the toes point away from the shin (as in standing on tiptoe).

23 DOK 1 · Recall

Define inversion and eversion of the foot.

Reveal answer

Inversion turns the sole medially (sole faces inward). Eversion turns the sole laterally (sole faces outward). Common in foot sprains, where the ankle is forced into excess inversion.

24 DOK 1 · Recall

Define protraction and retraction.

Reveal answer

Protraction moves a body part anteriorly in the horizontal plane (jutting the jaw forward, rounding the shoulders). Retraction moves it posteriorly (pulling the jaw back, squaring the shoulders).

Major joints 5 questions

25 DOK 2 · Apply

The shoulder is the most mobile joint in the body. What anatomical features make it so mobile, and what is the trade-off?

Reveal answer

The shoulder is a ball-and-socket joint where the large humeral head sits in the shallow glenoid cavity of the scapula. The shallow socket lets the head move in every direction (multiaxial). The trade-off is stability: the shoulder is the most commonly dislocated major joint. Surrounding muscles (rotator cuff) and the joint capsule have to do most of the stabilizing work that bone shape does at the hip.

26 DOK 2 · Apply

Compare the hip joint with the shoulder joint by structure and the trade-off between motion and stability.

Reveal answer

The hip is also a ball-and-socket joint, but the acetabulum is deep and largely surrounds the femoral head, with a fibrocartilage acetabular labrum adding more depth. The hip therefore is more stable than the shoulder but less mobile. The trade-off is reversed.

27 DOK 2 · Apply

The knee is described as a modified hinge joint. Why 'modified', and what major intra-articular structures stabilize it?

Reveal answer

The knee mostly flexes and extends like a hinge, but it also allows a small amount of rotation when flexed. The medial and lateral menisci improve the fit between the convex femoral condyles and the relatively flat tibial plateau. The anterior and posterior cruciate ligaments (ACL, PCL) cross inside the joint and limit anterior/posterior tibial slide. The medial and lateral collateral ligaments (MCL, LCL) limit side-to-side motion.

28 DOK 1 · Recall

What kind of joint is the elbow, and what motions does it allow?

Reveal answer

The elbow is a hinge joint between the trochlea of the humerus and the trochlear notch of the ulna. It allows flexion and extension only. The associated proximal radioulnar joint is a pivot, allowing pronation and supination of the forearm.

29 DOK 1 · Recall

What kind of joint is the temporomandibular joint, and what unusual feature does it have?

Reveal answer

The TMJ is a synovial joint between the mandible and the temporal bone. It combines hinge and gliding motions and contains an articular disc of fibrocartilage that divides the joint into two compartments. The disc enables the combined motion needed for chewing.

Clinical reasoning 3 questions

30 DOK 3 · Reason

A sprain is an injury to a ligament. Using what you know about ligament structure, predict why severe sprains heal slowly.

Reveal answer

Ligaments are dense regular connective tissue with parallel collagen fibers and limited blood supply. Healing depends on slow collagen turnover, and the original fiber orientation is hard to reproduce. The healed tissue may be weaker than the original, which is why severe ligament tears (e.g., ACL) often need surgical reconstruction rather than waiting on natural repair.

31 DOK 3 · Reason

The anterior shoulder is the most common dislocation site in the body. Using the joint's anatomy, explain why anterior dislocations are common.

Reveal answer

The glenoid is shallow, and the anterior capsule and ligaments are the thinnest part of the joint capsule. A forceful posterior-to-anterior push (such as falling on an outstretched arm) drives the humeral head out anteriorly through the weak spot, and the shallow socket cannot hold it in. The same anatomy that gives the shoulder its motion lets it dislocate.

32 DOK 3 · Reason

Osteoarthritis (OA) and rheumatoid arthritis (RA) both damage synovial joints. Compare what each one attacks and predict how the joint cavity looks in each.

Reveal answer

OA is wear-and-tear loss of articular cartilage; the cartilage thins and the underlying bone responds with bony spurs and remodeling. The joint cavity narrows and bone-on-bone contact develops. RA is autoimmune destruction targeting the synovial membrane, which proliferates into an inflamed pannus that erodes cartilage and bone. In RA the synovial membrane and the immune system drive the damage; in OA the cartilage is the primary problem.
