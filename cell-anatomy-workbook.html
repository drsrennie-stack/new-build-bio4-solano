The generalized cell 2 questions

01 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the three main regions of a generalized cell and what each contains.

Reveal answer

The plasma membrane is the boundary of the cell. The cytoplasm is the contents between the membrane and the nucleus; it includes the cytosol (fluid) and the organelles. The nucleus is the central compartment that houses the DNA.

02 DOK 1 · Recall

Roughly how big is a typical human cell, and what controls the upper limit of cell size?

Reveal answer

Most human cells are about 10 to 30 micrometers across; some specialized cells are much larger (an ovum is about 100 micrometers; a motor neuron can extend a meter as its axon). Surface-to-volume ratio limits practical cell size: as a cell gets larger, its volume grows faster than its surface area, so the membrane becomes inadequate for the cell's metabolic exchange needs.

The plasma membrane 7 questions

03 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the plasma membrane using the fluid mosaic model.

Reveal answer

The plasma membrane is a phospholipid bilayer in which protein molecules are embedded. The phospholipids form the fluid framework: hydrophilic phosphate heads face the watery environments inside and outside the cell, hydrophobic fatty acid tails face inward toward each other. Proteins float in this bilayer and can move within it (fluid), and they occur in characteristic patterns across the surface (mosaic).

04 DOK 1 · Recall

What does a phospholipid look like, and which end faces the cytoplasm and the extracellular fluid?

Reveal answer

A phospholipid has a hydrophilic phosphate head and two hydrophobic fatty acid tails. In the bilayer, the heads face outward, contacting the watery extracellular fluid on one side and the cytoplasm on the other. The fatty acid tails point inward, away from water, forming the membrane's hydrophobic interior.

05 DOK 1 · Recall

What is cholesterol doing in the plasma membrane?

Reveal answer

Cholesterol molecules sit interspersed among the phospholipids. They stabilize membrane fluidity over a range of temperatures, making the membrane less fluid when it would otherwise be too fluid and less rigid when it would otherwise be too rigid.

06 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare integral and peripheral membrane proteins by location and typical role.

Reveal answer

Integral (transmembrane) proteins span the bilayer all the way through, with hydrophobic regions in the interior and hydrophilic regions exposed to the watery environments on each side. They typically function as transporters, channels, receptors, or attachment points. Peripheral proteins sit on the inner or outer surface of the membrane without crossing it. They often anchor the cytoskeleton, link to enzymes, or work with integral proteins.

07 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the glycocalyx, what is it made of, and what does it do?

Reveal answer

The glycocalyx is a sugar-rich coating on the outer surface of the plasma membrane, made of carbohydrate chains attached to membrane proteins (glycoproteins) and lipids (glycolipids). It functions in cell-to-cell recognition, cell adhesion, protection of the membrane, and the chemical identity that lets the immune system tell self from non-self.

08 DOK 1 · Recall

List the major functions of the plasma membrane.

Reveal answer

Selective barrier (controls what enters and leaves the cell), transport (active and passive movement of substances), communication (receptors for hormones and signals), cell-to-cell recognition (via glycocalyx and surface proteins), attachment (to neighboring cells and to the basement membrane), and shape and support (via membrane-cytoskeleton attachments).

09 DOK 1 · Recall

Name three surface specializations of the plasma membrane and what each does.

Reveal answer

Microvilli are finger-like projections that increase surface area for absorption. Cilia are hair-like motile projections that sweep material across the surface. The glycocalyx is the sugar coat that handles recognition and protection. Membrane junctions (tight, adherens, desmosomes, gap, hemidesmosomes) are also surface specializations that connect the cell to its neighbors and to the extracellular matrix.

The nucleus 5 questions

10 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the nucleus, what does it contain, and what does it do for the cell?

Reveal answer

The nucleus is the largest organelle, usually centrally located. It contains the cell's DNA organized into chromatin, plus one or more nucleoli. It is the control center of the cell: it stores the genetic instructions, transcribes them into RNA, and directs all of the cell's activities through the proteins those instructions produce.

11 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the nuclear envelope.

Reveal answer

The nuclear envelope is a double membrane that surrounds the nucleus and separates it from the cytoplasm. It is studded with nuclear pores. The outer membrane is continuous with the rough endoplasmic reticulum, so the nuclear envelope and the ER are part of the same membrane system.

12 DOK 1 · Recall

What are nuclear pores and what passes through them?

Reveal answer

Nuclear pores are protein-lined channels through the nuclear envelope. They control the traffic of large molecules between the nucleus and the cytoplasm: RNA and ribosomal subunits exit, proteins (like transcription factors) enter. Small molecules and ions pass freely.

13 DOK 1 · Recall

What is chromatin, what is it made of, and how does it differ from a chromosome?

Reveal answer

Chromatin is the threadlike form of the cell's genetic material, made of DNA wrapped around histone proteins. It is what the genetic material looks like during most of the cell's life. A chromosome is the highly condensed, visibly distinct form chromatin takes during cell division. Same material; different state of compaction.

14 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the nucleolus, and what is it doing?

Reveal answer

The nucleolus is a dense, dark-staining region inside the nucleus, made of clustered loops of DNA, RNA, and proteins. It is where ribosomal RNA is synthesized and where ribosomal subunits are assembled before they leave the nucleus.

Membranous organelles 8 questions

15 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe rough endoplasmic reticulum and what it does.

Reveal answer

Rough endoplasmic reticulum (rER) is a system of flattened, interconnected membrane sacs (cisternae) in the cytoplasm. Its surface is studded with ribosomes, which is what makes it 'rough'. It synthesizes proteins that will be secreted from the cell, inserted into membranes, or sent to certain organelles. Ribosomes on the rER feed newly made proteins into the lumen for folding and modification.

16 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe smooth endoplasmic reticulum and what it does.

Reveal answer

Smooth endoplasmic reticulum (sER) is a system of tubular membranes with no ribosomes on its surface. It synthesizes lipids (cholesterol, phospholipids, steroids), detoxifies drugs and toxins (especially in liver cells), and stores and releases calcium ions. In muscle cells, a specialized form (the sarcoplasmic reticulum) stores the calcium that triggers contraction.

17 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the Golgi apparatus and what it does.

Reveal answer

The Golgi apparatus is a stack of flattened membrane sacs (cisternae) with a defined cis face (closer to the rER, receives material) and trans face (away from the rER, dispatches material). It modifies, sorts, and packages proteins and lipids that arrive in vesicles from the rER, and ships them to their final destinations in transport vesicles.

18 DOK 1 · Recall

What are lysosomes, what do they contain, and what do they do?

Reveal answer

Lysosomes are membrane-bound vesicles filled with digestive (hydrolytic) enzymes. They are the cell's recycling and demolition center: they digest worn-out organelles, materials taken in by the cell, and pathogens that have been engulfed. Their interior is acidic, which keeps the enzymes active there and quiet if accidentally released.

19 DOK 1 · Recall

What are peroxisomes, and what do they do?

Reveal answer

Peroxisomes are small membrane-bound vesicles that contain oxidase enzymes. They break down fatty acids and detoxify harmful substances (alcohol, hydrogen peroxide). Hydrogen peroxide is a toxic by-product; the peroxisome's own catalase enzyme breaks it down to water before it can do damage.

20 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the structure of a mitochondrion and its basic function.

Reveal answer

A mitochondrion is a sausage-shaped organelle with two membranes. The outer membrane is smooth and surrounds the whole organelle. The inner membrane is folded inward into shelves called cristae, which increase surface area. The interior compartment, the matrix, contains its own DNA and ribosomes. Mitochondria produce most of the cell's ATP through cellular respiration; the cristae carry the proteins that do that work.

21 DOK 2 · Apply

Mitochondria carry their own DNA and ribosomes. What does that tell you about their evolutionary origin, and why does it matter for inheritance?

Reveal answer

It supports the endosymbiotic theory: mitochondria are thought to descend from once-free-living bacteria engulfed by an ancestral cell. Practically, the consequence is that mitochondrial DNA is inherited from the mother only (the sperm contributes essentially no mitochondria), so mitochondrial diseases follow a maternal inheritance pattern.

22 DOK 1 · Recall

What is a transport vesicle, and what does it do?

Reveal answer

A transport vesicle is a small membrane-bound sac that buds from one organelle, travels through the cytoplasm, and fuses with another organelle or the plasma membrane. Vesicles move products between the rER, the Golgi, the cell surface, and back. Endocytic vesicles bring material into the cell; secretory vesicles release material outside.

Non-membranous organelles and the cytoskeleton 4 questions

23 DOK 1 · Recall

What are ribosomes, what are they made of, and where do they work?

Reveal answer

Ribosomes are small particles made of ribosomal RNA and ribosomal proteins. They are the sites of protein synthesis. Some ribosomes are free in the cytoplasm (making proteins that stay inside the cell). Others are bound to rough ER (making proteins that will be secreted, inserted into membranes, or sent to certain organelles).

24 DOK 1 · Recall

What are centrioles, where are they found, and what do they do?

Reveal answer

Centrioles are paired short cylinders made of nine triplets of microtubules in a 9+0 ring. They sit near the nucleus in a region called the centrosome. They organize the microtubules of the cytoskeleton, build the mitotic spindle during cell division, and serve as basal bodies that anchor cilia and flagella.

25 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the three components of the cytoskeleton and what each one does.

Reveal answer

Microfilaments are made of actin and provide cell shape, support, and the contractile machinery for movement and cell division. Intermediate filaments (e.g., keratin in epithelia, neurofilaments in neurons) provide tensile strength and resist mechanical stress. Microtubules are hollow tubes of tubulin that act as the cell's tracks for organelle movement, form the mitotic spindle, and build cilia and flagella.

26 DOK 2 · Compare

Compare microfilaments, intermediate filaments, and microtubules by their protein, their diameter, and the kinds of jobs they handle.

Reveal answer

Microfilaments: actin, about 7 nm, thinnest. They handle shape, surface movement, and contraction. Intermediate filaments: a family of proteins (keratin, vimentin, neurofilament, lamin, etc.), about 10 nm. They handle tensile strength and stress resistance. Microtubules: tubulin, about 25 nm, thickest. They handle intracellular transport, cell division, and cilia/flagella.

The secretory pathway 3 questions

27 DOK 2 · Apply

Trace the path of a secreted protein from gene to outside the cell, naming each structure it passes through.

Reveal answer

The gene is transcribed in the nucleus into mRNA, which exits through a nuclear pore. The mRNA binds a ribosome that attaches to the rough ER, and the protein is synthesized into the rER lumen. The rER buds off a transport vesicle that carries the protein to the cis face of the Golgi. The Golgi modifies and sorts the protein, then dispatches it in a secretory vesicle from the trans face. The secretory vesicle travels to the plasma membrane and fuses with it, releasing the protein outside the cell (exocytosis).

28 DOK 1 · Recall

What is endocytosis, and name two forms of it.

Reveal answer

Endocytosis is the process by which a cell takes in material by enveloping it with a piece of plasma membrane, which pinches off as a vesicle inside the cell. Phagocytosis is endocytosis of large solid particles (e.g., bacteria engulfed by macrophages). Pinocytosis is endocytosis of fluid and small dissolved molecules. Receptor-mediated endocytosis is selective uptake of specific molecules through their membrane receptors.

29 DOK 1 · Recall

What is exocytosis, and what kinds of materials does the cell typically release this way?

Reveal answer

Exocytosis is the fusion of an internal vesicle with the plasma membrane, releasing the vesicle's contents to the outside of the cell. Cells use exocytosis to secrete hormones, digestive enzymes, neurotransmitters, mucus, and waste.

Form follows function 4 questions

30 DOK 2 · Apply

A cell is packed with rough endoplasmic reticulum, ribosomes, and a prominent Golgi apparatus. Predict what kind of cell it is.

Reveal answer

A protein-secreting cell. Heavy rough ER and ribosomes mean lots of protein synthesis; a prominent Golgi means heavy modification, sorting, and packaging. Examples include pancreatic acinar cells (digestive enzymes), plasma cells (antibodies), and goblet cells (mucus, which is a glycoprotein).

31 DOK 2 · Apply

A cell is packed with mitochondria. What kind of work do you predict that cell does for a living?

Reveal answer

Energy-intensive work. Lots of mitochondria means lots of ATP demand. Examples: cardiac muscle cells (continuous contraction), skeletal muscle cells (especially slow-twitch endurance fibers), and the cells of the kidney tubules (which spend ATP to pump ions against gradients).

32 DOK 2 · Apply

A liver cell is packed with smooth endoplasmic reticulum. Predict why.

Reveal answer

The liver detoxifies drugs, alcohol, and other harmful substances, which is one of the smooth ER's specialties. It also makes large amounts of lipids, including the cholesterol used in bile. Both demands explain the abundant smooth ER.

33 DOK 3 · Reason

In Tay-Sachs disease, a single lysosomal enzyme is missing, and a specific lipid accumulates inside neurons. Using what you know about lysosomes, predict why the disease damages neurons.

Reveal answer

Lysosomes are the cell's recycling center; their enzymes break down old or imported material. When one enzyme is missing, the substrate it normally breaks down piles up inside lysosomes. In neurons that substrate (a ganglioside) accumulates fast because neurons turn over a lot of membrane lipid. The swollen lysosomes interfere with neuron function and eventually kill the cells, producing the progressive neurologic decline characteristic of the disease.
