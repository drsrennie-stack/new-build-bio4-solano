Articulations & Joints

A joint is where bones meet. Joints trade mobility for stability: some are locked solid for protection, others move freely. We classify them, build a synovial joint, name the movements and the six joint types, then study the shoulder and knee. Dr. Sharilyn Rennie

Part 1

Classifying Joints

Every joint is named two ways at once: by its structure (what holds it) and by its function (how much it moves).

Part 1 · Compare & contrast

Two ways to classify a joint

Joints

Structure asks what binds the bones and whether a synovial cavity is present. Function asks how much the joint moves. The two line up closely.

Part 2

Synovial Joints

The freely movable joints: a fluid-filled cavity, an articular capsule, and a set of accessory parts that fine-tune fit and reduce friction.

Part 2 · Structure

Inside a synovial joint

Joints
  • Articular cartilage: hyaline cartilage capping the bone ends; it reduces friction and absorbs shock but does not bind the bones, and is fed by synovial fluid.
  • Synovial cavity: the space holding synovial fluid, which lubricates and nourishes.
  • Articular capsule: an outer fibrous membrane (dense irregular tissue continuous with the periosteum; thickened into ligaments) and an inner synovial membrane that makes the fluid.

Part 2 · Accessory parts

Accessory structures, nerves, and vessels

Joints
  • Ligaments: extracapsular (outside the capsule, like the knee collaterals) and intracapsular (inside but wrapped out of the cavity, like the cruciates).
  • Articular discs (menisci): fibrocartilage pads that improve fit, absorb shock, and spread weight and fluid. A labrum is a fibrocartilage rim that deepens a ball-and-socket (shoulder, hip).
  • Bursae: fluid sacs that cut friction between moving parts; tendon sheaths are tube-shaped bursae wrapping tendons in tight spaces.
  • Supply: the same nerves that move the joint carry its pain; vessels feed the capsule and ligaments, while the cartilage is fed by synovial fluid.

Part 2 · Movements

The movements at synovial joints

Joints
  • Gliding: flat surfaces slide with no change in angle.
  • Angular: flexion (decreases the angle), extension (increases it), hyperextension (past anatomical position), abduction (away from midline), adduction (toward midline), and circumduction (a cone).
  • Rotation: turning around the long axis (medial and lateral rotation).
  • Special: supination and pronation of the forearm; dorsiflexion and plantarflexion of the foot; inversion and eversion; elevation and depression; protraction and retraction; opposition of the thumb.

Part 2 · Range of motion

What limits range of motion

Joints

Range of motion is the arc, in degrees, a joint allows. Several factors set the limit:

  • Bone shape and the fit of the surfaces.
  • Ligament tension and muscle arrangement, which restrain and guide the bones.
  • Contact of soft parts meeting each other.
  • Hormones (relaxin loosens the pubic symphysis late in pregnancy) and disuse (a joint long unused stiffens).

Part 3

Two Joints and Their Troubles

The shoulder trades stability for mobility; the knee is the largest and most often injured. Then the common joint disorders.

Part 3 · Shoulder

The shoulder (glenohumeral) joint

Joints
  • A ball-and-socket joint: the humeral head in the shallow glenoid cavity, deepened slightly by a labrum.
  • The most freely movable joint, and so the least stable and most often dislocated.
  • Its ligaments add little strength; stability comes mostly from the rotator cuff muscles, the SITS: supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, subscapularis, which hold the head in the socket.

Part 3 · Knee

The knee (tibiofemoral) joint

Joints
  • A modified hinge, the largest and most complex joint, and the most often injured. It is three joints in one cavity (two tibiofemoral and one patellofemoral).
  • Anterior support: the patellar ligament (quadriceps tendon to the tibial tuberosity) and the patellar retinacula.
  • The collateral and cruciate ligaments

Part 3 · Knee injuries

Common knee injuries

Clinical
  • Unhappy triad: a lateral blow to a planted knee tears the tibial collateral ligament, the medial meniscus, and the ACL at once.
  • ACL tears are three to six times more common in women (narrower intercondylar space, wider pelvis and knee angle, looser ligaments, less protective muscle).
  • Swelling: immediate is blood from torn vessels; delayed is excess synovial fluid. First aid follows PRICE: protection, rest, ice, compression, elevation.

Part 3 · Disorders

Common joint disorders

Clinical
  • Bursitis: inflamed bursa from overuse, infection, or arthritis. Torn meniscus: knee-cartilage tear, repaired by arthroscopy.
  • Arthritis (swollen, stiff, painful joints) is the leading cause of disability after 65:
  • Osteoarthritis: degenerative wear of cartilage in weight-bearing joints.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis: an autoimmune attack on the joint linings, usually symmetric.
  • Gouty arthritis: sodium urate crystals deposit in a joint (often the great toe).

Part 4 · Recall (DOK 1)

Name the joint type

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 1 · Recall

The hip and shoulder allow movement around three axes. What type of synovial joint are they?

Answer

Ball-and-socket. A ball in a cup gives triaxial movement, the widest range of any joint.

Part 4 · Skill / concept (DOK 2)

Apply the structure

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 2 · Skill / concept

Why is the shoulder so easily dislocated compared with the hip?

Answer

A shallow socket and weak ligaments. The shoulder relies on the rotator cuff for stability, so it is mobile but easily dislocated.

Part 4 · Strategic reasoning (DOK 3)

Reason it through

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 3 · Strategic reasoning

A soccer player is hit on the outside of a planted knee and tears three structures at once. Which three, and what is this called?

Answer

The unhappy triad: a lateral blow tears the tibial collateral ligament, the medial meniscus, and the ACL together.

Wrap-up

Key takeaways

Summary
  • Classify joints by structure (fibrous, cartilaginous, synovial) and by function (syn-, amphi-, diarthrosis).
  • A synovial joint has articular cartilage, a fluid-filled cavity, and a capsule; accessory parts (menisci, labrum, bursae) fine-tune it.
  • The six synovial types run from plane (gliding) to ball-and-socket (triaxial).
  • The shoulder trades stability for mobility and leans on the rotator cuff (SITS).
  • The knee is stabilized by collateral and cruciate ligaments; the unhappy triad and ACL tears are classic injuries.
  • Disorders: bursitis, torn meniscus, and arthritis (osteo-, rheumatoid, gouty).
