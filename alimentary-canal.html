The Alimentary Canal, an Overview

The digestive system has two parts: the alimentary canal, the long tube food passes through, and the accessory organs that help from the side. This page covers the canal.

  • Digestive systemthe organs that break food down, absorb nutrients, and eliminate wastes
  • Alimentary canalthe continuous muscular tube from the mouth to the anus, also called the gastrointestinal or GI tract
  • Accessory organsthe teeth, tongue, salivary glands, liver, gallbladder, and pancreas, which assist digestion without being part of the tube; covered on the next page
  • Lumenthe open channel of the canal; its contents are technically outside the body until they are absorbed
  • Peritoneumthe serous membrane that lines the abdominal cavity and drapes the digestive organs

The Wall of the Alimentary Canal

From the esophagus to the anus, the wall of the canal is built in the same four layers, the tunics. Compare them from the lumen outward.

TunicPositionTissueRole

Submucosajust outside the mucosadense connective tissue with blood vessels, nerves, and glandssupplies the mucosa and carries a nerve plexus

The Pathway of Food

The alimentary canal is one continuous tube. Follow a mouthful of food through its organs in order.

  1. Mouthfood enters, is chewed, and is mixed with saliva
  2. Pharynxthe throat; swallowed food passes through into the esophagus
  3. Esophagusa muscular tube that carries food down to the stomach
  4. Stomachfood is stored and churned into a paste
  5. Small intestinemost digestion and nearly all absorption of nutrients happen here
  6. Large intestinewater is absorbed and the remaining material is formed into feces
  7. Anusthe exit point, where feces are eliminated

The Mouth, Pharynx, and Esophagus

Digestion begins in the mouth. The pharynx and esophagus then carry the food to the stomach.

  • Oral cavitythe mouth, the space where digestion begins
  • Tonguethe muscular organ that moves and positions food and carries the taste receptors
  • Teeththe structures that cut and grind food, set in the alveolar processes of the jaws
  • Hard and soft palatethe bony front and muscular back of the roof of the mouth
  • Pharynxthe throat; the oropharynx and laryngopharynx route swallowed food toward the esophagus
  • Esophagusthe muscular tube that carries food from the pharynx to the stomach, running behind the trachea
  • Peristalsisthe wave of muscular contraction in the wall that pushes food along the canal
  • Lower esophageal sphincterthe ring of muscle where the esophagus joins the stomach, which closes to keep stomach contents from rising

The Stomach

The stomach is a muscular, J-shaped widening of the canal in the upper left abdomen. It stores a meal and churns it into a paste.

  • Stomachthe J-shaped organ that stores food and churns it into a paste
  • Cardiathe region surrounding the opening where the esophagus enters
  • Fundusthe dome-shaped region that bulges above and to the left of the cardia
  • Bodythe large central region of the stomach
  • Pylorusthe funnel-shaped region that narrows and empties into the small intestine
  • Pyloric sphincterthe ring of muscle that controls the exit from the stomach into the duodenum
  • Rugaethe folds of the stomach lining that flatten out as the stomach fills
  • Greater and lesser curvaturesthe long convex border and the short concave border of the stomach
  • Gastric pits and glandsthe pits in the lining that lead down to the glands which produce gastric juice

The Small and Large Intestines

The intestines are the longest stretch of the canal. The small intestine absorbs nutrients; the large intestine absorbs water and forms feces.

The small intestine

  • Small intestinethe longest part of the canal, where most digestion and nearly all absorption occur
  • Duodenumthe first and shortest part, which receives bile and pancreatic juice
  • Jejunumthe middle part, the main site of nutrient absorption
  • Ileumthe last part, which joins the large intestine at the ileocecal valve
  • Plicae circularesthe deep circular folds of the wall that slow the contents and add surface area
  • Villifinger-like projections of the mucosa that greatly increase the absorptive surface
  • Microvillithe brush border, tiny projections on each absorptive cell that add still more surface

The large intestine

  • Large intestinethe final part of the canal, which absorbs water and forms feces
  • Cecumthe pouch where the large intestine begins, just past the ileocecal valve
  • Colonthe main length, in four parts: ascending, transverse, descending, and sigmoid
  • Rectumthe straight final segment that stores feces before elimination
  • Anal canalthe short terminal segment, ending at the anus
  • Teniae colithree bands of longitudinal muscle that run the length of the colon
  • Haustrathe pocket-like sacs of the colon, formed because the teniae coli are shorter than the colon

Clinical tie-in

One continuous tube, examined end to end

Because the alimentary canal is a single open tube, a clinician can look inside it directly. An endoscope passed through the mouth reaches the esophagus and stomach; a colonoscope passed through the anus reaches the colon. Both travel the same lumen the food does.

Peristalsis moves food by muscular waves, not by gravity, which is why a person can swallow while lying down or even upside down. When the lower esophageal sphincter does not stay closed, stomach acid rises into the esophagus, the cause of reflux.

The huge absorptive surface of the small intestine comes from three nested features: plicae circulares, villi, and microvilli. In celiac disease the villi flatten, the surface shrinks, and absorption falls, showing how directly structure sets function here.
