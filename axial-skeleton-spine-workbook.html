The vertebral column 3 questions

01 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the regions of the vertebral column and the number of vertebrae in each.

Reveal answer

Cervical: 7 (C1-C7). Thoracic: 12 (T1-T12). Lumbar: 5 (L1-L5). Sacrum: 5 fused (one bone in the adult). Coccyx: 3-4 fused (one bone in the adult). Total: 26 separate bones in the adult, but 33 'vertebrae' counting the components that fused into sacrum and coccyx.

02 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the four normal curvatures of the spine and their direction (concave or convex anteriorly).

Reveal answer

Cervical curvature: concave posteriorly (convex anteriorly), develops after the infant lifts its head. Thoracic curvature: concave anteriorly, primary curvature present at birth. Lumbar curvature: convex anteriorly, develops when the child stands. Sacral curvature: concave anteriorly, primary curvature. The primary thoracic and sacral curvatures are present at birth; the cervical and lumbar curvatures develop with motor milestones.

03 DOK 1 · Recall

Name three abnormal curvatures of the spine.

Reveal answer

Kyphosis: exaggerated thoracic curvature (hunchback). Lordosis: exaggerated lumbar curvature (swayback). Scoliosis: lateral curvature (sideways deviation, often with rotation), usually in the thoracic or lumbar regions.

A typical vertebra and the regional types 5 questions

04 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the major parts of a typical vertebra.

Reveal answer

Body (anterior weight-bearing block), vertebral arch (posterior, formed by two pedicles and two laminae), vertebral foramen (the central hole in the arch through which the spinal cord passes), spinous process (single, posterior), transverse processes (paired, lateral), and superior and inferior articular processes (paired, for joining the vertebrae above and below).

05 DOK 1 · Recall

What distinguishes a typical cervical vertebra from other regions?

Reveal answer

Small body, large triangular vertebral foramen, transverse foramen in each transverse process (which carries the vertebral artery), and a short bifid spinous process (split at the tip). C3-C6 fit this pattern.

06 DOK 1 · Recall

What distinguishes a typical thoracic vertebra from other regions?

Reveal answer

Heart-shaped body with costal facets (or demifacets) for rib articulation, smaller circular vertebral foramen, long spinous process angled steeply downward, and costal facets on the transverse processes (for rib tubercles). Thoracic vertebrae are the only ones that articulate with ribs.

07 DOK 1 · Recall

What distinguishes a typical lumbar vertebra from other regions?

Reveal answer

Massive kidney-shaped body (built for weight-bearing), triangular vertebral foramen, short blunt spinous process oriented horizontally, and short stocky transverse processes. No transverse foramen; no costal facets.

08 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the sacrum, and what is the coccyx?

Reveal answer

The sacrum is a single triangular bone formed by the fusion of five vertebrae; it sits at the base of the spine and forms the posterior pelvis. The coccyx is a small bone formed by the fusion of three to four small terminal vertebrae; it is the vestigial tail.

The special cervical vertebrae 2 questions

09 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the atlas, and what makes it unusual?

Reveal answer

C1, the atlas, has no body and no spinous process; it is a ring of bone. It articulates superiorly with the occipital condyles of the skull. The atlanto-occipital joint allows nodding the head 'yes'.

10 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the axis, and what does its special projection do?

Reveal answer

C2, the axis, has a peg-like projection (the dens, or odontoid process) extending upward from its body into the ring of the atlas. The dens acts as the pivot around which the atlas (and the head) rotate for 'no' motion. The atlantoaxial joint is the classic pivot joint.

Intervertebral discs 3 questions

11 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the two parts of an intervertebral disc and what each is made of.

Reveal answer

Anulus fibrosus: the outer ring of fibrocartilage, with concentric layers of collagen running in alternating directions. Nucleus pulposus: the inner gelatinous core, derived from the embryonic notochord, mostly water and proteoglycan.

12 DOK 1 · Recall

What do intervertebral discs do?

Reveal answer

They sit between vertebral bodies as cushions. They absorb compressive forces, distribute load across the vertebral body surface, and allow limited bending and twisting between adjacent vertebrae.

13 DOK 3 · Reason

A 'slipped disc' is herniation of disc material posterolaterally. Using disc anatomy, predict what tissue is escaping and what it presses on.

Reveal answer

The nucleus pulposus pushes through a torn anulus fibrosus, usually posterolaterally where the anulus is thinner. The herniated material protrudes into the vertebral canal or intervertebral foramen and presses on a spinal nerve root, producing radicular pain (sciatica when L4-L5 or L5-S1 discs herniate). The location of the herniation predicts which nerve root is affected and therefore which dermatome and muscles are involved.

Ribs and sternum 3 questions

14 DOK 1 · Recall

How many pairs of ribs do humans have, and how are they classified anteriorly?

Reveal answer

12 pairs. True ribs (1-7) attach directly to the sternum via their own costal cartilages. False ribs (8-12) do not attach directly. Among false ribs, ribs 8-10 attach indirectly (their costal cartilages join the cartilage of rib 7), and ribs 11-12 are floating ribs that do not attach anteriorly at all.

15 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the major parts of a typical rib.

Reveal answer

Head (posterior, articulates with vertebral bodies), neck (just lateral to the head), tubercle (small bump where neck meets shaft, articulates with the transverse process of a vertebra), shaft (the long curved body), and the costal cartilage at the anterior end.

16 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the three parts of the sternum.

Reveal answer

Manubrium (superior portion, articulates with the clavicles and the first two ribs), body (long middle portion, articulates with ribs 2-7), xiphoid process (small inferior tip, attachment for some abdominal muscles). The manubrium-body junction is the sternal angle, a palpable landmark at the level of the second rib.
