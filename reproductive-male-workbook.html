Testes and scrotum 3 questions

01 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the scrotum, and why does it sit outside the body?

Reveal answer

The scrotum is a sac of skin and superficial fascia that houses the testes. It sits outside the abdomen because sperm production requires a temperature about 2-3 degrees Celsius below core body temperature. The dartos and cremaster muscles adjust scrotal position to regulate testicular temperature.

02 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the structure of a testis.

Reveal answer

Each testis is an ovoid organ covered by a tough fibrous capsule (tunica albuginea). Septa from the capsule divide the testis into lobules. Each lobule contains tightly coiled seminiferous tubules, where sperm are made. Tubules drain into the rete testis at the back of the testis, which empties into the efferent ductules that lead to the epididymis.

03 DOK 1 · Recall

Name two key cell types in or near the seminiferous tubules and what each does.

Reveal answer

Sertoli (sustentacular) cells: line the seminiferous tubules; support and nourish developing sperm; form the blood-testis barrier. Leydig (interstitial) cells: sit in the connective tissue between tubules; produce testosterone.

Duct system 3 questions

04 DOK 2 · Apply

Trace the path of a sperm from where it is made to where it leaves the body.

Reveal answer

Seminiferous tubule → rete testis → efferent ductules → epididymis → ductus (vas) deferens → ejaculatory duct → prostatic urethra → membranous urethra → spongy urethra → external urethral orifice.

05 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the epididymis, and what happens there?

Reveal answer

The epididymis is a long highly coiled tube on the posterior surface of each testis. Sperm complete their maturation here and gain motility. The epididymis also stores sperm until ejaculation.

06 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the ductus (vas) deferens.

Reveal answer

A muscular tube that ascends from the epididymis through the spermatic cord and inguinal canal into the abdomen, then loops over the bladder and joins the duct of the seminal vesicle to form the ejaculatory duct. Smooth muscle in its wall propels sperm during ejaculation. A vasectomy interrupts this duct.

Accessory glands 4 questions

07 DOK 1 · Recall

Where are the seminal vesicles, and what do they contribute?

Reveal answer

Paired glands behind the bladder. Their secretion (about 60 percent of semen volume) is a viscous yellowish fluid rich in fructose (energy for sperm), prostaglandins, and clotting proteins. The ducts join the ductus deferens to form the ejaculatory ducts.

08 DOK 1 · Recall

Where is the prostate, and what does it contribute?

Reveal answer

Inferior to the bladder, surrounding the prostatic urethra. Its secretion (about 30 percent of semen volume) is a milky slightly acidic fluid containing enzymes (PSA) that liquefy semen after ejaculation. Clinically important because it commonly enlarges with age and can compress the urethra.

09 DOK 1 · Recall

Where are the bulbourethral (Cowper's) glands, and what do they contribute?

Reveal answer

Small paired glands inferior to the prostate. They secrete a clear mucus that lubricates the urethra and neutralizes residual acidity from urine before ejaculation.

10 DOK 1 · Recall

What is semen made of, by rough composition?

Reveal answer

Sperm (about 1-5 percent of volume), seminal vesicle fluid (about 60 percent), prostatic fluid (about 30 percent), bulbourethral mucus (small fraction). The fluid is alkaline overall, protecting sperm from the acidic vaginal environment.

External genitalia 2 questions

11 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the internal structure of the penis.

Reveal answer

Three columns of erectile tissue: paired dorsal corpora cavernosa and a single midline corpus spongiosum that surrounds the urethra and expands distally to form the glans. Each column is wrapped by tunica albuginea. Erectile tissue fills with blood during arousal, producing erection. The penis has a root (attached), shaft, and glans (head).

12 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the foreskin (prepuce)?

Reveal answer

A double-layer fold of skin that covers the glans in uncircumcised males. Circumcision removes part or all of it. Beneath the prepuce, sebaceous-like glands produce smegma.
