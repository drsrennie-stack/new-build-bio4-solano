Cartilage & Bone Microanatomy

A skull that grew. A tibia that bowed. A remodeling system that lost its conductor.

Patient case

Helen, 68, comes to clinic complaining that her hat doesn't fit anymore. Her son had taken a photo of her two years ago at a birthday party and a new photo last week; in the second one her forehead and the bridge of her nose look noticeably wider.

She has been having a dull ache in her right shin for months, and when the physician looks she sees a clear anterior bowing of the tibia. Hearing in her right ear is reduced. Labs come back with a markedly elevated alkaline phosphatase but a normal calcium. An X-ray of the skull shows a "cotton wool" appearance; the right tibia shows thickened cortex and a curved shaft.

Guiding question Bone is a tissue that builds and breaks itself down every day of your life. What two cells are doing that, what regulates their balance, and what would you expect to see when one of those cells goes into overdrive and the other tries to keep up?

1 Bone is a tissue, not a rock

A living organ that builds and breaks itself down every day

The instinct most people have about bone is that it is hard and finished, like rebar. The instinct is wrong. Bone is a connective tissue that is actively being torn down and rebuilt at every moment of your life. The adult skeleton replaces roughly 10% of itself every year through this process, called remodeling.

Two cell types run the show. Osteoblasts build new bone matrix; once they become trapped in the matrix they have laid down, they mature into osteocytes, the resident sensor cells of bone. Osteoclasts, large multinucleated cells of monocyte lineage, resorb old or damaged bone. When build and resorb are balanced, the skeleton stays in steady state. When one side outruns the other, the skeleton changes shape. Helen's skeleton is changing shape.

What Paget's actually is. Helen has Paget's disease of bone: hyperactive osteoclasts resorb bone at three to ten times the normal rate. Osteoblasts try to keep up by laying down replacement matrix faster than they can organize it properly. The result is bone that is thicker but disorganized, weaker for its size, and locally hot, painful, and deformed.
2 Cartilage, the scaffold before bone

Three types, one biology

Before most bones are bone, they are cartilage. Cartilage is also a connective tissue, but a softer and avascular one. Its cells are called chondrocytes, and they live inside small chambers called lacunae within a gel-like matrix rich in collagen and proteoglycans. Because cartilage has no blood vessels of its own, nutrients diffuse in slowly from the surrounding perichondrium. That is why cartilage injuries heal poorly compared to bone.

Three types of cartilage do three different jobs:

Hyaline cartilageMost common type. Smooth, glassy. Articular cartilage on joint surfaces, costal cartilages, tracheal rings, fetal skeleton, epiphyseal plates. The template for endochondral bone formation.
Elastic cartilageHyaline plus elastic fibers. External ear, epiglottis. Maintains shape but bends and recoils.
FibrocartilageDense bundles of collagen, very tough. Intervertebral discs, pubic symphysis, menisci of the knee, TMJ disc. Resists compression and shear.

Hold onto hyaline cartilage in particular: it is the template most of your bones grew from in fetal development, and it is what still sits at the ends of your long bones, at the epiphyseal (growth) plates during childhood, and on the articulating surfaces of synovial joints throughout life.

The long bone, part by part

Most of bone microanatomy is best learned on a long bone, because every key feature lives there. Walk a femur in your head from end to end:

DiaphysisThe shaft. Tube of compact bone surrounding a hollow medullary cavity filled with marrow.
EpiphysisThe expanded end of the bone. Mostly spongy (cancellous) bone covered by a thin shell of compact bone. Articular cartilage on the joint surface.
MetaphysisThe region between diaphysis and epiphysis. Contains the epiphyseal plate (growth plate) in children; the epiphyseal line (a scar) in adults.
Epiphyseal plateA disc of hyaline cartilage in growing bones. Chondrocytes divide on the epiphyseal side; the diaphyseal side calcifies and converts to bone, lengthening the shaft.
PeriosteumDense fibrous outer covering of bone (except at articular surfaces). Inner cellular layer contains osteoblasts and osteoclasts; outer layer carries blood vessels and nerves. Source of width growth and fracture-callus cells.
EndosteumThin cellular layer lining the medullary cavity and the internal surfaces of spongy bone. Also contains osteoblasts and osteoclasts.
Medullary cavityThe hollow space inside the diaphysis. Red marrow in children (hematopoietic); converts mostly to yellow marrow (fat) in adults.
Articular cartilageHyaline cartilage on the joint surface of the epiphysis. Reduces friction and absorbs compression; no perichondrium, so injury healing is poor.
4 Bone microanatomy

Compact bone, spongy bone, and the cells inside both

Looking inside bone, you find two patterns of the same matrix arranged for different jobs.

Compact (cortical) bone

The dense outer shell that gives bone its strength. Built from repeating cylindrical units called osteons (or Haversian systems). Each osteon is a stack of concentric rings of bone matrix (lamellae) wrapped around a central canal (Haversian canal) that carries a blood vessel and a nerve. Osteocytes sit in small cavities called lacunae between the lamellae, and they send long thin processes through tiny channels called canaliculi to connect with each other and with the central canal. That network is how nutrients reach cells that are otherwise trapped inside a solid matrix.

Osteons are linked to each other by perpendicular perforating (Volkmann) canals, which run between central canals and connect the periosteum's blood supply to the marrow cavity.

Spongy (cancellous, trabecular) bone

Inside the epiphyses and along the inner surface of compact bone. Made of a meshwork of bony struts called trabeculae, oriented along lines of mechanical stress. The spaces between trabeculae are filled with bone marrow. Spongy bone is much lighter than compact bone for its volume, and it gives the skeleton most of its surface area for hematopoiesis and ion exchange. The trabecular pattern visibly reorients when load patterns change, which is one of the clearest demonstrations that bone is a living, responsive tissue.

The cells of bone

Osteogenic (osteoprogenitor) cellsStem cells of bone. In the periosteum and endosteum. Divide to produce osteoblasts.
OsteoblastsBuild bone. Secrete osteoid (the organic matrix, mostly type I collagen) and initiate its mineralization with hydroxyapatite.
OsteocytesMature osteoblasts that have become trapped in the matrix they laid down. Sit in lacunae, communicate through canaliculi. Sense mechanical strain and signal remodeling.
OsteoclastsLarge multinucleated cells of monocyte/macrophage lineage. Secrete acid and proteases that dissolve mineral and digest collagen. Resorb bone.

The matrix itself is two things in one: an organic scaffold (mostly type I collagen, with proteoglycans and glycoproteins) that gives bone its tensile strength, and an inorganic mineral (hydroxyapatite, a calcium phosphate crystal) that gives it compressive strength and hardness. Remove the mineral and bone becomes rubbery; remove the collagen and it becomes brittle. You need both.

5 How bone forms, grows, and remodels

Two ways to make a bone, two ways to grow one, one continuous rebuild

Ossification: two pathways

Intramembranous ossification forms bone directly from a sheet of embryonic mesenchymal tissue, no cartilage intermediate. This is how the flat bones of the skull, the mandible, and the clavicle form. Mesenchymal cells differentiate into osteoblasts in the middle of the sheet and lay down bony spicules outward, eventually forming flat bones.

Endochondral ossification uses cartilage as a template. The fetus forms a hyaline cartilage model of the future bone first; then a primary ossification center develops in the shaft and replaces cartilage with bone, while secondary centers later develop in each epiphysis. The remaining cartilage between primary and secondary centers becomes the epiphyseal plate, and that plate is the engine of length growth throughout childhood. Most bones in the body, including all long bones, form this way.

Length and width growth

A growing long bone gets longer at the epiphyseal plate, where chondrocytes divide and the cartilage they leave behind is steadily replaced by bone. The plate closes and ossifies fully in late adolescence; after that you cannot make a bone any longer. A growing long bone gets wider by apposition: osteoblasts in the periosteum lay down new bone on the outer surface while osteoclasts on the endosteal surface resorb the inner wall, enlarging the medullary cavity. The bone gets thicker without becoming heavier than it needs to be.

Remodeling, every day for the rest of your life

Even after growth stops, bone never stops turning over. Remodeling adjusts the skeleton in response to mechanical load (Wolff's law: bone deposits where it is stressed and resorbs where it is not), repairs microdamage, and maintains calcium balance under the regulation of parathyroid hormone, calcitonin, vitamin D, and mechanical signaling. The osteocyte network in compact bone is the sensor; osteoblasts and osteoclasts are the effectors.

How Helen's bone broke the rules. In Paget's disease, osteoclasts become abnormally active first. They resorb bone at high speed, creating a region of low density. Osteoblasts respond by laying down replacement matrix as fast as they can, but the new bone is laid down in a disorganized, mosaic pattern rather than the orderly lamellar layout. The result is bone that looks thicker on X-ray (the "cotton wool" appearance of Paget's skull) but is mechanically weaker and shaped abnormally. Helen's tibia is bowed because the weight of her body deforms the disorganized matrix faster than it can ossify properly. Her hat doesn't fit because the bones of her skull have been remodeled bigger. Her hearing is reduced because the auditory ossicles or the bones around the auditory canal have remodeled and changed shape. Her alkaline phosphatase is elevated because that enzyme is a marker of osteoblast activity, and her osteoblasts are sprinting to keep up. Her calcium is normal because the calcium that comes out of resorbed bone goes right back into the new matrix the osteoblasts are laying down.

Returning to Helen

Read her body through the remodeling loop

Every finding Helen brought into the clinic was the bone-cell biology you just learned, expressed at the gross-anatomy level. Walk the loop:

"My hat doesn't fit anymore."
Flat bones of the skull (intramembranous origin) have been remodeled larger. Osteoblasts laid down new matrix faster than osteoclasts resorbed, in disorganized pattern. The skull literally grew.
"Anterior bowing of the tibia."
Long bone (endochondral origin) with disorganized matrix. The cortex is thickened but mechanically weaker. Body weight slowly deforms the disorganized bone faster than osteoblasts can lay down a stronger replacement.
"Hearing reduced on the right."
Remodeling of the temporal bone or ossicles compresses the auditory canal or impairs ossicle motion. A classic Paget's complication.
"Alk phos markedly elevated."
Alkaline phosphatase is a marker of osteoblast activity. Her osteoblasts are working overtime to keep up with the runaway osteoclasts. Calcium stays normal because mineral is recycled directly into the new matrix.
"Cotton wool skull on X-ray."
Patchy areas of dense disorganized new bone (the cotton wool) sit next to areas where osteoclast resorption has won. The skull looks puffy and mottled instead of uniformly compact.
"Dull ache in the right shin."
Periosteal stretch from the enlarging, deforming bone. The periosteum carries the nerves; bone matrix itself is not innervated.

Every one of these maps to the cellular cast you just met. Osteoclast, osteoblast, osteocyte, matrix.
