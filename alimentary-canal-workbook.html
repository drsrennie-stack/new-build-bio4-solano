GI tract wall and overview 3 questions

01 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the four layers of the GI tract wall from lumen outward.

Reveal answer

Mucosa (innermost, with its own epithelium, lamina propria, and muscularis mucosae), submucosa (dense irregular CT with vessels and the submucosal nerve plexus), muscularis externa (typically inner circular and outer longitudinal smooth muscle, with the myenteric nerve plexus between), and serosa or adventitia (the outer covering — visceral peritoneum if intraperitoneal, fibrous adventitia if retroperitoneal).

02 DOK 1 · Recall

What is the enteric nervous system and where in the GI wall does it live?

Reveal answer

The enteric nervous system is the intrinsic nervous system of the gut. Its two main plexuses are the submucosal (Meissner's) plexus in the submucosa, which controls secretion and local blood flow, and the myenteric (Auerbach's) plexus between the two layers of muscularis externa, which controls motility.

03 DOK 2 · Apply

Which abdominal GI organs are intraperitoneal and which are retroperitoneal?

Reveal answer

Intraperitoneal: stomach, jejunum, ileum, transverse and sigmoid colon, cecum, appendix. Retroperitoneal: most of the duodenum (after the first part), ascending and descending colon, rectum. Knowing this matters because intraperitoneal injury can spread infection through the peritoneal cavity, while retroperitoneal injury tends to stay contained.

Mouth, pharynx, esophagus 5 questions

04 DOK 1 · Recall

What are the borders of the oral cavity, and what structures are in it?

Reveal answer

Bordered by the lips anteriorly, the cheeks laterally, the hard and soft palates superiorly, and the tongue inferiorly. Contents include the teeth, the tongue, openings of the salivary gland ducts, and the palatine and lingual tonsils.

05 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the four tooth types in an adult dentition and how many of each.

Reveal answer

Adult dentition: 8 incisors (cutting), 4 canines (tearing), 8 premolars (crushing), 12 molars (grinding) = 32 total. Includes the third molars (wisdom teeth).

06 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the tongue and its papillae.

Reveal answer

The tongue is a muscular organ with four types of papillae on its dorsal surface: filiform (most numerous, sensory but no taste buds), fungiform (mushroom-shaped, scattered, contain taste buds), foliate (lateral, contain taste buds), and circumvallate (large, V-shaped row at the back, contain taste buds). The frenulum tethers the tongue to the floor of the mouth.

07 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the three regions of the pharynx and which transmit food.

Reveal answer

Nasopharynx (air only). Oropharynx and laryngopharynx (both food and air; the bolus passes through these into the esophagus). At the bottom of the laryngopharynx, the airway turns into the larynx and the alimentary canal continues as the esophagus.

08 DOK 1 · Recall

Describe the esophagus.

Reveal answer

A muscular tube about 25 cm long that descends through the mediastinum from the laryngopharynx, passes through the diaphragm at the esophageal hiatus, and empties into the stomach at the gastroesophageal (cardiac) sphincter. Its upper third is skeletal muscle, lower third is smooth muscle, middle is mixed. Lined by non-keratinized stratified squamous epithelium that resists abrasion from food.

Stomach 5 questions

09 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the four regions of the stomach.

Reveal answer

Cardia (small region around the gastroesophageal opening), fundus (the dome above the cardia), body (the main central portion), and pylorus (the narrow distal funnel that empties into the duodenum).

10 DOK 1 · Recall

What are the lesser and greater curvatures of the stomach, and what attaches to each?

Reveal answer

Lesser curvature: the concave right border; the lesser omentum attaches here, running from the stomach to the liver. Greater curvature: the convex left border; the greater omentum drapes down from here over the small intestine. Both omenta are double layers of peritoneum that carry vessels and lymphatics.

11 DOK 2 · Apply

The stomach has three layers of smooth muscle instead of the usual two. Name them and explain why the third layer matters.

Reveal answer

Inner oblique, middle circular, outer longitudinal. The extra oblique layer lets the stomach churn and mix food in addition to moving it along, which is essential for breaking down a meal into the semi-liquid chyme before passing it into the duodenum.

12 DOK 1 · Recall

What are rugae?

Reveal answer

Rugae are the longitudinal folds of the inner stomach wall, visible when the stomach is empty. They flatten as the stomach fills, allowing the stomach to expand without dramatically increasing pressure.

13 DOK 1 · Recall

What are gastric pits and gastric glands, and name two cell types found in the glands.

Reveal answer

Gastric pits are tiny depressions in the stomach lining; gastric glands open into the bottom of each pit. Cell types include parietal cells (secrete hydrochloric acid and intrinsic factor), chief cells (secrete pepsinogen, the precursor to pepsin), mucous neck cells (secrete protective mucus), and enteroendocrine cells (secrete hormones like gastrin).

Small intestine 3 questions

14 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the three parts of the small intestine in order, and one defining feature of each.

Reveal answer

Duodenum (about 25 cm, mostly retroperitoneal, receives bile and pancreatic juice through the major duodenal papilla). Jejunum (about 2.5 m, has the largest plicae circulares and most prominent villi). Ileum (about 3.5 m, contains Peyer's patches, ends at the ileocecal valve).

15 DOK 2 · Apply

Name three structural features that increase the absorptive surface area of the small intestine.

Reveal answer

Plicae circulares: large permanent circular folds of the mucosa and submucosa. Villi: finger-like projections of the mucosa, each containing a capillary network and a lacteal (lymphatic). Microvilli: brush border on absorptive cells. Combined, they multiply the available surface area by hundreds of times.

16 DOK 1 · Recall

What is a lacteal, and what does it transport?

Reveal answer

A lacteal is a lymphatic capillary in the center of each intestinal villus. It absorbs digested fats (chylomicrons), which are too large to enter blood capillaries directly. The fats travel through the lymphatic system and ultimately drain into the venous blood via the thoracic duct.

Large intestine 4 questions

17 DOK 1 · Recall

Name the regions of the large intestine in order from where it begins to where it ends.

Reveal answer

Cecum (with the appendix), ascending colon, transverse colon, descending colon, sigmoid colon, rectum, anal canal. The colon makes a frame around the small intestine.

18 DOK 1 · Recall

Where do the cecum and appendix sit, and what is McBurney's point?

Reveal answer

Both sit in the right lower quadrant. The cecum is a blind pouch just below the ileocecal junction; the vermiform appendix hangs from the cecum and contains abundant lymphoid tissue. McBurney's point is a clinical surface landmark for the base of the appendix: about one-third of the way along a line from the anterior superior iliac spine to the umbilicus. Tenderness here suggests appendicitis.

19 DOK 2 · Apply

Name three unique anatomical features of the colon.

Reveal answer

Teniae coli: three longitudinal bands of smooth muscle that run along the colon (the colon's longitudinal layer is incomplete, gathered into these bands). Haustra: sacculations of the colon wall, created by the teniae coli being shorter than the bowel. Epiploic appendages: small fatty tags hanging from the serosa. The three together let you identify large intestine on inspection.

20 DOK 1 · Recall

Compare the internal and external anal sphincters by tissue type and control.

Reveal answer

Internal anal sphincter: smooth muscle, involuntary, autonomic control. External anal sphincter: skeletal muscle, voluntary, somatic control. Both must relax for defecation.
